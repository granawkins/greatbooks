# Plato's Apology: A Study Guide
## Plato (trans. Benjamin Jowett, 1892)
### Course: The Death of Socrates

---

## Before You Read

The scene is a courtroom in Athens. The year is 399 BC. The defendant is seventy years old.

His name is Socrates, and he has been charged with two crimes: impiety — failing to honor the gods of Athens — and corrupting the youth. He has been brought before a jury of 501 Athenian citizens, chosen by lot. They will vote. The majority will decide whether he lives or dies.

He could have avoided this. His friends had urged a quiet exit from Athens before the trial. He stayed. He could soften his defense, show remorse, bring his children before the jury to beg for mercy — it was common practice, and it worked. He refuses. He could accept exile afterward, promising to stop philosophizing. He says he would rather die.

What he gives instead is a speech. A defense, in the original Greek sense — *apologia* means "defense speech," not apology. He is not sorry. He has done nothing wrong. He has spent his entire adult life walking through Athens, questioning the people who claimed to know things — politicians, poets, generals, craftsmen — and showing them, carefully and publicly, that they didn't know what they thought they knew. If that's a crime, then Athens has confused itself about what a good city looks like.

The charges come from three men: Meletus, a poet; Lycon, a rhetorician; and Anytus, a wealthy democratic politician who is the real force behind the prosecution. Socrates calls Anytus out. He also cross-examines Meletus in open court and reduces his charges to logical rubble. It doesn't matter. The jury was made up their minds before he started speaking.

The translation you're reading is Benjamin Jowett's, from 1892 — the version that shaped how English speakers understood Plato for over a hundred years. It reads as beautiful Victorian prose. When Jowett's Socrates says, at the very end, "the hour of departure has arrived, and we go our ways — I to die, and you to live. Which is better only God knows" — that sentence has been in this form for over a century. You are hearing something that has lasted.

This is the first of three texts in *The Death of Socrates*. The *Apology* is the trial. The *Phaedo* is the last day — Socrates in his cell, surrounded by friends, waiting for the hemlock, talking about the soul. The *Republic* is what he was fighting for: a long, strange, brilliant attempt to describe what a just life and a just city actually look like. Read in sequence, they form a single argument: here is how to live; here is how to die for it; here is what it was about.

He loses the vote. He is condemned to death. And his response — to the men who just voted to execute him — is one of the most astonishing things anyone has ever said in a courtroom.

## Session I: The Defense (The Full Speech)

### Summary

**The Apology (Part One — The Defense Speech):** Socrates addresses the jury of ~501 men and immediately rejects the conventions of courtroom oratory. He will not flatter. He will not beg. He will speak as he always speaks — the way he speaks in the agora, at the money tables, in conversation. He begins by distinguishing himself from the Sophists, who charge money and claim wisdom. He is poor. He claims no wisdom. He tells the story of the Oracle at Delphi: his friend Chaerephon asked the Oracle whether anyone was wiser than Socrates, and the Oracle said no. Puzzled, Socrates set out to find someone wiser than himself — and couldn't. Politicians, poets, craftsmen: all thought they were wise, none of them were. His conclusion: his only wisdom is knowing that he knows nothing. He has been on a divine mission to show Athens its ignorance. He cannot stop. He calls himself the gadfly of Athens — a small, annoying insect that stings a great horse into movement. Remove the gadfly, the horse sleeps forever. He then cross-examines Meletus directly, leading him into a flat logical contradiction: Meletus accuses Socrates of both atheism and believing in novel divine beings. These cannot both be true. Socrates finishes by telling the jury: even if they offered to let him go on condition he stop philosophizing, he would refuse. "I owe a greater obedience to the god than to you."

---

### Scenes, Themes & Characters

**The Oracle Paradox**
Chaerephon asked the Oracle at Delphi whether anyone was wiser than Socrates. The answer was no. Socrates heard this and was baffled — he knew he wasn't wise. But the Oracle cannot lie. So he investigated. He questioned the most apparently wise people in Athens: politicians, poets, craftsmen. He found the same thing every time: each person thought they were wise about things they weren't wise about. Socrates thought no such thing. His conclusion: human wisdom is worth very little; he is wiser than others only in knowing that his wisdom is worth very little. This is not a paradox he invented — it was forced on him by the god. The examination of Athens is not arrogance. It is a religious obligation.

**Socrates vs. the Sophists**
Athens in 399 BC had a word for the kind of man Socrates appeared to be: a Sophist. Sophists were paid teachers of argument, experts in rhetoric who trained young men to win debates regardless of the truth. Aristophanes had lampooned Socrates as exactly this kind of figure in his play *The Clouds* twenty years earlier, and the jury had likely grown up with that image. Socrates' entire opening distinguishes himself systematically from the Sophist: he charges nothing, claims no wisdom, and uses the common idiom rather than ornamented speech. The Sophist is a performance of wisdom; Socrates is an investigation into ignorance. The distinction is not merely rhetorical — on it depends whether the corruption charge makes sense at all.

**The Gadfly**
Socrates says he has attached himself to Athens "in order to sting it." The image is of a gadfly landing on a great, sluggish horse — a small creature that keeps the animal from sinking into comfortable sleep. Athens is the horse. The sting is the examination. Socrates is not, he claims, attacking Athens. He is serving it, by keeping it awake. He says this is a god's gift to the city. He knows how it sounds. He says it anyway. The jury votes shortly afterward.

**The Daimonion**
Socrates describes a peculiarity of his experience: since childhood, a divine voice — a *daimonion* — has warned him away from certain actions. It has never told him what to do; only what not to do. It is this that partially grounds the impiety charge: a private divine voice not recognized by Athens' official religion looks like the introduction of a novel deity. Socrates does not conceal it. He says it openly, in court, to the jury that is considering whether to execute him for exactly this kind of claim. Its silence during the trial, he says later, tells him that what he's doing is right.

**The Meletus Cross-Examination**
The most formally Socratic passage in the text: Socrates questions Meletus directly, in open court. He leads him step by step into a contradiction — if Socrates believes in *daimonia* (divine things), then he believes in *some* gods; he cannot simultaneously be an atheist. Meletus has no answer. Aristotle later cited this cross-examination as an example of good interrogation technique. The logic is clean. The jury is unmoved. What happens in this passage is that Socrates demonstrates, on the spot, the very philosophical method that has earned him enemies for thirty years. The jury watches him do it, and then votes to execute him.

**"The Unexamined Life"**
Near the end of the defense speech, Socrates says: "the unexamined life is not worth living" (38a). This line is the philosophical center of the *Apology* and one of the most influential sentences in Western philosophy. The argument behind it: if you are not examining your own beliefs, your own conduct, your own assumptions about what makes a good life — you are living *unreflectively*, accepting what you were handed. That is not living as a rational being. Socrates would rather die than live that way. He would rather die than stop asking other people to live that way too.

The defense speech is over. Now comes the verdict — closer than it might have been, but guilty. Athens has convicted its gadfly. ### Essay

Socrates refuses to stop philosophizing even when his life depends on it. He says: "I owe a greater obedience to the god than to you." Is this courage or is it stubbornness? What would it actually look like to take him seriously — to accept that a person's obligation to truth can override their obligation to self-preservation?

## Session II: The Verdict

### Summary

**The Apology (Parts Two and Three — Sentencing and Closing Addresses):** The jury votes: guilty, by approximately 280 to 220. By Athenian procedure, each side now proposes a penalty and the jury chooses between them. The prosecution proposes death. Socrates is invited to counter-propose. He says that if he is being honest about what he deserves, it is free meals at the Prytaneum — the public dining hall that honors Olympic athletes and benefactors of Athens. He is not joking, or only slightly joking. He eventually proposes a fine of 100 drachmae, raised by his friends to 3,000. The jury votes again: death. Socrates then delivers two closing addresses. To those who voted for his death: their judgment is their problem, not his; his predictions about what comes next will prove correct. To those who voted to acquit: his *daimonion* was silent throughout the trial, which means what he did was right; death is either dreamless sleep or a journey to a better place where he can question Homer and Achilles; either way it is nothing to fear. His last sentence: "I to die, and you to live. Which is better only God knows."

---

### Scenes, Themes & Characters

**The Counter-Proposal: Free Meals**
When Socrates proposes the Prytaneum — public dining at state expense, the honor reserved for civic heroes and Olympic champions — it is an act of startling philosophical consistency. He has spent his defense arguing that he is the greatest benefactor Athens has ever had. If he actually believes that, then asking for anything less than full civic recognition would be dishonest. He believes it. He says so. The jury is not amused. His friends talk him into proposing a fine instead. It doesn't help.

**The Second Vote and Its Margin**
A detail Socrates notes: the initial conviction was close. He says he would have been acquitted if thirty more jurors had voted the other way (36a). But the second vote — on the penalty — goes more heavily against him. His conduct during the penalty phase, his refusal to perform contrition, his insistence on the Prytaneum, made the jury angrier than the original charges had. He was condemned more for his behavior at trial than for the acts he was charged with. This is not an accident. It is, arguably, the point.

**Address to Those Who Condemned Him**
Socrates tells the jurors who voted for his death that they have made a mistake — not morally but practically. They wanted to silence him. They have done the opposite. His death will produce more gadflies, not fewer. The young men who have followed him will step forward and question Athens more vehemently than he ever did. More importantly: "the difficulty, my friends, is not to avoid death, but to avoid unrighteousness; for that runs faster than death." He is not afraid of what they have done. He thinks they should be afraid of what they have done.

**Address to Those Who Acquitted Him**
To those who voted to spare him, Socrates offers a different kind of comfort. His *daimonion* gave him no warning — no negative sign — during the entire trial. This is how the divine sign works: it stops him when he is about to do something wrong. Its silence means he did nothing wrong. Therefore death is not to be feared. Either it is annihilation — dreamless sleep, the best night you ever had, extended forever — or it is a migration to another place, where the good dead are, where he can question Achilles and Odysseus and all the heroes. Either way: "What would not a man give, if he might converse with Orpheus and Musaeus and Hesiod and Homer?" He is almost cheerful.

**The Last Sentence**
"The hour of departure has arrived, and we go our ways — I to die, and you to live. Which is better only God knows." This sentence does several things at once: it accepts the verdict without complaint, it refuses false comfort about what death is, it declines to claim knowledge he doesn't have, and it leaves the question open — genuinely open. Socrates does not know whether death is better or worse than life. He has argued that it might be better. He cannot be certain. He says so. Then he goes.

**What It Means That He Loses**
The *Apology* is not a triumphant text. Socrates is right about the charges — the logical arguments he makes are sound. He is right that Athens should want to hear what he has to say. He is right that the examined life is better than the unexamined one. And the jury votes to kill him anyway. Philosophy argues. Power votes. Athens was, at this moment, both the most philosophically sophisticated culture in the Western world and a democracy that executed its best thinker for asking inconvenient questions. The *Apology* holds both of these facts together without resolving them.

### Coming Up

This is the end of the *Apology* — but not the end of the story. Socrates has been condemned. There is a delay before the execution — a sacred ship has left Athens for Delos, and no executions can be carried out until it returns. During those weeks, his friends visit him in prison. Crito comes and offers him a way out. He refuses. And on the last day, surrounded by everyone who loved him, he talks until he can no longer talk. That is the *Phaedo* — the final conversation, the death scene, and the most sustained argument in ancient philosophy for why death is not the end. We'll check in before you begin.

### Essay

Socrates proposes free meals at the Prytaneum as his punishment. He knows this will enrage the jury. His friends talk him into a fine instead — but too late to save the tone of the proceeding. Was this a mistake? Was Socrates obligated to give himself the best possible chance of survival, or was his insistence on honesty at any cost actually the right thing to do? What does his conduct during the penalty phase tell us about his character — and about his philosophy?
