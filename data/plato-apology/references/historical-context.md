## Athens After the Peloponnesian War

The Peloponnesian War (431–404 BC) was a catastrophic thirty-year war between Athens and Sparta, eventually drawing in most of the Greek world. Athens entered the war as the dominant power in the Aegean — wealthy, expansionist, culturally brilliant, ruling a maritime empire of allied city-states. It ended the war a defeated, humiliated city, its navy destroyed, its walls torn down, its empire dissolved.

The final defeat came at the Battle of Aegospotami in 405 BC, where the Spartan general Lysander destroyed the Athenian fleet. Athens, unable to import food without a navy, faced starvation under siege. In March 404 BC, Athens surrendered. The terms required Athens to demolish the Long Walls of Piraeus, surrender all but twelve warships, recall all exiles, and submit to Spartan direction in foreign affairs.

The Sicilian Expedition (415–413 BC), the disastrous attempt to conquer Sicily that ended with the entire invading force of more than 50,000 men killed or enslaved, was a turning point. It was driven by Alcibiades — a former student and close friend of Socrates. Though Socrates bore no responsibility for Alcibiades' decisions, the association between them was visible and remembered.

## The Thirty Tyrants (404–403 BC)

In the aftermath of defeat, the Spartans required Athens to form a new government. Thirty men were appointed to draft new laws and reform the constitution — and became instead an oligarchic terror regime known as the Thirty Tyrants.

The Thirty ruled for only eight months but killed approximately 5% of the Athenian population. They confiscated citizens' property, exiled democratic supporters, and used Spartan troops as personal enforcers. Their leader was Critias — who appears in two of Plato's dialogues and who had been, at some point, associated with Socrates.

Several of the Thirty had been students or associates of Socrates. This was not forgotten when Socrates came to trial five years later. Athenians who had survived the Thirty's terror had reason to be suspicious of anyone who had taught them.

Socrates himself had stayed in Athens during the Thirty's rule — a fact that Robin Waterfield argues contemporaries would have interpreted as at least tacit sympathy, since opponents of the regime had left the city [Waterfield, *Why Socrates Died*, 2009 — paywalled, claim cited via Wikipedia/Trial of Socrates]. On one documented occasion, the Thirty ordered Socrates to help arrest Leon of Salamis for execution. Socrates refused and went home — but he did not warn Leon, who was subsequently arrested by the other four men.

Democracy was restored in 403 BC when democratic exiles, led by Thrasybulus, overthrew the Thirty. The restoration came with an amnesty — Athenians were formally forbidden to prosecute each other for crimes committed during the Thirty's regime. This amnesty is one reason the charges against Socrates in 399 BC are for impiety and corrupting the youth rather than explicitly for his political associations: direct charges connected to the Thirty were legally blocked.

## Why 399 BC?

The timing of Socrates' trial — five years after the Thirty and four years after the restoration of democracy — is not accidental. Several converging factors:

1. **The amnesty** made direct political charges legally impossible, forcing Socrates' enemies to use the impiety and corruption framework.
2. **Anytus**, a leader of the democratic restoration and a hero of that movement, had both personal grievances against Socrates and political credibility to prosecute him without appearing anti-democratic.
3. **Social exhaustion**: Athens in 399 BC was a city trying to rebuild after thirty years of war and an oligarchic terror regime. Socrates' continued questioning of civic values, democratic assumptions, and conventional wisdom may have seemed less like philosophy and more like destabilization.
4. **Socrates was old** (approximately 70) and famous. A quiet exile or a private settlement was available. By refusing either — by insisting on fighting publicly in court — Socrates chose a confrontation that Athens could have avoided and he could have avoided. He did not avoid it.

## The Real Charges vs. the Official Charges

The official charges were: (1) impiety — failing to honor the gods the city honors, and introducing novel divine beings; (2) corrupting the youth of Athens.

The unofficial charges — which could not be stated openly because of the amnesty — included:

- **Political association with anti-democratic elements**: Critias and others among the Thirty had been Socrates' associates. Alcibiades, whose recklessness had contributed to Athens' worst military disaster, had also been close to Socrates.
- **Ideological subversion**: Socrates' method systematically questioned the assumptions behind Athenian democratic culture — that political virtue could be taught, that the most prominent citizens were the wisest, that conventional piety constituted genuine religious knowledge.
- **Persistent provocation**: Socrates had been embarrassing prominent Athenians for decades. He was not a young man with a new idea; he was a 70-year-old institution who had spent his entire adult life making powerful people look foolish in public.

The philosopher I. F. Stone, in *The Trial of Socrates* (1988), argues that Socrates was in some sense anti-democratic and that the trial, however unjust, had a genuine political logic [I. F. Stone — paywalled, cited via Wikipedia/Trial of Socrates]. Robin Waterfield takes a nuanced view: Socrates probably did not oppose democracy as such but his associations and his methods made him a genuine political liability [Waterfield — paywalled, cited via Wikipedia].

## How Plato Relates to Socrates

Plato (c. 429–347 BC) was Socrates' student and the primary source for almost everything we know about him. He was present at the trial — he mentions himself twice in the *Apology* (34a, 38b). He was also a supporter who offered money for Socrates' proposed fine.

Plato was 28 or 29 years old when Socrates was executed. The execution shaped the rest of his life. He left Athens afterward (possibly for Megara, then traveled widely [UNVERIFIED]). When he returned, he founded the Academy — the first institution of higher learning in the Western world.

The relationship between the Platonic Socrates and the historical Socrates is one of the central problems of classical scholarship (the "Socratic problem"). Most scholars believe Plato's early dialogues — including the *Apology*, *Crito*, and *Euthyphro* — represent a relatively faithful portrait of the historical Socrates. The later dialogues, including the *Republic*, are generally understood to put Platonic philosophical theories into Socrates' mouth. The *Apology* has the strongest claim to historical accuracy; Plato was there, and would have known if he was grossly misrepresenting a speech that was heard by 500 people.

Critias, the leader of the Thirty, was Plato's relative — either his uncle or his mother's cousin, depending on the source [UNVERIFIED — sources disagree on exact relationship]. This family tie made Plato's loyalty to Socrates, and his disillusionment with Athenian politics, particularly acute.

## Athens' Political Climate in 399 BC

The Athens of 399 BC was a democracy in recovery, not in confidence. It had lost an empire, suffered military catastrophe, survived an oligarchic terror, and just barely reconstructed democratic institutions. Its cultural prestige — the Parthenon, the playwrights, the philosophers — was increasingly a memory of a different era.

In this context, Socrates' philosophical mission — rousing the city to self-examination, questioning the credentials of politicians and generals, insisting that virtue matters more than success — reads differently than it would in a period of confidence. His insistence, in the *Apology*, that he has made Athens great by keeping it honest was not the kind of claim the city was in a mood to hear.
