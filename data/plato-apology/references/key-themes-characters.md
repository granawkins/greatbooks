## Socrates' Defense Strategy

Socrates' defense is philosophically unusual: he refuses to do what defendants typically do. He will not appeal to emotion. He will not bring his children before the jury to cry. He will not apologize or moderate his views to win sympathy. He will not even accept exile — when it is likely on offer — because that would mean agreeing to stop philosophizing, which he refuses.

His strategy is argument, not persuasion. He says the jury should judge him by the truth of his words, not by his oratorical skill. He then proceeds to be, by any conventional measure, a poor defendant: he insults the jury, mocks the charges, proposes his own reward, and antagonizes the very people who hold his life in their hands. This is not incompetence. It is a deliberate demonstration of his philosophy: the truth matters more than the outcome.

The defense has three moves:
1. Distinguish himself from the Sophists — who charge money, claim wisdom, and perform rhetoric. He does none of these.
2. Explain the Oracle at Delphi — and show that his apparent arrogance is actually a divine mission he cannot abandon.
3. Cross-examine Meletus to expose the internal contradictions in the charges.

## The Daimon (Daimonion)

*Daimonion* (δαιμόνιον) is the Greek term for Socrates' inner divine voice — often translated as "divine sign," "divine something," or simply "daimon." It is one of the strangest features of Socrates' self-presentation, and one of the most philosophically significant.

Socrates describes the *daimonion* as a voice that has come to him since childhood. It never tells him what to do — only what *not* to do. It stops him from taking certain actions. It is this that partially formed the basis of the impiety charge: by claiming to have a personal divine voice not recognized by the city's pantheon, Socrates appeared to be inventing new gods.

At the end of the *Apology*, Socrates says the *daimonion* was silent throughout the trial. He interprets this as a sign that what he did was right — that even death may be a good thing.

The *daimonion* is not the same as conscience in the modern sense. It does not reason with him. It is a signal, a warning, almost automatic. Its authority is direct, not mediated through argument. This makes it philosophically interesting: Socrates, who insists on the primacy of reason, also takes this non-rational divine signal as authoritative.

## Socrates vs. the Sophists

The confusion of Socrates with the Sophists is central to the *Apology* and to understanding why he was on trial at all. Aristophanes' comedy *The Clouds* (423 BC) — which many jurors would have seen as young men — portrayed Socrates as the archetypal Sophist: a man who teaches how to make weak arguments seem strong, who denies the gods, who corrupts the young by teaching them to disrespect their elders.

Socrates systematically distinguishes himself from the Sophists on three grounds:

1. **Money**: Sophists were paid teachers. Socrates charged nothing and was famously poor.
2. **Claimed wisdom**: Sophists presented themselves as wise men worth paying for. Socrates claimed only to know that he knew nothing.
3. **Method**: Sophists performed polished speeches designed to persuade. Socrates questioned, cross-examined, and often concluded in *aporia* (the state of being stuck, unable to answer).

The distinction matters because the impiety and corruption charges were specifically associated with Sophism in the Athenian public mind. To successfully distinguish himself from the Sophists would be to undermine the charges.

## The Piety Charge (Asebeia)

*Asebeia* (ἀσέβεια) is the Greek word for impiety — the failure to honor the gods of the city as the city honors them. The formal charge against Socrates was that he did not believe in the gods recognized by Athens, and that he introduced novel divine entities (*daimonia*).

The charge had two parts:
- Denying the traditional gods (implied atheism)
- Introducing new divine beings (the *daimonion*)

Socrates' cross-examination of Meletus exposes that these two charges contradict each other. A man who introduces new divine beings believes in *some* gods — he cannot simultaneously be an atheist. Meletus has no answer to this. Socrates says Meletus has designed a riddle, accusing him of being an atheist who believes in demigods.

Whether Socrates was genuinely pious is a matter of scholarly debate. He clearly believed in the gods in some sense — he mentions performing sacrifices, he takes the Oracle's pronouncement seriously, he speaks of divine mission. But his relationship to the traditional Athenian pantheon was not conventional, and his *daimonion* was genuinely heterodox.

## The Corruption Charge

The charge that Socrates "corrupted the youth" rested on a social observation: young, wealthy Athenian men followed Socrates around the city and imitated his method of questioning their elders and public figures. The result was that prominent Athenians found themselves publicly embarrassed — shown to not know what they claimed to know.

Socrates' defense is twofold:
1. No one would knowingly corrupt the young, because the corrupted person would later harm the corrupter. If he accidentally corrupted anyone, the right remedy is private instruction, not trial.
2. The young men who follow him do so voluntarily. Their families support him. If he had genuinely corrupted them, their families would testify against him — but instead they testify for him.

The deeper issue is political as much as philosophical. The Thirty Tyrants, the brutal oligarchic regime that ruled Athens from 404–403 BC, had included several former associates of Socrates. To many Athenians, Socrates' method of questioning democratic values and conventional wisdom was connected — at least loosely — to the anti-democratic movement that had recently terrorized the city.

## The Gadfly Metaphor

Socrates uses the image of a gadfly stinging a large, sluggish horse to describe his role in Athens. Athens is the horse — great but slow; Socrates is the gadfly — small but necessary, keeping the horse from sinking into sleep.

The metaphor captures several things at once: Socrates' self-understanding as a nuisance that serves a higher purpose; the disproportion between his apparent insignificance and his claimed importance; and the implicit insult to Athens — the city he professes to serve is too slow and comfortable.

**Important note from the Trial of Socrates Wikipedia article**: In the original Greek text, Socrates does not actually use the word *oîstros* (gadfly/horsefly). He says he has "attached himself to the city in order to sting it" — the gadfly image is allusive rather than explicit. The word "gadfly" is a traditional translation convention, not a direct quote. He does say he is "a god's gift to the Athenians." [Wikipedia — Trial of Socrates; verified accessible]

## Key Figures

### Meletus
The named plaintiff and the only accuser who speaks during the trial. Described by Socrates as a young, unknown man with an aquiline nose. Socrates treats him with a mixture of contempt and condescension. In cross-examination, Meletus is led into a logical contradiction — accusing Socrates of atheism and believing in demigods simultaneously. After Socrates dismantles the charge, Meletus does not recover.

Socrates says Meletus joined the prosecution because he was "vexed on behalf of the poets" — implying that poets whose work Socrates had examined and found lacking were behind him. He is widely regarded by scholars as a front man for Anytus.

### Anytus
The real political force behind the prosecution. Wealthy, socially prominent, a democratic hero who had helped overthrow the Thirty Tyrants. He appears in the *Meno* dialogue, where he warns Socrates that his habit of speaking ill of prominent men would someday cause him trouble. He represented, Socrates says, the craftsmen and politicians whose dignity Socrates had repeatedly pricked.

Anytus is the most politically significant of the three accusers. His democratic credentials made the prosecution difficult to dismiss as anti-democratic — Athens' most committed democrat was prosecuting Socrates.

### Lycon
The least-documented of the three. He represented the rhetoricians. His son Autolycus had been killed by the Thirty Tyrants, and he associated Socrates — somewhat unfairly — with that oligarchic regime. He had been ridiculed by Aristophanes, and had since become a successful democratic politician.

## What Makes This Philosophically Significant

The *Apology* is the foundational document of Western philosophy's relationship to death, integrity, and the examined life.

Three claims Socrates makes in this short text have reverberated for 2,400 years:

1. **"The unexamined life is not worth living" (38a).** This is the most direct statement in the Western tradition of the idea that self-reflection is constitutive of human flourishing — not merely pleasant, but necessary.

2. **"I know that I know nothing" (attributed, paraphrase of 21d).** The Socratic paradox: wisdom consists not in knowing many things but in knowing the limits of your knowledge. This is the starting point for all subsequent epistemology.

3. **Obedience to God over obedience to Athens (29d).** Socrates says: "I owe a greater obedience to the god than to you; and as long as I draw breath, I shall never stop practicing philosophy." This is one of the earliest Western articulations of conscience against law — the idea that a person's obligations to a higher principle can override obligations to the state.
