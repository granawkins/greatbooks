## The Title Problem: *Apologia* ≠ Apology

The most important translation issue in this text is the title itself. The Greek word *apología* (ἀπολογία) means **defense speech** — a formal rhetorical term for a speech made in one's own defense before a court or assembly. It is the opposite of a *kategoria* (accusation).

Modern English "apology" means an expression of remorse or regret. Socrates expresses neither. He is not sorry. He does not regret his conduct. He does not ask for mercy. He argues — forcefully — that everything he has done was right and divinely commanded.

Translating the title as "Apology" is therefore potentially misleading for a contemporary reader who has not been warned. Benjamin Jowett used it; all subsequent major English translations have retained it — partly because the alternative English constructions ("Defense," "Defense Speech") are less graceful. The convention is entrenched. But readers should understand from the first page that this text is the opposite of an apology in the modern sense.

**The Jowett translation** (1871, revised 1892) uses "Apology" as the title and reads well as prose, but the title note is important. Some more recent editions subtitle it "The Defense of Socrates" to clarify.

## Benjamin Jowett (1817–1893)

Benjamin Jowett was Regius Professor of Greek at Oxford and later Master of Balliol College. His complete translation of Plato — published in four volumes from 1871, revised in 1892 — was the standard English Plato for most of the 19th and 20th centuries. It shaped how educated Anglophone readers understood Plato for over a hundred years.

Jowett's translation is Victorian in sensibility: literary, polished, and written to be read as English prose. He was translating for a cultured general audience, not for Greek scholars, and the prose reflects that ambition. The rhythms are those of 19th-century English academic prose — formal, slightly elevated, more oratorical than contemporary readers might expect.

His scholarship was thorough; his Greek was excellent. But he was translating in 1871, and some of his vocabulary choices carry Victorian connotations that modern readers may find misleading or dated.

## What Jowett Gets Right

**Prose clarity**: Jowett's Apology reads smoothly as continuous prose. The defense speech has a natural rhetorical flow; Socrates' arguments build clearly, and the shifts in tone — from reasoned argument to irony to provocation to something approaching serenity — come through.

**Rhetorical register**: Jowett successfully captures Socrates' claim to be speaking plainly, without ornamented rhetoric, while actually speaking with considerable rhetorical skill. The paradox of the plain-speaking Socrates who is better at rhetoric than his accusers is preserved.

**Irony**: Jowett handles Socratic irony reasonably well. When Socrates proposes free meals at the Prytaneum as his penalty, the dark comedy of the gesture is visible in Jowett's rendering.

**The closing**: The final address to the jury — "the hour of departure has arrived, and we go our ways — I to die, and you to live. Which is better only God knows" — is famous in Jowett's rendering, and deservedly so. It is one of the great sentences in Western literature. ## What Jowett Gets Wrong or Softens

**"Daimonion"**: Jowett typically renders Socrates' inner divine voice as "divine sign" or "divine faculty." Contemporary translators tend to keep *daimonion* or render it more precisely as "divine something" (following the Greek's deliberate vagueness). Jowett's rendering smooths over the philosophical strangeness of the concept. Socrates is at pains to describe something that doesn't quite fit into standard theological categories; Jowett's "divine sign" makes it sound more domesticated than it is.

**Victorian vocabulary**: Jowett uses "good" and "virtue" in ways that carry 19th-century moral philosophy baggage. The Greek *arete* (ἀρετή) — translated as "virtue" — means something closer to "excellence" or "the capacity to do what you're for." A warrior's *arete* is fighting skill; a knife's *arete* is cutting well. Socrates' use of the term is more complex than Victorian "virtue" suggests. ## Major Alternative Translations

### G.M.A. Grube (Hackett, 1981; revised by Cooper 1997)
The standard contemporary academic translation. Plain prose, precise terminology, minimal archaism. Grube renders *daimonion* as "divine sign" like Jowett, but his terminology throughout is more philosophically careful. This is the translation used in most American philosophy courses. If someone has read the *Apology* in college, they probably read Grube.

The Hackett *Plato: Complete Works* (1997) includes Grube's translation revised by John Cooper — the version cited in academic footnotes with the reference "21d" etc. (Stephanus numbers, the standard scholarly pagination).

### Hugh Tredennick (Penguin, 1954)
Published in the Penguin Classics volume *The Last Days of Socrates* — which also includes *Euthyphro*, *Crito*, and *Phaedo*, making it the standard popular package for the "death of Socrates" reading sequence. Tredennick is more readable than Grube, less literary than Jowett. His prose is mid-20th-century British — clear and unfussy. This is the translation most likely to be found on a generalist's shelf.

### Thomas G. West & Grace Starry West (Cornell/Hackett, 1984)
A translation with a strong political-philosophy orientation. The Wests render the text in a way that emphasizes its political dimensions — the relationship between philosophy and politics, Socrates' challenge to democratic Athens. The accompanying notes are extensive and philosophically serious. Preferred by Leo Strauss-influenced readers and those interested in Socrates as political thinker. ### Harold North Fowler (Loeb Classical Library, 1914)
The Loeb edition provides Greek text facing English translation, useful for those who want to check the original. Fowler's translation is more literal than Jowett or Tredennick; it reads as slightly stilted. The Loeb is a scholar's tool, not a reading edition.

## Key Term: *Apologia*

- **Greek**: ἀπολογία (*apología*)
- **Literal meaning**: defense speech; a formal self-defense before a tribunal
- **What it is NOT**: an expression of remorse or regret
- **Translation problem**: English "apology" has shifted meaning since the 16th century. Early English uses preserved the legal/rhetorical sense; modern usage does not.
- **Jowett's choice**: retains "Apology" — convention, not error

## Key Term: *Daimonion*

- **Greek**: δαιμόνιον (*daimonion*)
- **Literal meaning**: divine something; a diminutive form of *daimon* (a divine being or spirit, between god and mortal)
- **Jowett**: "divine sign" or "divine faculty"
- **Grube**: "divine sign"
- **Better rendering**: Many scholars prefer "divine something" precisely because Socrates uses a neuter form that resists precise categorization — he is deliberately vague about what kind of divine entity it is

## Key Term: *Elenchus*

- **Greek**: ἔλεγχος (*elenkhos*)
- **Literal meaning**: argument of disproof or refutation; cross-examination
- **In context**: Socrates' method of questioning — leading an interlocutor through a series of questions until their position is exposed as internally inconsistent
- **Jowett**: does not use the term directly; the method is shown in the Meletus cross-examination
