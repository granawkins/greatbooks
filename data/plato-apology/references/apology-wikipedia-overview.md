## Overview

*The Apology of Socrates* (Ancient Greek: Ἀπολογία Σωκράτους, *Apología Sokrátous*) is a Socratic dialogue written by Plato. It presents the speech of legal self-defense that Socrates (469–399 BC) gave at his trial for impiety and corruption in 399 BC. Specifically, the *Apology* is a defense against two formal charges: "corrupting the youth" and "not believing in the gods in whom the city believes, but in other *daimonia* that are novel" (24b).

The *Apology* is one of four Socratic dialogues — along with *Euthyphro*, *Phaedo*, and *Crito* — through which Plato details the final days of Socrates. Scholars debate how closely the *Apology* reflects what Socrates actually said at trial; Plato himself twice notes that he was present at the trial (34a and 38b).

## The Three Accusers

Three orators formally accused Socrates at trial:

- **Anytus** — a rich, socially prominent Athenian, the driving force behind the prosecution. He opposed the Sophists on principle and was "vexed on behalf of the craftsmen and politicians" (23e–24a). His encounter with Socrates in the *Meno* dialogue, where Socrates implies that prominent Athenians raise inferior sons, shows a man who had specific personal grievances.

- **Meletus** — described by Socrates as a young man with an aquiline nose, the only accuser to speak directly during the trial. Socrates says Meletus acted because he was "vexed on behalf of the poets" (23e). In cross-examination, Socrates maneuvers Meletus into contradicting himself — accusing Socrates of both atheism and of believing in demigods at the same time.

- **Lycon** — a politician who represented the professional rhetoricians. He joined the prosecution partly because he associated Socrates with the pro-Spartan Oligarchy of the Thirty Tyrants (404 BC), who killed his son, Autolycus.

## Structure of the Text

The *Apology* is structured in three parts:

1. **The Defense Speech** — Socrates addresses the jury of ~500 Athenian men, denies the charges, explains the Oracle at Delphi story, cross-examines Meletus, and declares his philosophical mission non-negotiable.

2. **The Sentencing Plea** — After conviction by a narrow margin (280 to 220, by one reckoning), both sides propose penalties. The prosecutors propose death. Socrates first proposes free meals at the Prytaneum — the dining hall reserved for Olympic athletes and state benefactors — then reluctantly offers a fine of 100 drachmae (raised by supporters to 3,000).

3. **The Closing Addresses** — Socrates addresses first the jurors who condemned him, then those who voted to acquit. He predicts that his death will produce more gadflies, not fewer. He says his *daimonion* did not stop him from conducting his defense as he did — which he takes as a sign that what he did was right. He speculates that death is either dreamless sleep or a migration to a better place where he can interrogate the great dead. He asks the jurors to treat his sons as he has treated them.

## Key Themes from Wikipedia

- **The Oracle Paradox**: Socrates structures his entire defense around a divine riddle. The Oracle at Delphi said no one was wiser than Socrates. But Socrates knew he was not wise. So he tested everyone who seemed wise — politicians, poets, craftsmen — and found none of them truly wise. His conclusion: his wisdom consists in knowing that he knows nothing. He is the wisest man only in this minimal sense.

- **Socrates vs. the Sophists**: Socrates insists he is not a Sophist. Sophists charged money for their teaching; Socrates lived in poverty. Sophists claimed to impart wisdom; Socrates claimed only ignorance. The confusion was partly Aristophanes' fault — his comedy *The Clouds* (423 BC) lampooned Socrates as a paradigm Sophist, and the jury would have grown up with that image.

- **The Gadfly**: Socrates describes himself as a gadfly — an insect that stings a great sluggish horse (Athens) into action. He says the god has assigned him to rouse, persuade, and reprove the Athenians. This is why he cannot stop. To stop would be to disobey a divine command.

- **The Daimonion**: Socrates' inner divine voice — a personal sign that warns him away from wrong actions. He says it has never told him what *to* do, only what *not* to do. Its silence during the trial, he concludes, suggests his defense was correct and that death may be good.

## Jury Size

Wikipedia notes the jury was "perhaps 500 Athenian men" (the *dikastes*). The Trial of Socrates article gives a more specific figure: 501 jurors (+/- as was standard for Athenian courts, which used odd numbers to avoid ties). Diogenes Laërtius reports 280 voted for death, 220 for a fine.

## Historical Reliability

Scholarly opinion is divided. Charles Kahn (1996) calls the *Apology* "the most reliable guide of all our testimonies concerning Socrates." Donald Morrison (2000) argues for caution. Aristotle classified it as a form of fiction, though he also noted that the scene where Socrates questions Meletus showed good use of interrogation technique. The broad consensus is that the *Apology* reflects Socrates' actual character and approach even if the words are Plato's.
