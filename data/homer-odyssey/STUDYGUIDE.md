# The Odyssey: A Study Guide
## Homer (trans. Samuel Butler, 1900)

---

## Before You Read

The first word of the *Iliad* is *rage*. The first word of the *Odyssey* is *man*.

That shift tells you everything. The *Iliad* is about what a great emotion costs — a single man's fury radiating outward until hundreds are dead. The *Odyssey* announces a person. Its opening epithet for Ulysses is *polytropos* — "of many turns," "of many ways" — a word that signals not power or beauty but a particular kind of mind. Where Achilles was the strongest, Ulysses is the cleverest. Where Achilles' story ends on a Greek beach at Troy, Ulysses' story is about getting home.

The war is over. Troy has fallen. Ten years have passed. The Greek fleet has dispersed. Most of the heroes are either home or dead. Ulysses is neither. He is stranded on a remote island, held there for seven years by the goddess Calypso, who has offered him immortality if he will stay. He has refused. He wants to go home. That desire — *nostos* (homecoming) — is the poem's engine. Not glory, not conquest. Home.

Here is something unusual about how Homer tells the story: the poem begins not with Ulysses but with his son, Telemachus, in Ithaca. And when Ulysses does appear, his great voyages — the monsters, the underworld, the island of the goddess — are told in flashback, narrated by Ulysses himself to a king who has never heard them. The poem begins in the middle. You are being trusted to reassemble what happened from what survives.

Take the gods seriously. Minerva is Ulysses' divine patron throughout — not occasionally, but constantly, actively engineering his return. She sets the whole plot in motion. She coaches his son. She rearranges his face. When you encounter her interventions, don't read them as symbols of inner states. In Homer's world, the gods move.

Pay attention to hospitality — the Greeks called it *xenia* (guest-friendship). It is the poem's moral compass. Almost every episode turns on whether a host or a guest behaves honorably. The Cyclops violates *xenia*. The Phaeacians embody it. The suitors consuming Ulysses' household from within are its living inversion. Track *xenia* and the poem's ethics snap into focus.

A note on the translation: Samuel Butler uses Latin names for the Greek gods. Odysseus is Ulysses, Athena is Minerva, Zeus is Jove, Poseidon is Neptune, Hermes is Mercury. You'll adjust quickly.

The poem opens in Ithaca. Telemachus is twenty years old. He has never met his father. His house has been overrun for years by over a hundred powerful men who are eating his inheritance and pressuring his mother to remarry. He doesn't know if his father is alive. Something is about to tell him to go find out.

## Session 1: The Son Sets Out (Books I–IV)

### Summaries

- **Book I — The Council and the Son:** A divine council. Minerva, while Neptune is absent, persuades Jove to allow Ulysses' return. She descends to Ithaca disguised as an old friend and finds Telemachus sitting in his father's hall, unable to stop the suitors feasting around him. She tells him: seek news of your father. For the first time, Telemachus publicly rebuffs the suitors. Something has changed.

- **Book II — The Assembly:** Telemachus calls an assembly of the Ithacans — the first in twenty years. He stands up and states his case. The suitors are unmoved. Minerva, now disguised as Mentor, helps Telemachus outfit a ship. He sails in secret, at night, without telling his mother — or the suitors, who are already plotting to kill him.

- **Book III — Nestor at Pylos:** Telemachus and Minerva arrive at Pylos, where old Nestor is sacrificing on the beach. Nestor gives Telemachus his first real lesson in the world: the story of Agamemnon, murdered by his wife's lover on his return from Troy. He sends Telemachus on to Sparta.

- **Book IV — Menelaus and Helen at Sparta:** At the court of Menelaus and Helen — both recently returned from their own difficult wandering — Telemachus learns that Ulysses is alive, stranded on Calypso's island. Back in Ithaca, the suitors discover Telemachus has sailed. They set an ambush for his return.

---

### Scenes, Themes & Characters

***andra* — "Man"**
The *Odyssey* opens: *Tell me, O Muse, of that ingenious hero...* The Greek first word is *andra* — man. Where the *Iliad* announced an emotion (*mēnis*, rage), the *Odyssey* announces a person. The epithet that follows, *polytropos* — "of many turns" or "of many ways" — is Ulysses' defining quality. He is the man who has a different solution for every situation, who bends rather than breaks, who outthinks rather than overpowers. From the first syllable, this is a poem about a particular human being rather than a force.

**Telemachus**
He is not ready — that is the point. He sits in his father's hall watching men eat his inheritance, and he cannot stop them, not because he's a coward but because authority requires more than claiming it. The Telemachy (Books I–IV) is his training sequence. Minerva's first gift to him is the ability to speak in assembly. His voyage is how he becomes his father's son. When Ulysses returns, Telemachus will be ready to stand beside him.

**Minerva**
She appears on the first page and never entirely leaves. She is Ulysses' patron goddess — champion of *metis* (cunning), which is his defining quality. In the *Iliad*, the gods took sides from passion and politics. In the *Odyssey*, Minerva is Ulysses' consistent and active ally: she argues his case at the divine council, guides his son, orchestrates his return, and eventually imposes the poem's final peace. Notice how often she works through disguise and inspiration — she is Ulyssean in her methods.

**The Agamemnon Foil**
Nestor tells Telemachus about Agamemnon's murder almost immediately. It is not accidental. The poem invokes this story early and returns to it often: Agamemnon came home from Troy to be killed by his wife's lover; his son Orestes eventually avenged him. The contrast with Ulysses — parallel cases, one failing catastrophically where the other succeeds — runs through the entire poem. The poem will eventually reveal the key difference: Penelope held.

***xenia* (guest-friendship)**
Every episode of the Telemachy illustrates the hospitality code. Nestor welcomes Telemachus before knowing who he is. Menelaus offers him the finest feast. Against all of this, the suitors — occupying another man's house, eating another man's food, threatening his son — stand in stark relief. *Xenia* is not courtesy. It is a sacred obligation. Jove himself protects guests and strangers. Violating it is an offense against the gods, and the poem tracks this like a running account.

**Penelope**
She appears briefly in these books but powerfully. She comes downstairs and asks a court singer to change his song — he is singing of the Greek heroes' homecoming and it is unbearable. Telemachus, for the first time, tells her to go back upstairs. He is learning authority. What we see of Penelope even here — her grief, the way she holds herself together, the control over her own face — is not passivity. It is a woman managing an impossible situation with everything she has.

---

### Coming Up

The son has set out. Now the poem shifts to the man himself — stranded on a distant island, seven years from home, offered immortality and refusing it. He will get his ship, cross the sea, and arrive at the court of strangers who will ask him who he is and what he has been through. What follows is one of the most extraordinary acts of storytelling in ancient literature: a man recounting his own adventures to a room full of people who have no idea what they're about to hear. We'll check in next before you start Book IX.

## Session 2: The Wanderings Begin (Books V–VIII)

### Summaries

- **Book V — Calypso's Island / The Raft / The Storm:** Jove sends Mercury to Calypso's island with a command: let Ulysses go. Calypso — who has kept him for seven years and offered him immortality — must comply. Ulysses builds a raft. He sails four days. Neptune, returning from abroad, spots him and sends a shattering storm. The raft is destroyed. The sea-nymph Ino gives him a magical veil as a lifesaver. He swims two days to reach the island of Phaeacia.

- **Book VI — Nausicaä:** Minerva sends the Phaeacian princess Nausicaä to do laundry at the shore, where she will find the shipwrecked Ulysses. He emerges from the bushes, naked and terrifying, and speaks with extraordinary tact — addressing her as nearly divine, asking only for cloth and directions. She leads him toward the city and tells him: find my mother, Queen Arete, first.

- **Book VII — The Palace of Alcinous:** Minerva (disguised as a young girl) guides Ulysses through the Phaeacian city to the palace. He finds King Alcinous and Queen Arete at dinner and makes his supplication. He is welcomed, fed, questioned gently. He does not yet reveal his name. Alcinous promises him passage home.

- **Book VIII — Games and Song:** The Phaeacians hold games in Ulysses' honor. Taunted by a young lord, he responds by throwing a discus farther than anyone thought possible. The bard Demodocus sings of the Trojan War and Ulysses weeps. Demodocus sings of the affair between Mars and Venus, caught in Vulcan's net — the hall laughs. Demodocus sings of the Trojan horse and Ulysses weeps again, unable to stop. Alcinous notices. He asks: who are you?

---

### Scenes, Themes & Characters

**Calypso's Offer**
She is not a villain. She loves Ulysses. She offers him eternal life, exemption from aging, endless pleasure on a beautiful island. By rational accounting, he should stay. He refuses. He sits on the beach every day and weeps for home. When Mercury arrives with Jove's command, she argues — unfairly, she says, the gods take lovers themselves but begrudge goddesses the same — and loses. What drives Ulysses from this paradise is exactly what the poem is about: *nostos* (homecoming) is not merely where you sleep. It is who you are.

**Neptune's Enmity**
He is absent from the divine council in Book I. The moment he reappears and sees Ulysses' raft, he unleashes his full fury. His grievance is real — Ulysses blinded his son. He cannot kill Ulysses (Jove has forbidden it) but can make the journey as nearly fatal as possible. Neptune's antagonism is the structural engine of the wanderings: every near-disaster connects back to this divine enemy who will eventually have to stand down.

**The Phaeacians and Ideal *xenia***
The Phaeacians are the poem's perfect host society. They feed Ulysses before asking his name. They hold games in his honor. They listen to his story for days. They send him home with treasure and safe passage, their miraculous ships sailing through the night while he sleeps. Everything the suitors refuse to do — honor the stranger, protect the guest, eventually let him leave — the Phaeacians do. They are the poem's vision of *xenia* fully practiced.

**Demodocus and the Power of Song**
The blind bard at Alcinous's court sings of the Trojan War and Ulysses weeps. He hides it, but Alcinous sees. This is one of the poem's quiet miracles: the man who lived the events is now in the audience for the song that will outlast them. *Kleos* (glory, or fame) is what survives — not the man, but the story about the man. Ulysses is the hero of the poem; he is also sitting in the audience, hearing himself sung about, and it undoes him.

**Ulysses Revealed**
The end of Book VIII: Alcinous asks who he is, why he weeps, what his name is. The poem has been withholding this moment since the opening. Ulysses is about to tell his own story. The poem's most famous section — the *apologos* (the narrative of wanderings) — is not narrated by Homer. It is narrated by Ulysses himself, in first person, to an audience of strangers. This matters: we are hearing a man describe his own adventures, which means we are also hearing how he wants to be understood.

Ulysses opens his mouth and begins. What follows is the heart of the poem's mythic imagination — encounters that have shaped the way Western culture thinks about danger, desire, the lure of forgetting, and the cost of not listening to warnings. He begins with a raid gone wrong and a people whose fruit makes you forget you ever wanted to go home. We'll check in next before you start Book XIII.

## Session 3: Monsters and the Underworld (Books IX–XII)

### Summaries

- **Book IX — Cicones, Lotus-Eaters, Cyclops:** After Troy, Ulysses' fleet raids the Cicones. The men ignore his order to leave and are devastated when the Cicones counterattack. The Lotus-Eaters: three scouts eat the lotus fruit and lose all desire to go home. The Cyclops: Ulysses and twelve men are trapped in Polyphemus's cave. He eats two men per day. Ulysses gives him wine, calls himself "Nobody," and drives a sharpened olive stake into his eye while Polyphemus is drunk. The blinded giant cries that "Nobody" has hurt him; no one comes to help. The crew escapes clinging to the bellies of the rams. Then — as the ships pull away — Ulysses shouts his real name across the water. Polyphemus prays to his father Neptune. The poem's central divine conflict begins here.

- **Book X — Aeolus, Laestrygonians, Circe:** Aeolus gives Ulysses a bag containing all the contrary winds. The ships near Ithaca; Ulysses falls asleep; the crew opens the bag. They blow back. The Laestrygonian giants destroy eleven of Ulysses' twelve ships. On the island of Aeaea, the goddess-witch Circe transforms the scouting party into pigs. Mercury gives Ulysses the herb *moly* to resist her magic. He threatens Circe; she restores his men and becomes his lover. They remain a year. Then she tells him: before you go home, you must visit the dead.

- **Book XI — The Underworld:** At the edge of Ocean, Ulysses performs the blood rite and descends into the *nekuia* (the land of the dead). He meets Elpenor, a crew member who died falling drunk off Circe's roof, who begs for burial; the prophet Tiresias, who warns him not to touch the cattle of Helios; his mother Anticleia, who died of grief while he was gone — he tries three times to embrace her and she slips through his arms; Agamemnon, who warns him to approach Ithaca secretly; and Achilles, who tells him he would rather be a living slave than king of the dead. Then the great sinners in their endless punishments. Ulysses flees before the dead overwhelm him.

- **Book XII — Sirens, Scylla and Charybdis, Cattle of Helios:** Circe has warned him. He has the crew plug their ears with wax and orders himself lashed to the mast — he hears the Sirens' song and cannot act. Between Scylla and Charybdis: he loses six men (one per head of the six-headed Scylla) and passes, having been told not to fight her. On Thrinacia, stormbound and starving, the crew slaughters the sacred cattle of the sun god despite every warning. Jove destroys the ship. Ulysses alone survives.

---

### Scenes, Themes & Characters

***metis* (cunning) vs. *bia* (force)**
The Cyclops episode is the *Odyssey*'s central demonstration of what makes Ulysses a different kind of hero. He cannot overpower Polyphemus — a boulder blocks the door and no man could move it. Instead he plans, waits, deceives, and executes: wine, the false name, the stake. *Metis* (cunning) wins where *bia* (brute force) fails. Then Ulysses compromises the escape by shouting his real name across the water — pride overcoming prudence at the moment of triumph. The great cunning hero is not immune to the need to be known.

**The Crew**
The crew is not incompetent — they are human. They open the bag of winds because they think it holds treasure their captain isn't sharing. They kill the sacred cattle because they're starving and the storm won't lift. Again and again, Ulysses' men fail to follow his warnings and pay for it, and eventually there are no crew left. They measure what survival actually requires: a discipline so sustained and unnatural that ordinary men cannot hold it. Only the hero makes it home.

**The *nekuia* (underworld)**
Book XI is a descent into death — the living man among the shades. What Ulysses finds there is not comfort. He finds his mother and cannot hold her. He finds Agamemnon, destroyed by his wife. He finds Achilles, who tells him plainly that *kleos* (glory) is a lie: he would give anything to be alive, even as the lowest slave. The great choice Achilles made — glory over long life — sounds hollow from the underworld. The *Odyssey* answers the *Iliad* here. The man who chose *nostos* visits the man who chose the other way.

**Scylla and Charybdis**
Circe's instruction is blunt: take the Scylla side. You will lose six men. You cannot fight her — if you try, you lose the ship and everyone. Ulysses knows this. He watches six men plucked screaming from the deck and does not prevent it. This is one of the poem's hardest passages on what leadership costs. To lead is sometimes to choose which loss is bearable. He does not fight Scylla. He passes. It is the right choice. The six men are still dead.

**The Cattle of Helios**
Tiresias warned him. Circe warned him. Ulysses warned his men. They eat the sacred cattle anyway — not out of stupidity, but out of desperation, the specific failure that comes when suffering exceeds judgment. The punishment from Jove is total: everyone dies except Ulysses. He floats alone on wreckage back past Charybdis, washes up on Calypso's island, and disappears for seven years. The *apologos* is over. We now know why he arrived at Phaeacia with nothing and no one.

---

### Coming Up

The flashback ends. The Phaeacians sail Ulysses home while he sleeps, their miraculous ships guided by thought alone. He will wake on a beach in Ithaca and not recognize it at first. What follows is not a triumphant return but a long, careful, patient infiltration of his own house — disguised as a beggar, watching and waiting. The patience this requires will test him in a different way than any monster did. We'll check in next before you start Book XVII.

## Session 4: The Return (Books XIII–XVI)

### Summaries

- **Book XIII — Ithaca:** The Phaeacians sail Ulysses to Ithaca overnight and leave him asleep on the shore with his treasure. Ulysses wakes and doesn't recognize his own island — Minerva has veiled it in mist. She reveals herself and transforms him: she ages his body, shrivels his skin, strips his hair, puts beggar's rags on him. He will enter his own house as no one.

- **Book XIV — Eumaeus:** Ulysses goes to the hut of his faithful swineherd Eumaeus, who welcomes the stranger with generous hospitality — food, his own cloak, the best pig — without knowing who he is. He speaks of his absent master with love. Ulysses, testing him, tells an elaborate false tale: a soldier fallen on hard times who has heard Ulysses is alive and coming. Eumaeus doesn't quite believe it — he has been disappointed too many times — but feeds him anyway.

- **Book XV — Telemachus Returns:** Minerva in Sparta tells Telemachus it is time to come home. He sails back, evading the suitors' ambush. He lands and goes directly to Eumaeus's hut. The poem pauses to give us Eumaeus's own backstory: once a nobleman's son, kidnapped by a nurse and sold into slavery. He is not incidental. He is one of the poem's moral touchstones.

- **Book XVI — Father and Son:** Telemachus arrives at the hut and meets the disguised beggar. While Eumaeus steps out, Minerva restores Ulysses — briefly — to his true appearance. He tells Telemachus who he is. Telemachus weeps and can't believe it: you must be a god. No — his father. They embrace. They plan: Ulysses will enter the house as a beggar, Telemachus will move the weapons, they will act when the time is right.

---

### Scenes, Themes & Characters

**Homecoming and Estrangement**
He wakes up on Ithaca and doesn't know where he is. He has been gone twenty years. He has been changed by everything he endured. Minerva has veiled the island and transformed his body. The *nostos* he has sought for ten books turns out not to be a simple arrival — home has to be recognized and claimed, not just reached. This is one of the poem's quieter claims: twenty years away means twenty years have happened to both the man and the place. Neither is what the other left.

**Eumaeus: *xenia* from Below**
The swineherd practices perfect *xenia* toward a stranger without knowing the stranger is his master. The poem gives Eumaeus a name, a backstory, and a genuine interior life. He is not loyal to Ulysses out of obligation but out of something that looks like love. This is deliberately placed: we have just watched the Phaeacians honor the guest-code at royal scale; here it is practiced by a slave toward a beggar. *Xenia* is not rank-dependent. That is the point.

**The Recognition in Book XVI**
Father and son meeting is delayed, prepared, earned. The poem has been building toward it since Book I. When it comes, it is not triumphant — it is tearful, disbelieving on both sides. Telemachus, who has grown up wondering whether his father is alive, cannot quite believe the man before him. Ulysses has to explain himself, to prove who he is to the son he has never truly known. The poem is interested in the difficulty of recognition — in the gap between what we expect and what we find when we do.

**Planning**
The conversation in Book XVI between Ulysses and Telemachus is their first as men. It is also a military briefing. They have over a hundred armed enemies in the house; they have four men on their side (plus Minerva, which counts for a great deal). The plan — bide time, remove the weapons, wait for the right moment — is Ulyssean in its patience. Telemachus's willingness to follow it is proof of how far he has come since Book I.

**The Suitors' Conspiracy**
When the suitors learn Telemachus has sailed, their response is immediate: ambush him and kill him. The casual murderousness of this — to kill a twenty-year-old boy to protect their position — tells us everything we need to know about who these men are. They are not merely rude houseguests. They are dangerous. When the poem finally resolves their story, the violence will be enormous. These books make that violence feel earned.

---

### Coming Up

Ulysses is inside his house as a beggar. The suitors are still there, eating and drinking. Something is building — you feel it in every scene. An old dog will recognize him before any human does. A nurse will find him out and have to be silenced. Penelope will invite him to speak with her alone, and the conversation will be the strangest in the poem. The tension of identity almost-breaking-through, held back by Ulysses' iron will, is the engine of everything that follows. We'll check in next before you start Book XXI.

## Session 5: The Disguise Holds (Books XVII–XX)

### Summaries

- **Book XVII — Entering the House:** Telemachus goes home first. Then Ulysses, as beggar, approaches with Eumaeus. Outside the palace gates, lying on a dunghill, is Argos — Ulysses' old dog, a puppy when his master left, now ancient and neglected. Argos sees Ulysses, wags his tail, and dies. Inside the hall, the suitors feed the beggar reluctantly. Antinous throws a footstool at him.

- **Book XVIII — The Beggar Fight:** A professional beggar challenges Ulysses to fight, egged on by the suitors. Ulysses wins easily, barely restraining his strength. One relatively decent suitor, Amphinomus, accepts a warning from the disguised Ulysses to leave while he still can — but fate keeps him there. Penelope appears before the suitors and extracts costly gifts from them while appearing to soften toward their attentions.

- **Book XIX — Penelope's Interview:** Penelope comes downstairs to speak with the beggar. It is the poem's longest private conversation. The beggar claims to have met Ulysses, describes him, says he is coming. Penelope weeps. Eurycleia, the old nurse, washes the beggar's feet and finds a familiar scar on his thigh — from a boar hunt in his youth. She knows immediately. Ulysses grabs her throat. She keeps silent. Penelope announces the bow contest.

- **Book XX — The Last Day:** Ulysses lies awake listening to the suitors' slave women going out to sleep with them. He controls his anger. Minerva puts him to sleep. The next morning — feast day of Apollo — the household wakes for what will be the suitors' last day alive. Omens pile up: an eagle with a dove in its claws, a prophetess laughing strangely, meat dripping with blood. The suitors mock her. They cannot read what is coming.

---

### Scenes, Themes & Characters

**Argos**
He is dying on a dunghill when Ulysses walks past. No one has cared for him since his master left twenty years ago. He recognizes Ulysses — by scent, immediately, through the disguise and the years and the suffering. He wags his tail and drops his ears and dies. It is the briefest scene in the book and perhaps the most devastating. Not because of Argos, but because of what his recognition tells us: that something in Ulysses is unchanged, recognizable to an animal who knew him when he was young, before everything happened.

**The Tension of Non-Recognition**
These books are structured around recognition that keeps almost happening. Eurycleia finds the scar and knows. Penelope speaks with the beggar at length and seems to suspect. Amphinomus is warned almost directly. The suspense is not about what will happen — the reader knows — but about whether anyone will break the containment before Ulysses is ready. It is Ulyssean patience at its maximum extension: the man who fooled the Cyclops, who resisted the Sirens, waiting in his own house while men throw things at him.

**Penelope's Cunning**
The scene in Book XVIII where Penelope appears before the suitors is remarkable. She comes down — Minerva has made her radiant — and extracts costly gifts from each of them while appearing to soften toward their courtship. Ulysses, watching from the corner, is pleased. What looks to the suitors like Penelope yielding is Penelope relieving them of their wealth. Her intelligence mirrors her husband's. She cannot fight the suitors directly — she manages them, delays them, drains them.

**The Interview in Book XIX**
Penelope and the beggar talk for a long time, and the poem does not tell us whether she knows. She describes her dreams: an eagle killing twenty pet geese, then reassuring her the geese were the suitors. She weeps. She speaks of Ulysses with specific detail and grief that feels like more than performance. The scholarly debate — does she know, does she suspect, is she deliberately staging the moment? — cannot be resolved from the text. The poem holds the ambiguity as a feature, not a flaw. Form your own view and hold it lightly.

**The Omens**
Book XX ends with a prophetess suddenly seeing blood on the walls, shades rushing to the underworld, the sun going out. The suitors think she is mad. The poem is telling us, again, that what is coming is not just planned by Ulysses but sanctioned by something larger. *Xenia* has been violated on an extraordinary scale. The gods are owed a reckoning.

---

The bow is about to be strung. Penelope will walk into the hall with Ulysses' great weapon and offer herself to whoever can do what her husband could do. The suitors will line up. None of them will manage it. And then a beggar will ask for a turn. We'll check in next before you start Book XXI.

## Session 6: The Reckoning (Books XXI–XXIV)

### Summaries

- **Book XXI — The Bow:** Penelope retrieves Ulysses' bow from a storeroom and announces the contest: string it, shoot through twelve axe-heads, win her hand. The suitors try one by one. None can string it. Ulysses has already identified his two loyal servants — Eumaeus and the cowherd Philoetius — and revealed himself to them. Over protest, he is allowed to try. He strings it quietly — like a man who knows his own instrument. He fires through all twelve axes.

- **Book XXII — The Slaughter:** He turns the bow on Antinous — one shot through the throat, while drinking. The suitors reach for their weapons. The weapons are gone; Telemachus locked them away. Ulysses announces who he is. He and Telemachus and the two servants fight over a hundred armed men. Minerva appears in the hall. The suitors are killed, some begging, some brave, all dying. The disloyal slave women are made to clean the hall and then hanged by Telemachus. The hall is scoured. Years of violation ends in an afternoon.

- **Book XXIII — Recognition:** Eurycleia runs to tell Penelope. Penelope comes downstairs slowly, sits across from Ulysses, and says nothing. She tests him: she orders Eurycleia to move the marriage bed into the outer room. Ulysses erupts — how can the bed be moved? He built it himself around a living olive tree, the trunk still rooted in the earth, serving as its bedpost. No one could move it without cutting through the root. He knows this because he made it. She weeps. She knows. They are reunited.

- **Book XXIV — Resolution:** Ulysses goes to his old father Laertes, living in rustic grief, and reveals himself. The families of the slaughtered suitors gather for revenge. A brief battle begins. Minerva, acting on Jove's authority, calls a halt and imposes peace. The cycle of vengeance is arrested. The poem ends.

---

### Scenes, Themes & Characters

**The Bow Contest**
It is Penelope's contest — her invention, her announcement, her weapon. She chooses this moment. Whether or not she knows the beggar is her husband, she has placed before the hall a test that only one man in the world can pass. The bow was a gift Ulysses received from a hero he befriended; only he can string it. The contest is structured to fail for everyone except the right man. Ask yourself whose plan this really is.

**The Slaughter**
Four men against over a hundred. The poem does not soften this. It is long and graphic and relentless. The suitors try to fight. Some beg. Some are given specific final moments — their fear, their surprise, a last attempt at dignity. The violence is the point: years of *xenia* violated, years of theft and threat and the murder plot against Telemachus — this is the reckoning. Homer gives the suitors faces at the moment of their deaths. They are not abstractions. But they chose this.

**Penelope's Test**
She tests him. After twenty years, after the suitors are dead, after Eurycleia has told her — she sits across from him and waits. She has been offered false hope too many times. She needs proof on her own terms. The bed test is perfect: only the man who built the bed knows the secret. The secret involves the living olive tree — Ithaca's own earth, rooted, immovable, the literal foundation of the marriage. The recognition is also a reunion with the island, with home. When she weeps, she weeps for everything at once.

***homophrosýnē* (like-mindedness)**
The word the poem uses for the reunion of Ulysses and Penelope is *homophrosýnē* — not love, not passion, but "like-mindedness." Two people who think the same way, who operate on the same moral frequency, who have waited without breaking while every other character either lost faith, lost their way, or died. The *Odyssey*'s vision of the ideal marriage is not romance. It is intellectual partnership — two equally cunning, equally patient people recognizing each other across twenty years.

**The Ending**
Minerva stops the final battle and imposes peace. Critics have found this ending abrupt — even evasive. The families of the suitors want revenge, which is a legitimate claim; the poem acknowledges it. Minerva shuts it down by divine fiat. Perhaps the poem is making a deliberate argument: some cycles, left to themselves, never stop. Agamemnon's house required a generation of killing to resolve. The *Odyssey* ends differently — with a god saying: it stops here. Whether that is satisfying or an evasion is a question worth arguing about.

### Essay

The *Odyssey* ends not with the reunion of Ulysses and Penelope but with a peace imposed by a goddess on people who are still angry. Is this a satisfying ending? What is Homer saying about the limits of human justice — and about what it takes to actually end a cycle of violence?
