# The Odyssey: Chapter Summaries
## Homer (trans. Samuel Butler, 1900)

---

### Book I
- The poem opens with a divine council on Olympus. Minerva, taking advantage of Neptune's absence, persuades Jove to allow Ulysses' return home after twenty years (ten at Troy, ten wandering).
- Minerva descends to Ithaca disguised as Mentes, an old family friend, and finds Telemachus sitting helplessly in his father's hall while over a hundred suitors feast on his household's wealth.
- She tells Telemachus to seek news of his father by sailing to Pylos (Nestor) and Sparta (Menelaus), and to assert himself publicly against the suitors.
- Telemachus publicly rebukes the suitors for the first time — a significant turning point. The suitor Antinous is dismissive; Eurymachus is smoother but equally unmoved.
- Penelope comes downstairs and asks the bard Phemius to stop singing about the Greeks' return from Troy — it is too painful. Telemachus tells her to go back upstairs, asserting authority over the household for the first time.

### Book II
- Telemachus calls an assembly of the Ithacans — the first in twenty years, since Ulysses sailed for Troy.
- He publicly accuses the suitors of consuming his estate and demands they leave. Antinous deflects blame onto Penelope, revealing her trick of the shroud: she promised to choose a suitor after finishing a burial shroud for Laertes, but secretly unraveled her weaving each night for three years until a maid betrayed her.
- The assembly is sympathetic but powerless; no one intervenes against the suitors. Jove sends two eagles that tear at each other overhead — an omen the seer Halitherses interprets as doom for the suitors.
- Minerva, now disguised as Mentor, helps Telemachus secretly outfit a ship and crew. He sails at night without telling Penelope or the suitors.

### Book III
- Telemachus and Minerva (still as Mentor) arrive at Pylos, where old King Nestor is sacrificing bulls to Neptune on the beach.
- Nestor welcomes them with full hospitality before asking who they are — a model of proper *xenia* (guest-friendship).
- Nestor tells Telemachus the fates of the Greek heroes after Troy: Agamemnon was murdered on his return by his wife Clytemnestra and her lover Aegisthus; Agamemnon's son Orestes later avenged him. This parallel story — a king murdered at homecoming, a son who must act — runs through the entire poem as a foil to Ulysses and Telemachus.
- Nestor has no news of Ulysses but sends Telemachus onward to Sparta with his own son Pisistratus as companion. Minerva departs in the form of an eagle, astonishing Nestor, who realizes a god was present.

### Book IV
- Telemachus arrives at the court of Menelaus and Helen in Sparta, where a double wedding feast is underway.
- Helen recognizes Telemachus by his resemblance to Ulysses. Both Helen and Menelaus share memories of Ulysses at Troy — his cunning disguise inside the city, his discipline inside the wooden horse when Helen mimicked the Greek wives' voices to lure men out.
- Menelaus tells of his own difficult return from Troy and his encounter with the Old Man of the Sea, Proteus, who told him Ulysses was alive but trapped on Calypso's island.
- Back in Ithaca, the suitors discover Telemachus has sailed. Antinous organizes an ambush: they will lie in wait at the strait between Ithaca and Samos to kill him on his return.
- Penelope learns of both the voyage and the ambush and is distraught. Minerva sends her a comforting dream.

### Book V
- A second divine council. Jove sends Mercury to Calypso's island with a direct command: release Ulysses.
- Calypso has kept Ulysses for seven years and offered him immortality. She protests — the gods allow male deities to take mortal lovers but begrudge goddesses the same — but must comply.
- Ulysses, who has been sitting on the shore weeping for home every day, builds a raft over four days with tools Calypso provides.
- He sails for seventeen days. Neptune, returning from abroad and spotting him, sends a devastating storm that destroys the raft. The sea-nymph Ino (Leucothea) gives Ulysses a magical veil to save him from drowning.
- After two days of swimming, Ulysses reaches the coast of Phaeacia (Scheria). He finds a river mouth, drags himself ashore, and buries himself in leaves to sleep — naked, battered, and alone.

### Book VI
- Minerva appears in a dream to Nausicaä, the young Phaeacian princess, and prompts her to go wash clothes at the river near the shore.
- Nausicaä and her handmaidens are playing ball after washing when Ulysses wakes and emerges from the bushes — naked, crusted with salt, terrifying.
- The maids flee, but Nausicaä stands firm. Ulysses addresses her with extraordinary tact, comparing her to a young palm tree he once saw at Delos, asking for clothing and directions without approaching too close.
- Nausicaä gives him clothes and food and instructs him to follow her toward the city but to stop at a grove outside the walls and enter alone, to avoid gossip. She tells him to find Queen Arete first — Arete holds real power in the household.

### Book VII
- Minerva, disguised as a young Phaeacian girl, guides Ulysses through the city to the palace of King Alcinous, wrapping him in mist so no one challenges him.
- The Phaeacian palace is described in lavish detail: gold and silver walls, bronze thresholds, golden statues of boys holding torches, an orchard that bears fruit year-round.
- Ulysses enters and supplicates Queen Arete directly, clasping her knees. He is welcomed and given food and drink before anyone asks his name — exemplary *xenia*.
- Arete notices he is wearing Phaeacian clothes (Nausicaä's). Ulysses explains his shipwreck tactfully, crediting Nausicaä's kindness without compromising her reputation. Alcinous promises him safe passage home.

### Book VIII
- Alcinous holds an assembly and announces he will provide Ulysses a ship. The Phaeacians hold athletic games in his honor.
- The young lord Euryalus taunts Ulysses, saying he looks more like a merchant than an athlete. Ulysses, stung, hurls a discus farther than anyone else and challenges the Phaeacians to any contest except running (his legs are weakened from the sea).
- The blind bard Demodocus sings three songs: (1) a quarrel between Ulysses and Achilles at Troy — Ulysses weeps and covers his face; (2) the comic tale of Mars and Venus caught in adultery by Vulcan's unbreakable net — the hall laughs; (3) the story of the Trojan Horse — Ulysses weeps again, unable to stop.
- Alcinous notices the weeping and stops the song. He asks directly: Who are you? Why do the tales of Troy make you weep? This question launches Ulysses' great narration.

### Book IX
- Ulysses reveals his identity: "I am Ulysses, son of Laertes, and I live in Ithaca." He begins telling the story of his wanderings from Troy.
- First stop: the Cicones at Ismarus. Ulysses' men sack the city but refuse to leave quickly; the Cicones regroup and kill six men from each ship. A storm from Jove drives them off course.
- They reach the land of the Lotus-Eaters, where some men eat the lotus and lose all desire to return home — Ulysses drags them back to the ships by force.
- The main episode: the Cyclops Polyphemus. Ulysses and twelve men enter the giant's cave. Polyphemus eats two men at a time, trapping the rest. Ulysses devises a plan: he gets the Cyclops drunk on wine, tells him his name is "Nobody," then drives a sharpened stake into his single eye.
- The blinded Polyphemus opens the cave to let his sheep out; the Greeks escape clinging to the undersides of the rams. Once at sea, Ulysses shouts back his real name in pride. Polyphemus prays to his father Neptune to curse Ulysses' voyage — the origin of Neptune's enmity that drives the entire poem.

### Book X
- Ulysses reaches Aeolia, the floating island of Aeolus, keeper of the winds. Aeolus gives him a leather bag containing all adverse winds — a clear path home. Within sight of Ithaca, Ulysses' men open the bag thinking it holds treasure; the winds escape and blow them back to Aeolus, who refuses to help again.
- They reach the Laestrygonians, giant cannibals who destroy eleven of Ulysses' twelve ships by hurling boulders from the cliffs and spearing the crews like fish. Only Ulysses' own ship escapes because he anchored outside the harbor.
- With one ship remaining, they arrive at Aeaea, the island of the goddess Circe. She turns half the crew into pigs with a drugged feast.
- Mercury appears and gives Ulysses the herb *moly* to protect him from Circe's magic. He resists her spell and forces her to restore his men. They stay a full year — Ulysses in Circe's bed, his men feasting.
- When the crew finally demands to leave, Circe tells Ulysses he must first visit the land of the dead to consult the prophet Tiresias.

### Book XI
- Ulysses sails to the edge of the world and performs a blood sacrifice at the entrance to the underworld. The shades of the dead gather to drink the blood, which temporarily gives them voice and memory.
- The prophet Tiresias appears and delivers a prophecy: Ulysses will reach home, but Neptune will make the journey hard; he must not touch the cattle of the Sun on Thrinacia; after reaching home, he must one day travel inland carrying an oar until someone mistakes it for a winnowing fan.
- Ulysses sees his mother Anticlea, who died of grief during his absence. She tells him Penelope still waits, Laertes lives in misery on his farm, and Telemachus manages the estate. She cannot embrace him — her shade slips through his arms three times.
- A parade of famous women appears (Tyro, Antiope, Alcmene, Jocasta, and others), each telling her story.
- Agamemnon's shade appears and bitterly recounts his murder by Clytemnestra, warning Ulysses to return home in secret and trust no woman fully. Achilles appears and delivers his famous reversal: "I would rather be a hired laborer on earth than king of all the dead." Ajax refuses to speak — still angry over the armor dispute. Ulysses sees Minos judging, Orion hunting, and the punishments of Tantalus, Sisyphus, and Tityos.

### Book XII
- Ulysses returns to Circe's island briefly. She gives detailed instructions for the dangers ahead: the Sirens, Scylla and Charybdis, and the island of the Sun.
- The Sirens: Ulysses plugs his crew's ears with wax and has himself tied to the mast so he can hear their song — the only mortal to hear it and survive.
- Scylla and Charybdis: forced to choose between a six-headed monster (Scylla) who will take six men, and a whirlpool (Charybdis) that could destroy the whole ship, Ulysses chooses Scylla. Six men are snatched and eaten — he watches and can do nothing.
- On Thrinacia, the island of the Sun, Ulysses' men are trapped by contrary winds for a month. Starving, they slaughter the sacred cattle of the Sun while Ulysses sleeps. The Sun demands punishment from Jove. When they sail, Jove destroys the ship with a thunderbolt. Every man dies except Ulysses, who clings to wreckage and drifts back past Charybdis, eventually reaching Calypso's island — where the story began.

### Book XIII
- The Phaeacians load Ulysses with treasure and sail him home overnight. He sleeps the entire voyage and is set ashore on Ithaca still sleeping.
- Neptune, furious that the Phaeacians helped Ulysses, turns their ship to stone as it enters their harbor — and Jove allows him to ring their city with mountains, ending the Phaeacians' tradition of escorting travelers. Their kindness is punished.
- Ulysses wakes and does not recognize Ithaca — Minerva has wrapped it in mist. She appears to him (first disguised as a shepherd, then in her own form) and they have a remarkable exchange: he lies to her about who he is, and she laughs, admiring his cunning as a mirror of her own.
- Minerva reveals the situation at home: the suitors, the danger, the need for disguise. She transforms him into an old beggar — ragged, bald, withered — and hides his treasure in a cave. She sends him to the swineherd Eumaeus first.

### Book XIV
- Ulysses, disguised as a beggar, arrives at the hut of Eumaeus, the loyal swineherd who has faithfully managed Ulysses' pig herds for twenty years.
- Eumaeus welcomes the stranger with food and shelter before asking who he is — a perfect act of *xenia* from a slave, contrasting sharply with the suitors' behavior in the palace.
- Ulysses tells an elaborate false story: he claims to be a Cretan warrior fallen on hard times, who heard news that Ulysses is alive and returning. Eumaeus is kind but disbelieving — too many strangers have come with false tales of Ulysses.
- Eumaeus speaks warmly of his absent master and bitterly of the suitors' waste. He gives the beggar his own cloak for the cold night.
- This book is notably quiet and domestic — a long evening of firelight, pork, wine, and conversation between a disguised king and his faithful servant.

### Book XV
- Minerva goes to Sparta and tells Telemachus it is time to return home. She warns him about the suitors' ambush at the strait and gives him a route to avoid it: sail by night, land on the far side of Ithaca, and go first to Eumaeus' hut.
- Telemachus takes his leave of Menelaus and Helen. Helen interprets an omen — an eagle carrying a goose — as meaning Ulysses will swoop down on the suitors. Menelaus gives parting gifts.
- On the journey back, Telemachus takes aboard the fugitive prophet Theoclymenus, who has killed a man and is fleeing blood-guilt. Theoclymenus will later play a role as a seer in Ithaca.
- Meanwhile at Eumaeus' hut, Ulysses (still disguised) draws out Eumaeus' own story: Eumaeus was born a prince on the island of Syria, kidnapped as a child by Phoenician traders, and sold as a slave to Ulysses' father Laertes. He has been loyal to the household his entire life.
- Telemachus lands safely on Ithaca, avoiding the ambush, and heads for Eumaeus' hut as Minerva instructed.

### Book XVI
- Telemachus arrives at Eumaeus' hut and meets the disguised beggar. Eumaeus is sent to tell Penelope that Telemachus has returned safely.
- With Eumaeus gone, Minerva signals Ulysses. He steps outside, she restores his true appearance, and he reveals himself to Telemachus. Father and son embrace and weep — their first meeting in twenty years.
- Ulysses and Telemachus plan the attack on the suitors: Telemachus will return to the palace and behave normally; Ulysses will come as a beggar; they will remove the weapons from the great hall; and when the time is right, they will strike.
- Back in town, the suitors' ambush ship returns empty-handed. Antinous wants to murder Telemachus openly; Amphinomus, the most decent of the suitors, argues against it. Penelope confronts Antinous directly about the murder plot.
- The suitors agree to postpone killing Telemachus — for now.

### Book XVII
- Telemachus returns to the palace. The prophet Theoclymenus, staying as his guest, tells Penelope he is certain Ulysses is already on Ithaca — she is moved but does not quite believe it.
- Ulysses walks to the palace with Eumaeus, still disguised as a beggar. At the gate, his old hunting dog Argos — now ancient, flea-ridden, lying on a dung heap — recognizes his master, wags his tail, and dies. Ulysses secretly wipes away a tear.
- Inside the hall, Ulysses begs food from each suitor, testing them. Most give reluctantly or mockingly. Antinous throws a footstool at him, striking his shoulder — a flagrant violation of *xenia* that shocks even the other suitors.
- Penelope hears about the beggar and asks Eumaeus to bring him to her — she wants to question him about Ulysses. Ulysses (through Eumaeus) postpones the meeting until evening, when the suitors will be gone.

### Book XVIII
- Another beggar, Irus, challenges Ulysses to a fight for begging rights at the palace door. The suitors encourage the match for sport. Ulysses, still disguised, knocks Irus out with a single punch to the jaw, dragging him outside.
- Penelope, inspired by Minerva, comes downstairs looking radiant and rebukes Telemachus for allowing a guest to be mistreated. She tells the suitors that Ulysses, before leaving, instructed her to remarry once Telemachus came of age — and that any proper suitors should be bringing her gifts, not consuming her household.
- The suitors, moved by her beauty, send for lavish gifts. Ulysses is quietly pleased — Penelope has extracted wealth from them while keeping her options open.
- The maid Melantho (who is sleeping with the suitor Eurymachus) insults the disguised Ulysses. Eurymachus throws a footstool at him. Amphinomus, the kindest suitor, urges everyone to stop and go to bed.

### Book XIX
- After the suitors retire, Ulysses and Telemachus remove all weapons from the great hall and store them in a locked room — Minerva lights their way.
- Penelope comes down to speak with the beggar. Ulysses tells her an elaborate false identity (a Cretan prince who once hosted Ulysses) and describes Ulysses' appearance and clothing so precisely that Penelope weeps.
- He assures her that Ulysses is alive and near. She is cautious — too many travelers have told her similar lies.
- Penelope orders the old nurse Eurycleia to wash the beggar's feet. Eurycleia recognizes a scar on his thigh — from a boar hunt on Mount Parnassus in his youth — and realizes this is Ulysses. He grabs her throat and silences her. She promises to keep the secret.
- Penelope, unaware of the recognition, tells the beggar about a dream: an eagle killed her twenty geese — she interprets it herself but does not trust the interpretation. She announces she will set a contest the next day: whoever can string Ulysses' great bow and shoot an arrow through twelve axe-heads will be her husband.

### Book XX
- Ulysses lies awake on the floor of the vestibule, struggling with rage as he hears the disloyal maids sneaking out to sleep with the suitors. Minerva appears and assures him that tomorrow he will have his revenge.
- Penelope wakes weeping from a dream in which Ulysses lay beside her, so vivid she thought it real.
- Morning: Eumaeus arrives with pigs for the suitors' feast. The loyal cowherd Philoetius also arrives and speaks kindly of the absent Ulysses — another ally identified.
- The suitors plot again to kill Telemachus but are warned off by an omen: an eagle carrying a dove flies on their left. Amphinomus persuades them to drop the plan.
- During the feast, the suitor Ctesippus hurls an ox-hoof at the disguised Ulysses — another act of escalating violence. Telemachus warns them sharply. The prophet Theoclymenus has a terrifying vision: he sees the walls dripping blood, the porch full of ghosts heading to the underworld. The suitors laugh at him. He leaves, saying doom is coming for every one of them.

### Book XXI
- Penelope retrieves Ulysses' great bow from the storeroom — she weeps when she touches it — and announces the contest: whoever strings the bow and shoots through twelve axe-heads wins her hand.
- Telemachus sets up the axes. He tries the bow himself and nearly strings it on the fourth attempt, but Ulysses signals him to stop, and he puts it down, pretending he's too weak.
- The suitors try one by one and fail. Antinous delays his own attempt, suggesting they pause and sacrifice to Apollo first.
- Meanwhile, Ulysses slips outside and reveals himself to Eumaeus and Philoetius, showing them his scar. He instructs them: Eumaeus is to bring him the bow; Philoetius is to lock the courtyard gate.
- Over the suitors' outrage, Eumaeus carries the bow to the beggar. Ulysses strings it effortlessly and shoots through all twelve axes. Telemachus arms himself and stands at his father's side.

### Book XXII
- Ulysses throws off his rags, leaps to the threshold with the bow and a full quiver, and shoots Antinous through the throat mid-drink — the first kill.
- He reveals his identity to the stunned suitors. Eurymachus tries to bargain, blaming everything on the dead Antinous. Ulysses refuses. Eurymachus charges and is killed. The battle begins.
- Ulysses, Telemachus, Eumaeus, and Philoetius fight the suitors. Telemachus makes a critical error: he leaves the storeroom door unlocked, and the suitor Melanthius sneaks in and brings out armor for twelve suitors. Eumaeus and Philoetius catch and bind Melanthius.
- Minerva appears disguised as Mentor, then as a swallow on the rafters, deflecting the suitors' spears. Every suitor is killed — including Amphinomus, the most sympathetic.
- The disloyal maids are forced to clean the blood from the hall, then hanged in the courtyard. Melanthius is mutilated. Ulysses orders the hall purified with sulfur.

### Book XXIII
- Eurycleia rushes upstairs to tell Penelope that Ulysses has returned and killed the suitors. Penelope refuses to believe it — she suspects a god's trick.
- She comes downstairs and sits opposite the stranger in silence, studying him. Telemachus scolds her coldness. Ulysses tells Telemachus to be patient — she will test him in her own way.
- Penelope sets her test: she tells Eurycleia to move the great bed out of the bedroom. Ulysses reacts with alarm — he built that bed himself around a living olive tree; one post is the tree trunk, rooted in the ground. The bed cannot be moved unless someone has cut the tree. Only Ulysses would know this.
- Penelope breaks down. The test is passed. She runs to him and they embrace. Minerva holds back the dawn to give them a longer night together.
- Ulysses tells Penelope about Tiresias' final prophecy: he must one day make one more journey, carrying an oar inland until someone calls it a winnowing fan, then sacrifice to Neptune. After that, a gentle death will come to him in old age, from the sea.

### Book XXIV
- Mercury leads the souls of the dead suitors to the underworld. The shade of Agamemnon, encountering them, contrasts Penelope's faithfulness with Clytemnestra's betrayal — the foil story that has run through the poem reaches its conclusion.
- Ulysses goes to the countryside to find his aged father Laertes, who has been living in squalor on his farm, consumed by grief. Ulysses tests him with yet another false story before revealing himself. Laertes asks for proof; Ulysses shows his scar and names the fruit trees Laertes gave him as a boy. Laertes weeps and embraces him.
- The families of the dead suitors gather and arm for revenge, led by Antinous' father Eupeithes. They march on Laertes' farm.
- Laertes, rejuvenated by Minerva, kills Eupeithes with a spear — his final act of valor.
- Minerva, disguised as Mentor, intervenes and commands both sides to stop. Jove throws a thunderbolt at her feet to enforce the peace. A covenant is sworn. The poem ends with order restored to Ithaca — by divine force.
