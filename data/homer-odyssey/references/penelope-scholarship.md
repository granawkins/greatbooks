## Penelope in the Poem: The Basic Facts

Penelope is Odysseus's wife, queen of Ithaca, and mother of Telemachus. She has waited twenty years for her husband's return — ten years of war, ten years of his wandering home. During this time:
- Over 100 suitors have occupied her household, eating Odysseus's stores and competing for her hand
- She has maintained fidelity to Odysseus while under enormous social pressure to remarry
- She has deployed multiple strategies to delay the suitors
- She is, in the poem's closing movement, finally reunited with her husband in a recognition scene she herself controls

Penelope is arguably the *Odyssey*'s second protagonist — not a passive figure but an active, intelligent agent whose choices shape the plot's resolution.

## The Weaving and Unweaving

Penelope's most famous stratagem: she told the suitors she would choose a husband when she had finished weaving a burial shroud (*peplos*) for Odysseus's elderly father Laertes. Each night, she secretly unraveled the day's work. She maintained this deception for three years until a slave (Melantho, in some versions another servant) betrayed her secret to the suitors.

**The symbolism is rich:**
- Weaving as a metaphor for narrative itself — the Greek word for "plot" (*mēchanē*) shares conceptual space with craft and construction. Penelope *constructs* and *deconstructs* time.
- Her weaving and unweaving mirrors Odysseus's own strategy of concealment and revelation — both husband and wife defer, delay, and work toward the right moment.
- The loom was literally her domain: weaving was women's work, the household's productive center. Penelope uses the tools of her confinement as instruments of resistance.

## The Central Debate: Did Penelope Know?

The most contested question in Penelope scholarship: **did she recognize Odysseus before the formal recognition scene?**

### The Recognition Scene (Book 23)
After Odysseus has slaughtered the suitors, Penelope famously holds back — she refuses to immediately accept that the man before her is her husband. She tests him by ordering the slave Eurycleia to move their marital bed into the outer room. Odysseus reacts with indignation: the bed cannot be moved — he built it himself from a living olive tree still rooted in the earth, its trunk serving as one of the bedposts. Only the real Odysseus would know this secret. Penelope then accepts him.

The moment is described as their *homophrosýnē* — "like-mindedness" — a term of deep equality and intellectual kinship.

### Arguments That She Did Not Know
The straightforward reading: Penelope has been deceived by strangers claiming to bring news of Odysseus for years. When a beggar in her hall (the disguised Odysseus) tells her Odysseus is coming, she has no reason to believe him more than the others. Her caution is rational and self-protective. Her delay in recognizing him reflects the trauma of twenty years of waiting and the fear of hoping wrong.

### Arguments That She Did Know (or Suspected)
Several scholars — notably John J. Winkler [PAYWALLED SOURCE] — have argued that Penelope is not deceived but is conducting her own parallel strategy, making decisions with full or partial awareness of Odysseus's identity.

Evidence sometimes cited:
- Penelope's decision to hold the bow contest *at this particular moment* — when a disguised stranger has just arrived claiming Odysseus is near — seems too convenient to be coincidental [UNVERIFIED as definitive — this is scholarly debate, not established fact]
- The bow contest is framed as Penelope's choice: only she can string it, as far as the suitors know. She may be staging the scene to allow Odysseus to reveal himself.
- The conversation in Book 19 between Penelope and the disguised Odysseus is extraordinarily intimate — they discuss his nature, his coming, and her own situation. Some scholars read this as near-recognition.
- Penelope's dream in Book 19 — an eagle killing her pet geese, then reassuring her the geese were the suitors — might indicate unconscious or conscious awareness.

**The counterargument**: Even if Penelope suspects, she maintains deniability. This is itself cunning — she cannot act on recognition without proof that would be socially credible. Her delay is strategically rational even if she suspects.

## Penelope's Cunning (*Mētis*)

Penelope shares her husband's defining quality: *mētis* — cunning intelligence. The poem frequently parallels them:
- Odysseus delays, disguises, tests; so does Penelope
- Odysseus tells strategic lies; Penelope deceives the suitors for years
- Odysseus controls when he reveals himself; Penelope controls the recognition scene

The word *polytropos* ("of many turns") is used of Odysseus — but Penelope is also described with terms indicating intelligence and craft. Her name may etymologically connect to *pēnē* (weft of woven cloth) — she is the weaver in more senses than one.

Some scholars describe Penelope as the poem's counterpoint intelligence — the patient, domestic version of Odysseus's active, wandering *mētis*. Where he tests others while disguised, she tests him from her position of domestic power.

## Penelope and the Suitors: Agency and Ambiguity

The *Odyssey* allows room for ambiguity in Penelope's relation to the suitors. The poem presents:
- Her explicit grief and longing for Odysseus
- Her cunning resistance to the suitors
- But also: Athena's instruction that she "show herself to the wooers, that she might set their hearts a-flutter and win greater honor from her husband and her son" — Penelope appears before the suitors and receives gifts

Italian philosopher Giulia Sissa has offered a controversial reading: that Penelope is not merely a passive resister but an active agent who responds to the erotic dynamic of the suitors' attention, managing desire while maintaining fidelity. [Source: Wikipedia, "Penelope" — verified accessible; this is a scholarly interpretation, not the poem's explicit claim.]

This reading is a minority position — most scholarship emphasizes Penelope's fidelity and resistance. But it reflects genuine textual ambiguity in how the poem presents her interiority.

## The Bed Test: Penelope's Final Authority

The bed test (Book 23) is not merely a test of identity — it is Penelope exercising **authority over the recognition**. She controls when and how Odysseus is accepted back. Even after Eurycleia has recognized him by the scar, even after the suitors are slaughtered, Penelope holds back. She must be satisfied on her own terms.

The bed test reveals:
1. Only Odysseus knows the secret (narrative function: confirmation of identity)
2. The secret is about the marriage bed itself — their most intimate shared knowledge
3. The olive tree at the bed's root connects the marriage to the earth of Ithaca — to home itself

The moment of recognition is described through *homophrosýnē* — "like-mindedness." This term of mutual understanding, used of their reunion, captures the poem's vision of ideal marriage: not submission but intellectual partnership between equals.

## Penelope's Ambivalence

The poem does not present Penelope as simply serene in her waiting. She:
- Weeps frequently and openly
- Asks Artemis to kill her
- Considers that remarrying one of the suitors might be appropriate
- Fluctuates between hope and despair about Odysseus's return

This emotional complexity is part of what makes her a great character. She is not a patient saint but a human being under extraordinary pressure, maintaining her position through cunning, grief, and determination.

## Later Tradition and Literary Legacy

**Latin tradition**: In Roman literature (Ovid's *Heroides*, Letter I; Horace; Propertius), Penelope became the emblem of *chaste wifely fidelity* — her complexity flattened into an icon. Saint Jerome included her among pagan women celebrated for chastity.

**Alternative myths**: Some ancient sources presented radically different versions of Penelope — in one tradition (recorded by Pausanias), Odysseus banished her on return for infidelity; in another, she slept with all 108 suitors and gave birth to Pan. The *Odyssey* carefully suppresses these variants, constructing its own more complex and flattering version.

**Modern reception**: Feminist classicists have reclaimed Penelope as one of antiquity's most sophisticated female characters — a figure of intelligence and agency within patriarchal constraints, not merely a passive prize. Margaret Atwood's *The Penelopiad* (2005) retells the story from Penelope's perspective, foregrounding the hanged slave women as a dark counter-narrative.
