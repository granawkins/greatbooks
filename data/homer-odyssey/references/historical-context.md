## Dating and Composition

The *Odyssey* was composed in **Homeric Greek** — an artificial literary dialect, never the vernacular of any specific population, blending Ionic Greek with other dialects. It was probably composed in **oral form across many generations**, with its current written form emerging in the **late 8th century BC** or early 7th century BC.

Key dating evidence:
- The Greeks adopted a modified Phoenician alphabet during the 8th century BC; if the Homeric epics were among the earliest products of that literacy, they were written down toward the end of that century
- Robert Lamberton places the epics at "the beginnings of widespread literacy" from the mid-5th century BC, but the poems' language predates this substantially
- By the **mid-6th century BC**, the Odyssey had become part of the Greek literary canon
- The Athenian tyrant Peisistratos (or his son Hipparchus) institutionalized recitation of Homeric poetry at the **Panathenaia** festival — requiring a "correct" version be performed, suggesting some canonization had occurred

No single date of composition is accepted. The poem as we have it is likely a crystallized version of a tradition centuries older than its writing down.

## Oral Tradition: Parry, Lord, and Oral-Formulaic Theory

The most important development in 20th-century Homeric scholarship was the work of **Milman Parry** (1902–1935) and his student **Albert Lord** (1912–1991). They demonstrated that Homer's poems contain the hallmarks of **oral-formulaic composition**:

### What Is Oral-Formulaic Composition?
Oral poets compose in performance using a stock of **formulae** — fixed phrases that fit particular metrical slots. Because the *Odyssey* uses dactylic hexameter, phrases must fit the six-beat line. A formula like *rhododaktylos eos* ("rosy-fingered Dawn") occupies a specific metrical pattern. When a poet needs to describe dawn, this formula slots in — no ad-hoc invention required.

Formulae extend into **type-scenes**: conventionalized sequences for repeated actions — preparing a ship, feasting, arming for battle, hosting a guest. The *Odyssey*'s *xenia* sequences (hospitality rituals) are classic type-scenes: every host goes through the same sequence of bathing, feeding, questioning, and gift-giving.

### Evidence in the Text
Of the *Odyssey*'s ~12,109 lines, a substantial portion are **repetitions** — formulaic lines, half-lines, and passages that appear nearly identical across the poem. This repetition was historically explained away as "interpolations" or "lazy composition" by early scholars; Parry and Lord showed it is the fundamental feature of oral poetry, not a defect.

Examples:
- *"When rosy-fingered Dawn appeared..."* (recurring formula for morning)
- Epithets: *much-enduring Odysseus*, *grey-eyed Athena*, *goddess Circe with her lovely hair*
- Recurring feast descriptions using nearly identical lines

### Parry's Fieldwork
Parry and Lord did not study Homer directly (no living oral Homer tradition exists). Instead, they traveled to Yugoslavia in the 1930s and recorded living oral epic singers (*guslars*) who composed in exactly this way — improvising long epics in performance using formulaic building blocks. This comparative evidence was decisive in establishing the oral nature of Homeric composition.

## The Homeric Question Applied to the Odyssey

The "Homeric Question" refers to the cluster of debates about authorship, composition, and unity of the Homeric epics, active since the 18th century. Applied specifically to the *Odyssey*, the key debates are:

### Was Homer a Single Author?
The **Unitarians** argue that the *Iliad* and *Odyssey* each show enough internal consistency and artistry to imply a single organizing intelligence — a poet who may have composed in oral form but produced a coherent whole. The **Analysts** (dominant in the 19th century) argued that the epics are assemblages of originally separate poems, combined by later editors.

Modern consensus: the poems emerged from **long oral traditions** but were shaped into their current form by one or more organizing poets at the moment of writing down. Whether "Homer" was one person, two people, or a school remains unanswered.

### Were the Iliad and Odyssey Written by the Same Poet?
Ancient tradition attributed both to Homer. Modern scholars are divided:
- **Arguments for the same author**: similarities of style, shared formulaic stock, the Odyssey's implicit awareness of the Iliad's events
- **Arguments for different authors**: significant differences in tone, theology, treatment of women, and social world; the Odyssey's world is more domestic and bourgeois; the Odyssey is less interested in martial glory; the gods behave differently

The Odyssey does **not directly reference any specific event from the Iliad** — only the general context of the Trojan War. This suggests the poems formed independently within a shared tradition.

The Alexandrian scholars Aristarchus and Aristophanes of Byzantium believed the two epics were by different poets ("the *chorizontes*" — "separators"). This view was a minority position in antiquity but has found more favor in modern scholarship.

### The Telemachy Debate
Some scholars regard Books 1–4 (the Telemachy) as a later addition to the poem. Others argue that later books don't make sense without them — Telemachus's coming-of-age story is necessary for the poem's thematic development. This debate remains unresolved.

### The Ending Debate
Ancient scholars Aristarchus and Aristophanes of Byzantium considered the poem's "real" ending to be Book 23, lines 293–295 — after the recognition of Odysseus and Penelope, before the events of Book 24 (Odysseus revealing himself to Laertes, the gathering of the suitors' families, Athena's intervention). Book 24 is widely considered a possible later addition. Modern scholarship is divided.

## Relation to the Iliad

The *Odyssey* and *Iliad* presuppose knowledge of the same mythological world — the Trojan War, its heroes, its gods — but function as independent poems:

| Feature | Iliad | Odyssey |
|---------|-------|---------|
| Timing | Final weeks of the Trojan War | 10 years after the war ends |
| World | Military, aristocratic, martial | Domestic, maritime, folkloric |
| Hero type | Achilles: force (*biē*), wrath (*mēnis*) | Odysseus: cunning (*mētis*), endurance |
| Divine apparatus | Gods directly on the battlefield | Gods operating behind scenes |
| Ending | Hector's burial (unresolved) | Homecoming and reunion (resolved) |
| Tone | Tragic, elegiac | More varied — comic, folktale, romantic, tragic |
| Lines | 15,693 | 12,109 |

The *Odyssey* is generally thought to have been composed **after** the *Iliad*, but this is not established — arguments exist for either order.

## Near Eastern Influences

Scholar Martin West identified substantial parallels between the *Odyssey* and the **Epic of Gilgamesh** (Mesopotamian, ~2000 BC):
- Both heroes travel to the ends of the earth and to the land of the dead
- Both receive directions from a semi-divine female helper at the world's edge (Circe ~ Siduri)
- Both heroes encounter beings that can transform people

Whether these parallels reflect direct influence or parallel development from shared ancient Mediterranean traditions is debated. The Mesopotamian *Humbaba* (monster killed by Gilgamesh) may have influenced the figure of Polyphemus. [Source: Wikipedia, "Odyssey" — verified accessible. West's argument referenced therein.]

## Performance Context

In its original context, the *Odyssey* was not read — it was **performed** by an *aoidos* (singer) or *rhapsode* (reciter). The *Odyssey* itself depicts professional singers: **Phemius** performs in Odysseus's hall (to the suitors' pleasure, to Penelope's grief), and **Demodocus** performs at the Phaeacian court, reducing Odysseus to tears.

These in-narrative performances suggest the epics were originally heard at aristocratic feasts — embedded in the very world they describe. The audience's emotional response to Demodocus (including Odysseus's own weeping) is a meditation on the power of epic poetry itself.
