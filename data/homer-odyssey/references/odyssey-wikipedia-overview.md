# The Odyssey — Wikipedia Overview

## Basic Facts

- **Original title**: Ὀδύσσεια (*Odýsseia*)
- **Attributed to**: Homer
- **Composed**: c. 8th century BC (Homeric Greek); probably written down late 8th century BC
- **Language**: Homeric Greek (artificial literary dialect, mixture of Ionic and other dialects)
- **Lines**: 12,109
- **Books**: 24
- **Meter**: Dactylic hexameter
- **Subject**: Odysseus's 10-year journey home from Troy; events in Ithaca during his absence
- **Relation to Iliad**: Generally considered composed independently; neither epic references the other's specific events

In 2018, BBC Culture polled experts worldwide on literature's most enduring narrative — the *Odyssey* topped the list.

## Synopsis

The story begins *in medias res* — ten years after the Trojan War ended. Odysseus (Latin: Ulysses) has still not returned to Ithaca, where 108 suitors compete for his wife Penelope's hand. Penelope stalls by weaving a burial shroud for Odysseus's father Laertes, secretly unweaving it each night.

### The Telemachy (Books 1–4)
Athena (disguised as Mentes, then as Mentor) visits Odysseus's son Telemachus and urges him to seek news of his father. Telemachus visits Nestor and Menelaus, learning that Odysseus is alive but held by the nymph Calypso. Zeus sends Hermes to negotiate Odysseus's release. As Odysseus leaves Calypso's island on a raft, Poseidon destroys it with a storm. The sea nymph Ino protects him; he swims to Scheria, home of the Phaeacians.

### At the Phaeacian Court (Books 5–8)
Athena leads the Phaeacian princess Nausicaä to recover the shipwrecked Odysseus. At the court of King Alcinous and Queen Arete, Odysseus excels at athletic games and is overcome with emotion when the bard Demodocus sings of the Trojan War. He reveals his identity and recounts his adventures.

### The Apologos — Odysseus's Wanderings (Books 9–12)
Odysseus narrates his adventures in flashback to the Phaeacians:
- **Cicones**: His men raid the island of Ismara, suffering losses.
- **Lotus-Eaters**: Men eat the intoxicating lotus fruit and forget home.
- **Cyclops**: Trapped by Polyphemus, Odysseus blinds him after calling himself "Nobody." By boasting his real name while escaping, he triggers Poseidon's wrath.
- **Aeolus**: Receives a bag of winds; crew opens it, blown back from Ithaca.
- **Laestrygonians**: Giant cannibals destroy all ships but one.
- **Circe**: The goddess turns men into pigs; with Hermes' help (herb *moly*), Odysseus resists and forces her to restore them. They become lovers for a year.
- **The Underworld (Nekuia)**: Odysseus travels to the land of the dead, consults the prophet Tiresias, and meets the shades of his mother Anticleia, Agamemnon, Achilles, and others.
- **Sirens**: Crew plugs ears with beeswax; Odysseus ties himself to the mast to hear safely.
- **Scylla and Charybdis**: Navigates between the six-headed monster Scylla and the whirlpool Charybdis, losing six men.
- **Cattle of Helios**: Men kill and eat the sun god's sacred cattle despite Odysseus's warning. Zeus destroys the ship; Odysseus alone survives, washes ashore on Calypso's island.

### The Return (Books 13–24)
The Phaeacians transport Odysseus to Ithaca while he sleeps. Athena disguises him as an elderly beggar. The swineherd Eumaeus offers him hospitality without knowing his identity. Telemachus returns, evading the suitors' ambush. Odysseus reveals himself to his son. In his own home, the suitors mock and mistreat "the beggar." Odysseus's old dog Argos — neglected for twenty years — recognizes him through his disguise and dies peacefully upon seeing him.

The servant Eurycleia recognizes Odysseus from a scar on his leg while washing his feet, but he silences her. Penelope announces she will marry whoever can string Odysseus's bow and shoot an arrow through twelve axe-heads. Each suitor fails; Odysseus succeeds, then slaughters all the suitors with help from Telemachus and two loyal servants. Disloyal slave women are hanged.

Odysseus reveals himself to Penelope; she tests him by asking to move their bed. He correctly explains the bed is immovable — he built it from a living olive tree still rooted in the ground. They reunite. The next day, the families of the slaughtered suitors gather for revenge; Athena intervenes and prevents further bloodshed.

## Structure

The narrative opens **in medias res** — the preceding events are described through flashbacks and storytelling. Classical sections include:
- **Telemachy** (Books 1–4): Telemachus's perspective and journey
- **Apologos / Apologoi** (Books 9–12): Odysseus narrates his wanderings to the Phaeacians
- **Mnesterophonia** (Book 22): the slaughter of the suitors

The 24-book division was probably made long after composition, possibly by Alexandrian scholars. Aristarchus of Samothrace and Aristophanes of Byzantium considered the poem's "real" ending to be Book 23, lines 293–295.

## Style and Language

- **Meter**: Dactylic hexameter (12–17 syllables per line); catalectic form (lacks expected final syllable)
- **Narrative method**: Primarily through speech — characters serve as narrators alongside the Homeric narrator
- **Epithets**: Formulaic compound adjectives (*much-enduring Odysseus*, *grey-eyed Athena*, *rosy-fingered Dawn*)
- **Similes**: Extended Homeric similes drawing from nature and everyday life
- **Tone**: Simpler, more varied, and faster-paced than the Iliad; more domestic and folkloric in character

## Key Themes (per Wikipedia)

### Nostos (Homecoming)
*Nostos* (νόστος) — "homecoming by sea" — is the poem's central theme. The Greek word signifies both the physical voyage and the narrative tradition of homecoming. Odysseus's nostos is contrasted with Agamemnon's fatal return (murdered by Clytemnestra and Aegisthus), and Achilles' choice to forgo nostos for kleos. Odysseus succeeds where others failed because of Penelope's faithfulness.

### Xenia (Guest-Friendship)
Throughout the epic, *xenia* (ξενία, "guest-friendship/hospitality") provides models of proper and improper behavior. The Phaeacians model exemplary *xenia*. Polyphemus's only "gift" is to eat Odysseus last. The suitors violate *xenia* by overrunning their host's home. Kingship implies generosity: Antinous refusing food to the disguised beggar marks him as a false king.

### Disguise and Testing
Odysseus repeatedly tests others while disguised. Others test him to confirm his identity. This follows a type-scene pattern: hesitation → questioning → revelation → testing → recognition → reconciliation. The bed test is the culmination — only the real Odysseus could know its secret.

### Omens
Omens recur throughout (primarily bird omens). Only Odysseus receives thunder or lightning — Zeus's symbol, signifying his kingship.

## Historical Note on Reception

- Homer was the foundational text for Greek education from the archaic period onward
- The first printed edition (*editio princeps*) was produced in 1488 by Demetrios Chalkokondyles in Milan
- The first English translation: George Chapman, early 17th century (famously celebrated by Keats's sonnet)
- In 2018: Ranked #1 in BBC Culture's poll of literature's most enduring narrative
