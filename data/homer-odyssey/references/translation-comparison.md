## Overview

The *Odyssey* has one of the longest translation histories of any literary work — from Livius Andronicus's Latin *Odusia* (3rd century BC) to Emily Wilson's 2017 English translation. For the English-speaking reader, four translations dominate modern discussion: **Samuel Butler** (1900), **Richmond Lattimore** (1965), **Robert Fagles** (1996), and **Emily Wilson** (2017).

**Copyright note**: Butler (1900) is public domain. Lattimore, Fagles, and Wilson are under copyright — quoting at length requires permission.

## Samuel Butler (1900) — The greatbooks.fm Translation

### Basic Facts
- **Translator**: Samuel Butler (1835–1902), English novelist and critic
- **Published**: 1900
- **Format**: Prose
- **Copyright**: Public domain
- **Greek names used**: Romanized/Latinized — Ulysses, Minerva, Jove, Neptune, Mercury, etc.

### Who Was Butler?
Samuel Butler was primarily a novelist (*Erewhon*, *The Way of All Flesh*), not a classicist. He approached Homer as a skeptical outsider and brought an idiosyncratic perspective. He also translated the *Iliad* (1898) and is best known in Homeric studies for his eccentric argument (in *The Authoress of the Odyssey*, 1897) that the *Odyssey* was written by a young Sicilian woman — a view not accepted by mainstream scholarship [UNVERIFIED as to scholarly acceptance, but Butler's argument is well-documented].

### Butler's Style
Butler translated into **plain, readable Victorian prose** — his explicit goal was to make Homer accessible to modern readers without the archaic diction that characterized earlier translations. Where Pope (1725) used heroic couplets and grand rhetoric, Butler used direct, conversational sentences.

**Characteristic features:**
- **Prose, not verse**: Butler rejected verse translation as inevitably false to Homer's directness
- **Latinized names**: Follows the Roman tradition — Ulysses (not Odysseus), Minerva (not Athena), Jove (not Zeus), Neptune (not Poseidon), Mercury (not Hermes), Juno (not Hera)
- **Plain diction**: Avoids archaisms and inflated diction; sometimes blunt to the point of flatness
- **Speed**: Butler's prose moves quickly; the narrative momentum is strong
- **Accessibility**: Written for the general reader, not the scholar

**Sample opening (Book 1):**
> "Tell me, O Muse, of that ingenious hero who travelled far and wide after he had sacked the famous town of Troy."

Compare to the Greek's emphasis on *polytropos* ("man of many ways/turns") — Butler's "ingenious hero" captures the clever sense but softens the term.

### Strengths and Weaknesses
**Strengths**: Readable, fast, accessible, public domain. The Latinized names may feel natural to readers coming from Roman or Renaissance traditions.

**Weaknesses**: Prose loses Homer's rhythmic texture. "Ingenious hero" is a pale rendering of *polytropos*. Butler sometimes smooths over ambiguities that the Greek preserves. The Latinized names obscure the Greek literary tradition and require a translation note for new readers.

## Richmond Lattimore (1965)

### Basic Facts
- **Translator**: Richmond Lattimore (1906–1984), classical scholar
- **Published**: 1965 (his *Iliad* was 1951)
- **Format**: English hexameter verse (unrhymed)
- **Copyright**: Under copyright — **do not quote at length**
- **Greek names used**: Greek — Odysseus, Athena, Zeus, Poseidon, Hermes, etc.

### Lattimore's Style
Lattimore is a scholar-translator, committed to reproducing the form of Homeric verse. His translation uses a loose six-beat line (not strict dactylic hexameter, but evoking it) and maintains Greek names throughout. His tone is elevated and archaic — closer to the formality of the Greek than any prose translation can be.

**Characteristic features:**
- Long, rolling lines that preserve something of Homer's metrical weight
- Greek names throughout — restores the Hellenic context
- Scholarly accuracy — less willing to simplify than Butler
- Formal diction — feels like literature, not prose narrative

**Reputation**: Widely used in university courses through the late 20th century. Regarded as the most faithful to Homer's formal qualities among verse translations.

## Robert Fagles (1996)

### Basic Facts
- **Translator**: Robert Fagles (1933–2008), Princeton classicist and translator
- **Published**: 1996; Penguin Classics
- **Format**: Free verse
- **Copyright**: Under copyright — **do not quote at length**
- **Greek names used**: Greek — Odysseus, Athena, Zeus, Poseidon, etc.

### Fagles's Style
Fagles aimed for a translation that felt **immediate and dramatic** to contemporary readers — literary, but not academic. His free verse is energetic and speakable. His *Odyssey* became a bestseller and is the most widely read translation of the last thirty years.

**Characteristic features:**
- Free verse with strong rhythmic drive
- More dynamic and colloquial than Lattimore — characters speak as people, not as literary monuments
- Greek names throughout
- Vivid, occasionally modern idiom
- Excellent for reading aloud; widely used in audiobook form

**Reputation**: The popular standard. Praised for energy and accessibility. Criticized by purists for occasionally loosening Homer's meaning for dramatic effect.

## Emily Wilson (2017) — First Woman to Translate the Odyssey into English Verse

### Basic Facts
- **Translator**: Emily Wilson (born 1971), University of Pennsylvania classicist
- **Published**: 2017; W. W. Norton
- **Format**: Iambic pentameter verse (1 English line per Greek line)
- **Copyright**: Under copyright — **do not quote at length**
- **Greek names used**: Greek — Odysseus, Athena, Zeus, Poseidon, etc.

### Wilson's Significance
Wilson's 2017 translation was the **first complete translation of the Odyssey into English verse by a woman**. This is not just a biographical footnote — Wilson's translation made explicit choices previous translators had obscured.

**Characteristic features:**
- **Line-for-line**: Wilson maintained the same number of lines as the Greek (12,109), forcing economy and precision
- **Clear, contemporary English**: Avoids both archaism and anachronism
- **Attention to social dynamics**: Wilson paid particular attention to how earlier translators had softened or glorified aspects of the original — particularly regarding slavery, women, and violence
- **Restoring ambiguity**: Where Butler or Fagles sometimes resolve textual ambiguities in favor of Odysseus's heroism, Wilson often preserves the Greek's uncomfortable complexity

**Key translation choice example**: The slave women killed at the end (Book 22) — hanged for sleeping with the suitors. Wilson's translation of their role and their fate was notably less euphemistic than predecessors.

**Opening line comparison:**
- Butler: "Tell me, O Muse, of that ingenious hero..."
- Fagles: "Sing to me of the man, Muse, the man of twists and turns..."
- Wilson: "Tell me about a complicated man." (famous for its directness)

Wilson's "complicated man" has been both celebrated (for honest directness about Odysseus's moral complexity) and debated (for losing the invocation's formal register).

### Scholarly Reception
Wilson's translation was praised by critics and scholars as a genuine re-reading of Homer, not just a re-rendering. Her translator's note is an important piece of scholarship in itself, addressing the politics of translation and the history of how Odysseus has been idealized.

## Note on Names for Butler Translation Users (greatbooks.fm)

Since greatbooks.fm uses the Butler translation, readers encounter Latin names:

| Butler (Latin) | Greek Original | Role |
|----------------|----------------|------|
| Ulysses | Odysseus | Hero, king of Ithaca |
| Minerva | Athena | Goddess, Ulysses's patron |
| Jove | Zeus | King of the gods |
| Neptune | Poseidon | God of the sea; Ulysses's antagonist |
| Mercury | Hermes | Divine messenger |
| Juno | Hera | Queen of the gods |
| Diana | Artemis | Goddess of the hunt |
| Vulcan | Hephaestus | God of the forge |

When scholars discuss the Odyssey using Greek names, the story is the same — just translated back from Butler's Roman vocabulary.
