## The First Word: *Andra*

The *Odyssey* opens: *"Tell me, O Muse, of that ingenious hero..."* (Butler). The Greek opens with **ἄνδρα** (*andra*, "man") — unlike the *Iliad*'s *mēnis* (wrath). Where the *Iliad* announces an emotion, the *Odyssey* announces a person. Homer signals immediately: this poem is about a specific human being, defined by *polytropos* — "of many turns," "much-wandering," or "many-wiled." Odysseus is the man of many ways.

**Source:** Standard classical philology, confirmed against Wikipedia "Odyssey" (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odyssey).

## Key Theme: *Nostos* (Homecoming)

*Nostos* (νόστος) is the poem's animating concept — homecoming by sea, and the narrative tradition that attends it. The Greek word carries both senses: the physical voyage home and the genre of tale-telling about such a voyage. The *Odyssey* belongs to the *nostoi* tradition — stories of what happened to Greek heroes on their way home from Troy.

The poem holds multiple *nostoi* in tension:
- **Agamemnon's nostos**: catastrophic — murdered by his wife upon return. This serves as a negative model, explicitly invoked by Athena and Nestor to motivate Telemachus.
- **Menelaus's nostos**: difficult but successful.
- **Achilles's nostos**: deliberately foregone — he chose kleos over a return home, and died at Troy.
- **Odysseus's nostos**: the poem's subject — successful but delayed by divine opposition (Poseidon), his own crew's failures, and supernatural obstacles.

Agamemnon's fate functions as a shadow-double of Odysseus's throughout the poem. Agamemnon in the Underworld explicitly praises Penelope's faithfulness as the key difference. Odysseus wins where Agamemnon failed not only by skill but because his wife held.

## Key Theme: *Xenia* (Guest-Friendship / Hospitality)

*Xenia* (ξενία) — guest-friendship or hospitality — is the social code that governs relationships between host and guest in the heroic world. It is divinely sanctioned: Zeus Xenios is the protector of guests and suppliants. Violations of *xenia* are offenses against the gods.

The *Odyssey* repeatedly presents contrasting models of *xenia*:

**Exemplary hosts:**
- The Phaeacians: feed Odysseus, clothe him, offer entertainment, shower him with gifts, and transport him safely home — the perfect *xenia* sequence.
- Eumaeus the swineherd: offers food and shelter to the disguised beggar without knowing his identity. His *xenia* toward a stranger is morally superior to the suitors' treatment of their host's property.

**Failures of *xenia*:**
- Polyphemus: mocks the sacred code — his "gift" is to eat Odysseus last. His violation brings divine sanction through Odysseus's blinding him.
- Calypso: keeps Odysseus against his will on her island — hospitable in form but coercive in practice.
- The suitors: the most sustained violation. They occupy Odysseus's house, eat his food, consume his livestock, and abuse his household. They invert the guest-host relation entirely.

The proper *xenia* sequence (per J. B. Hainsworth): arrival → reception → bathing/clothing → food and drink → entertainment → questions may be asked → sleep → gift exchange → safe departure.

## Key Theme: *Polytropos* — Cunning Intelligence (*Mētis*)

Where the *Iliad*'s hero Achilles is defined by *biē* (force, prowess), Odysseus is defined by *mētis* (μῆτις) — cunning intelligence, resourcefulness, practical wisdom. He wins not by overpowering but by outwitting.

*Mētis* manifests throughout:
- Blinding Polyphemus after calling himself "Nobody" — Polyphemus's cries that "Nobody has blinded me" prevent rescue
- Strapping beneath a ram's belly to escape the cave
- Tying himself to the mast to hear the Sirens safely
- Maintaining his beggar disguise for most of Books 13–22
- The bow contest — only he can string it

This intelligence is presented as both heroic and morally complex. Odysseus lies freely, deceives allies and enemies alike, and conceals his identity even from Penelope for much of the return. Later ancient and Renaissance tradition debated whether his cunning made him admirable or untrustworthy.

## Key Theme: Disguise and Recognition (*Anagnorisis*)

The *Odyssey* is structured around a series of recognition scenes — the Greek concept of *anagnorisis* (later theorized by Aristotle as a key element of tragedy). Odysseus is recognized by:
- **Argos** (his dog): by scent, immediately — the dog dies
- **Eurycleia** (the nurse): by the scar on his leg while washing his feet
- **Eumaeus and Philoetius** (loyal servants): Odysseus reveals himself deliberately
- **Telemachus**: Odysseus reveals himself deliberately
- **The suitors**: during the bow contest and massacre
- **Penelope**: last — only after the bed test confirms identity

Each recognition is carefully staged. The pattern of delay, testing, and revelation is one of the poem's organizing principles. Penelope's delayed recognition is the most debated — see the Penelope scholarship file.

## Key Theme: The Suitors

The 108 suitors (*mnēstēres*) are not merely villains — they are a social and political problem. In Odysseus's absence, with no king and no certainty of succession, the leading families of Ithaca and neighboring islands have sent their sons to compete for the kingship (by marriage to Penelope). Their presence is a form of political crisis, not just domestic invasion.

Key suitors:
- **Antinous**: the most aggressive and violent — Odysseus kills him first
- **Eurymachus**: charming and politically savvy, but ultimately cowardly
- **Amphinomus**: relatively decent; Odysseus even warns him to leave, but fate keeps him there

Their violations accumulate:
- Eating and drinking Odysseus's stores for years
- Plotting to murder Telemachus
- Sleeping with the household slave women
- Mocking the disguised Odysseus

The slaughter of the suitors (Book 22) is one of epic poetry's most violent set pieces — Odysseus, Telemachus, and two loyal servants against over a hundred men. It raises ethical questions the poem itself acknowledges: afterward, Odysseus must face the suitors' families.

## Key Theme: The Role of the Gods

The divine apparatus in the *Odyssey* differs from the *Iliad*. The gods are less present on the battlefield; instead, they shape circumstance, inspire characters, and serve as structural forces:

- **Athena** (= Minerva in Butler): Odysseus's patron and champion. She advocates for him with Zeus, disguises him, guides Telemachus, inspires Penelope, and finally halts the vendetta cycle. She is the poem's most active divine agent.
- **Poseidon** (= Neptune in Butler): Odysseus's primary divine antagonist. Blinding Polyphemus — Poseidon's son — earns his lasting enmity. He cannot kill Odysseus (Zeus won't allow it) but delays and torments him.
- **Zeus** (= Jove in Butler): The ultimate authority. He arbitrates between Athena and Poseidon and ultimately enables Odysseus's return.
- **Circe** and **Calypso**: Semi-divine women who detain Odysseus — one as a temporary partner, one as a captor.
- **Hermes** (= Mercury in Butler): The divine messenger; delivers Zeus's command to Calypso to release Odysseus; provides the herb *moly* to protect Odysseus from Circe.

The *Odyssey* opens with a divine council debating Odysseus's fate — establishing the poem's governing framework. The gods' interest in Odysseus depends on his *aretē* (excellence) and his piety. Unlike Agamemnon (careless), Odysseus manages his divine relationships carefully.

## Key Characters

### Odysseus (= Ulysses in Butler)
King of Ithaca. Husband of Penelope, father of Telemachus. Defined by *polytropos* — "of many ways." Cunning, resourceful, patient, and frequently deceptive. His great flaw is pride — boasting his name to Polyphemus triggers the poem's central divine conflict. His journey is as much internal as external: he must learn to restrain himself, to wait, to maintain disguise.

### Penelope
Queen of Ithaca. Her fidelity, cunning, and intelligence are the poem's second great subject. Her weaving and unweaving of Laertes's shroud is emblematic of the poem's themes of delay, intelligence, and concealment. See the Penelope scholarship file for scholarly debates about her character.

### Telemachus
Odysseus's son, approximately twenty years old at the poem's opening. The Telemachy (Books 1–4) traces his coming-of-age — he must learn to assert himself as his father's son. He is not yet capable of stringing the bow, but aids crucially in the massacre.

### Athena (= Minerva in Butler)
Divine sponsor of Odysseus throughout. Patron of *mētis* — cunning intelligence — as well as crafts and war. Her role in the *Odyssey* is more intimate and persistent than in the *Iliad*.

### Eumaeus the Swineherd
Odysseus's loyal slave, who becomes a crucial ally in the return. His dignified portrayal — he practices exemplary *xenia* without knowing his guest is his own master — is one of the poem's moral touchstones.
