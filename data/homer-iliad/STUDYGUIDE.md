# Study Guide: The Iliad
## Homer (c. 8th–7th century BCE)

---

## Chapter Summaries

1. **Book I** — A plague from Apollo; Achilles and Agamemnon quarrel over captive women; Achilles withdraws from battle; Thetis appeals to Zeus to honor her son.
2. **Book II** — Agamemnon's dream tests the army's will; the Catalogue of Ships; Trojans mass their forces under Hector.
3. **Book III** — Helen surveys the Greek leaders from Troy's walls; Paris and Menelaus duel; Aphrodite saves Paris and brings him to Helen.
4. **Book IV** — The gods engineer a truce-breaking; Pandarus wounds Menelaus; full battle resumes across the plain.
5. **Book V** — Diomedes' great day of battle: he wounds Aphrodite and Ares, driving gods from the field.
6. **Book VI** — Hector visits Troy; his mother Hecuba, Paris, Helen, and finally his wife Andromache and infant son; the farewell on the city walls.
7. **Book VII** — Hector challenges any Greek to single combat; Ajax and Hector fight to a draw; both sides collect their dead.
8. **Book VIII** — Zeus forbids divine interference and tilts the battle toward Troy; Greeks retreat behind their wall at nightfall.
9. **Book IX** — Agamemnon sends an embassy — Odysseus, Ajax, Phoenix — offering gifts and Briseis; Achilles refuses everything.
10. **Book X** — A night raid: Odysseus and Diomedes spy on the Trojan camp, kill the spy Dolon, and slaughter Rhesus and his Thracians.
11. **Book XI** — An Achaean aristeia turns; Greek leaders are wounded one by one; Patroclus, watching the carnage, is moved to act.
12. **Book XII** — The Trojans storm the Achaean wall; Hector hurls a boulder and breaks through the gate.
13. **Book XIII** — Poseidon secretly rallies the Greeks; savage fighting at the ships; neither side can break the deadlock.
14. **Book XIV** — Hera seduces and lulls Zeus to sleep; Poseidon leads the Greeks in a counter-assault; Ajax stuns Hector.
15. **Book XV** — Zeus wakes and reasserts divine control; Apollo revives Hector and leads the Trojans back to the ships.
16. **Book XVI** — Patroclus begs Achilles for his armor; he enters battle, kills Sarpedon, ignores Achilles' limits, reaches Troy's walls — and is killed by Hector.
17. **Book XVII** — The great fight over Patroclus' body and armor; Menelaus and Ajax finally retrieve the corpse as darkness falls.
18. **Book XVIII** — Achilles learns Patroclus is dead; his lamentation; Thetis goes to Hephaestus; the forging of the shield — a world in miniature.
19. **Book XIX** — Achilles publicly reconciles with Agamemnon; arms himself; his divine horse Xanthus warns him he will die soon.
20. **Book XX** — Zeus lifts all restrictions; gods fight gods; Achilles begins his rampage but is turned away from Aeneas and Hector by divine intervention.
21. **Book XXI** — Achilles slaughters Trojans in the river Scamander; the river rises against him; gods fight each other on the plain; Achilles drives survivors inside Troy's walls.
22. **Book XXII** — Priam and Hecuba beg Hector to come inside; Hector waits; Achilles pursues him three times around the walls; Hector is killed; his body defiled.
23. **Book XXIII** — Patroclus' ghost demands burial; funeral and cremation; Achilles holds elaborate funeral games in his honor.
24. **Book XXIV** — The gods intervene to stop Achilles' desecration of Hector's body; Priam crosses enemy lines by night; he and Achilles weep together; Hector's body is ransomed and buried.

## Before You Read

We don't know who Homer was. That's the first thing to understand, and it's important.

"Homer" is a name attached to two epics — the *Iliad* and the *Odyssey* — that were composed in ancient Greek sometime around the 8th or 7th century BCE. Whether Homer was a single poet, a tradition, a name applied to a school of singers, or a brilliant individual who crystallized centuries of oral performance into something fixed — scholars have debated this since antiquity. What we can say is this: the poems exist, they are extraordinary, and they are old. Old in a way that most readers haven't fully reckoned with.

The Trojan War, if it happened at all, was fought around 1180 BCE — during the Bronze Age. Homer composed the *Iliad* roughly 400 years after those events. He was separated from his subject by about the same span of time that separates us from the American Revolution. In between was the Greek Dark Ages: a period of collapse, depopulation, and forgetting so severe that writing itself was lost and then reinvented. Homer was composing from the far side of a cultural catastrophe, drawing on traditions that had survived only in oral performance — singers carrying the past forward in their bodies and voices.

This matters for how you read. The *Iliad* is not a novel. It is not a play. It began as something sung, probably to a lyre, by professional singer-poets (*aoidoi*) performing at aristocratic courts and public festivals. The American classicist Milman Parry showed in the 1920s that the poem is saturated with "formulas" — repeated phrases that fit the meter: *swift-footed Achilles*, *rosy-fingered Dawn*, *the wine-dark sea*. These formulas were not clichés; they were tools. An oral poet improvising in real time needed ready-made building blocks to fill out the rhythm while his mind moved forward. Every repeated epithet is a trace of a tradition in which the poem was alive, changing slightly with each performance, carried by the memories of singers who had never read a book.

So when you hear "rosy-fingered Dawn" for the fifth time, don't skip it. Sit with it. It is a piece of ancient technology, and it is also quietly beautiful.

The poem begins with a word: *mēnis*. Rage. In ancient Greek, *mēnis* was almost exclusively reserved for the wrath of gods — a divine, overwhelming, annihilating fury. Homer uses it in the poem's first line to describe a man: Achilles, son of the sea-nymph Thetis and the mortal king Peleus, greatest fighter in the Greek army. The *Iliad* is not the story of the Trojan War. It covers only a few weeks near the end of a ten-year siege. It is the story of what happens when one man's rage tears a world apart — and what it takes, finally, to put it down.

Achilles is at the center, but the *Iliad* is not simply his story. It is also Hector's. If Achilles is the Greeks' greatest warrior, Hector is the Trojans' — a man fighting to defend his city, his wife, his infant son, in a war his brother Paris started. Hector is, in many ways, the poem's moral center: a soldier who knows his city will fall, who tells his wife as much, and goes back to the wall anyway. His dignity and his doom are inseparable.

Then there is old Priam, the king of Troy, whose walk across the enemy camp in the poem's final book is one of the most daring scenes in all of Western literature. And Agamemnon, commander of the Greeks, whose pride and poor judgment set the catastrophe in motion. And Patroclus, whose death is the hinge on which everything turns.

The French philosopher Simone Weil, writing in 1939 as France fell to Germany, read the *Iliad* as a poem about *force* — "that which turns anyone subjected to it into a thing." Weil noticed what many readers miss: the poem does not glorify war. It shows war's glory and its horror simultaneously, refusing to look away from either. It treats Greeks and Trojans with the same compassion, the same unflinching eye. No one controls force; it controls everyone who touches it, including the powerful.

What kind of attention does the poem reward? Slow attention. Notice who speaks and who is silent. Notice when a character's name is given before death — Homer almost always names a man right before killing him, a quiet act of dignity. Notice the similes: moments when the poem suddenly pulls back from the battlefield and shows us peasants harvesting grain, or bees swarming from a hollow rock, or a woman dyeing wool. These are glimpses of a world of ordinary life that the war is consuming. They are the poem's heartbeat.

You're reading Samuel Butler's 1898 prose translation. Butler used Roman names for the Greek gods — Zeus is Jove, Athena is Minerva, Hera is Juno, Poseidon is Neptune, Hephaestus is Vulcan, Ares is Mars. This can be momentarily disorienting. Stick with it. The prose is clear and moves quickly.

The *Iliad* is 15,693 lines long. It has been read continuously for nearly three thousand years. No one who has read it carefully has walked away unchanged. The question is what it changes in you — and that depends entirely on the attention you bring.

## Chapter Materials

---

### Chapter 1

#### Before You Read

We begin at a crisis point: nine years into the Trojan War, and something has gone wrong inside the Greek camp itself. This opening book is entirely about a quarrel — not between Greeks and Trojans, but between the two most powerful Greeks. Pay attention to what triggers the dispute, whose pride is at stake, and how the gods are immediately entangled in what looks like a purely human conflict. The first word of the poem is *rage*. Ask yourself, from the very beginning, whose rage this is and what it costs.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Agamemnon and Achilles both believe they're in the right. After reading Book I, who do you think has the stronger case — and does the poem seem to agree with you?

**Probing:**
- The goddess Athena physically stops Achilles from drawing his sword, visible only to him. What does this moment suggest about how the poem understands the relationship between divine will and human choice?
- Achilles' mother Thetis is a sea-goddess who can appeal directly to Zeus. How does this fact — that one side in a very human dispute has a divine ally — change the stakes of what seems like a camp quarrel?
- Nestor, the oldest Greek commander, tries to mediate and is ignored. What does the poem seem to be saying about wisdom and its limits in a world governed by *timē* (honor)?

**Broader:**
- Achilles' withdrawal is a conscious refusal to fight for a commander who dishonored him. Is there a difference between justified grievance and self-destructive pride? Where does Achilles fall?
- The concept of *timē* (honor as social recognition, embodied in prizes and public standing) drives this quarrel. Do we still live by some version of this code — or have we replaced it with something else?

---

### Chapter 2

#### Before You Read

Book II is a test of patience and also a structural foundation. Agamemnon runs a strange experiment on his troops that backfires badly, and then we get the famous Catalogue of Ships — a long, metrically dense inventory of every Greek contingent and its commander. Modern readers often skim it. Don't. The Catalogue is the poem anchoring itself to a real world: geography, names, communities. These are the people the war belongs to. It also includes the Trojans' catalogue — their allies, their lands. Notice how both sides are catalogued with equal care.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Agamemnon tests his troops by suggesting they sail home — and they almost do. What does that moment reveal about the state of the Greek army nine years in?

**Probing:**
- Odysseus forcefully beats the common soldier Thersites back into silence when Thersites voices what many soldiers must have been thinking. What does the poem appear to endorse here — and does that endorsement sit comfortably with you?
- The Catalogue of Ships preserves pre-Dorian Greek geography, suggesting it may encode genuinely ancient information. What does it mean that Homer includes this almost encyclopedic list of places in a poem about rage and war?
- Zeus has promised to favor the Trojans until Achilles' honor is restored. But Zeus's plan seems to require enormous Greek suffering. Who is responsible for what follows?

**Broader:**
- The poem carefully names the lands and peoples on both sides. Why might it matter — morally, poetically — to name everyone before a war begins?
- Thersites is the poem's only "common soldier" who speaks at length, and he is mocked and beaten. Does the *Iliad* take the perspective of ordinary soldiers seriously, or is it fundamentally a poem about elites?

---

### Chapter 3

#### Before You Read

For a moment, the war pauses. Paris — whose abduction of Helen caused all of this — steps forward to fight Menelaus, the man he wronged, in single combat. If this duel could settle everything, it would. Watch for what actually happens, and what it reveals about Paris as a character. We also get our first sustained view of Helen, looking down at the Greek leaders from Troy's walls. She is not what you might expect — and the poem gives her a voice that is worth listening to carefully.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Paris challenges Menelaus to single combat. Menelaus immediately accepts. Why does Hector respond to Paris's challenge with such contempt — and is he right to?

**Probing:**
- Helen, on Troy's walls, identifies the Greek leaders for Priam with apparent detachment — but her description of her own situation reveals something more complex. How does the poem present her? Does she seem to be a victim, a willing participant, or something harder to categorize?
- When Aphrodite saves Paris from Menelaus and delivers him to Helen's bedroom, Helen responds with striking anger at the goddess. What does that moment reveal about how the poem understands divine compulsion versus human agency?
- A truce was negotiated. The duel was fought. A winner was apparent. And then nothing changed. What does this failed resolution tell us about the kind of war this is?

**Broader:**
- Helen is blamed by almost everyone in the ancient tradition for the war. The poem is more ambiguous. What does it mean to hold someone responsible for a situation they were partly forced into?
- Paris is beautiful, cowardly, and beloved by a goddess. Menelaus is plain, brave, and betrayed. Homer stacks the moral deck clearly. Does the universe of the poem agree with that moral stacking?

---

### Chapter 4

#### Before You Read

A truce has been arranged, and for a moment it looks as though the war might end without further bloodshed. Then a god intervenes. Book IV is about how fragile peace is — how easily it shatters — and what it takes to restart a killing machine once it has briefly stopped. The battle catalogues here are visceral and specific. Homer names the men who die. Try to hold that specificity rather than letting it blur into abstraction.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Athena engineers the breaking of the truce by persuading the Trojan archer Pandarus to shoot Menelaus. The gods, not the humans, restart the war. How does that framing affect how we assign blame?

**Probing:**
- Agamemnon walks through his troops before battle, praising some and shaming others — a commander's inspection. What does this scene add to our picture of Agamemnon as a leader?
- The battle descriptions are anatomically precise: spear through throat, sword against collarbone, arrow through shin. Scholars have noted this specificity is part of Homer's honoring of the dead — to die nameless is worse than to die painfully. Do you feel that in the reading?
- The gods watch the resumed fighting "like vultures." What does the poem suggest about the relationship between divine interest and human suffering?

**Broader:**
- Truces are broken throughout history by individuals who refuse to accept negotiated peace. The *Iliad* puts the blame, ultimately, on a god. Is that a satisfying account — or a dodge?
- Is there such a thing as a just war in the world of the *Iliad*? Does the poem seem to think so?

---

### Chapter 5

#### Before You Read

This book belongs to Diomedes — and it is unlike anything else in the poem. A mortal warrior, empowered by a goddess, does something the poem treats as almost unprecedented: he wounds gods. Not metaphorically. Literally. Aphrodite bleeds on the battlefield. Ares shouts like ten thousand men and retreats to Olympus. Book V is deliberately excessive — a warrior at the limit of what a human being can do. Watch how the poem frames this excess: with wonder, but also with discomfort.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Diomedes wounds Aphrodite and then — with Athena's encouragement — wounds Ares too. What does the poem seem to think about a mortal who can do this?

**Probing:**
- Aphrodite, wounded, flees to her mother Dione on Olympus. Dione lists other gods who have been hurt by mortals in the past and consoles her daughter. What does this scene on Olympus suggest about the gods' relationship to pain and mortality?
- Diomedes' aristeia (his great battle-day) is the most sustained example of heroic excellence in the poem. Yet Achilles, who is absent, is still called the greatest warrior. How does the poem maintain Achilles' supremacy while showing us Diomedes?
- Sarpedon — a Lycian ally of Troy and, importantly, a son of Zeus — fights against Diomedes here. Zeus cannot save him. What does this tell us about the limits of divine power?

**Broader:**
- Weil argued that force in the *Iliad* ultimately enslaves even those who wield it. Does Diomedes' rampage support or complicate that reading?
- The poem presents Diomedes as righteous in his violence, Achilles as dangerous in his rage. What distinguishes heroic force from destructive force — and does the distinction hold?

---

### Chapter 6

#### Before You Read

The battle pauses, and Hector enters the city of Troy. This book is the emotional heart of the poem's first half — perhaps of the entire poem. You will meet Hector as a son, a brother, a friend, a husband, a father. Pay close attention to each conversation he has in Troy, and to what he cannot say. The scene with his wife Andromache and their baby son Astyanax is one of the most extraordinary passages in Western literature. Read it slowly.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Hector moves through Troy speaking with his mother, Paris, Helen, and finally Andromache. Each conversation reveals something different about him. Which one struck you most, and why?

**Probing:**
- Hector explicitly tells Andromache that he knows Troy will fall and she will be enslaved — and then he goes back to fight. He is not fighting because he believes he can win. What is he fighting for?
- The baby Astyanax screams in terror at Hector's plumed helmet, and Hector removes it, laughing. It is one of the most human moments in the poem. What work does this scene do in a poem so saturated with killing?
- Helen, in her conversation with Hector, describes herself with unusual self-awareness and bitterness. How does this scene complicate whatever judgment you brought to her character?
- Hector invokes the future when he speaks to Andromache — imagining the day she is enslaved, imagining her grief when he is dead. What does it mean that he can foresee this so clearly and still choose to return?

**Broader:**
- Hector is fighting for a city, a family, a code of honor — not because he chose the war or believes it can be won. What does the poem ask us to feel about this kind of heroism?
- The farewell scene has been called one of the most anti-war passages ever written — not because it argues against war, but because it shows exactly what war destroys. Do you agree?

---

### Chapter 7

#### Before You Read

Book VII is one of the quieter structural moments of the poem — a single combat, a truce, and the collection of the dead. But don't let the lower temperature fool you. This book is about the mechanics of heroic culture: how a challenge is issued and accepted, how a fight ends with honor intact on both sides, and what happens to the bodies of the fallen. Notice how the Greeks and Trojans behave during the truce. The poem treats both sides with the same care.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Hector and Ajax fight to a draw as nightfall stops the combat. They exchange gifts before parting. What does this gesture mean in the world of the poem — and what makes it possible between enemies?

**Probing:**
- The heralds from both sides move freely between armies during the truce. What does this institution — the herald's inviolability — reveal about the rules of war in the Homeric world?
- The Trojans debate whether to give Helen back and end the war. Priam's son Antenor argues for it; Paris refuses. Priam sides with Paris. What does this scene reveal about Troy's leadership?
- The Greeks build a defensive wall around their ships during the truce. The gods watch this with some unease, as though it challenges their prerogatives. Why might the building of a wall be a theologically charged act in this world?

**Broader:**
- Both sides agree to honor their dead, regardless of which side they're on. This practice — the shared care for the dead — has been called the *Iliad*'s deepest moral commitment. What does it mean to honor an enemy's body?
- Is single combat — two individuals fighting to spare the armies — a more humane way to settle war? What does the failure of Paris's duel in Book III, and this book's less conclusive combat, suggest about that hope?

---

### Chapter 8

#### Before You Read

Zeus has made his decision: the Greeks will suffer until Achilles' honor is restored. Book VIII shows what that looks like from the outside — a battlefield where the tide turns decisively and the Greeks, despite their courage, cannot hold. We're about to see what it feels like when fate sets its thumb on the scale. The Greek retreat to their wall is not cowardice; it is the world adjusting to a divine will. Watch for the moment at sunset when the Trojans, for the first time, camp on the open plain.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Zeus explicitly forbids the other gods from helping either side, and for the first time in the poem the Trojans dominate completely. What changes when divine interference is removed — and what does that tell us about the natural balance of the war?

**Probing:**
- Hera and Athena attempt to intervene anyway and are stopped by Zeus with a detailed threat. How does this scene on Olympus affect the emotional tone of the battle scenes below?
- The Trojans light fires on the plain and sleep in the open, watching the Greek camp from the heights. It is one of the poem's few moments of almost peaceable grandeur — soldiers at rest under the stars. What is Homer doing by giving the Trojans this moment of triumph?
- Agamemnon weeps before his troops for the first time. A commander's visible grief — is this a moment of humanity or a sign of poor leadership in this world?

**Broader:**
- Zeus's "plan" to honor Achilles requires enormous Greek suffering. No individual Greek deserves this. What does the poem suggest about the relationship between divine justice and individual innocence?
- The *Iliad* does not have a villain. Hector is noble, Achilles is absent, Agamemnon is flawed but not evil. Is there a moral framework operating in this poem, or does it resist that kind of accounting?

---

### Chapter 9

#### Before You Read

This is one of the most philosophically dense passages in all of Homer. Agamemnon sends three men — Odysseus, Ajax, and the old tutor Phoenix — to offer Achilles everything: gifts, honor, Briseis returned, future wealth, a princess in marriage. By any measure of the heroic code, the offer is more than sufficient. Pay close attention to what Achilles says in response. It is not what anyone expects. He speaks in a way that sounds almost modern — and it shakes the foundations of the heroic world.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Achilles refuses the embassy's offer — gifts, honor, Briseis, a royal marriage. What reason does he give, and does it satisfy you as an explanation?

**Probing:**
- Achilles says: "I carry two sorts of destiny toward the day of my death. Either, if I stay here and fight beside the city of the Trojans, my return home is gone, but my glory shall be everlasting; but if I return home to the beloved land of my fathers, the excellence of my glory is gone, but there will be a long life left for me." He already knows the choice. Why is he still angry? What has Agamemnon actually taken from him?
- Phoenix tells Achilles a long story — the Parable of the Prayers — about a man named Meleager who refused to fight until it was too late and got no credit for eventually helping. The parable doesn't work on Achilles. Why not?
- Ajax's brief speech at the end of the embassy — "Think of your comrades here who honor you above all others" — is the most emotionally direct argument. Achilles admits it nearly moves him. What does Ajax understand about Achilles that Odysseus does not?

**Broader:**
- Achilles invents something in this book: the idea that life itself might be worth more than glory. This is a genuinely radical thought in the heroic world. Is he right?
- The embassy fails because Achilles has been dishonored in a way that cannot be undone by gifts. What would it take — in any world — to restore genuine trust after a public humiliation?

---

### Chapter 10

#### Before You Read

Book X is something of an anomaly — a night raid episode with a different texture and pace from the rest of the poem. Many ancient critics and some modern scholars believe the "Doloneia" was composed separately and inserted into the *Iliad* later; others defend its place. Whatever its origins, it gives us a different side of Odysseus: cunning, ruthless, efficient in violence in a way that contrasts sharply with Achilles' fury. Take it as a change of register — a tighter, darker piece inside the larger poem.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Odysseus and Diomedes volunteer for a dangerous night mission behind enemy lines. What does this episode reveal about Odysseus's character that we haven't seen before?

**Probing:**
- Dolon, the Trojan spy, is captured and gives up everything he knows before Diomedes kills him — despite having been promised his life. What does this moment reveal about the ethics of deception in the poem's heroic world?
- The episode ends with a raid on the Thracian camp — killing men in their sleep and stealing their famous horses. This is not glorious battle; it is closer to assassination. How does the poem frame it?
- Why do you think many scholars suspect this book was added later? What is different about its tone, its values, or its style?

**Broader:**
- Odysseus will be the hero of the *Odyssey* — a poem about cleverness, survival, and homecoming. The *Iliad* treats his kind of intelligence with a certain ambivalence. What does the Homeric tradition seem to value more: strength or cunning?
- Night raids, ambushes, and deception were real tactics of ancient warfare. Do the "rules" of heroic combat apply in the dark?

---

### Chapter 11

#### Before You Read

Book XI belongs to Agamemnon — a brief, brilliant moment of heroic action before the Greeks begin to collapse. One by one, the Greek commanders are wounded and removed from the field: Agamemnon, Diomedes, Odysseus, Machaon. The Greeks are falling apart from the top down. At the end of the book, Achilles sends Patroclus to find out who the wounded man being carried from the field is. This is the moment the mechanism of Patroclus' death begins to turn.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Agamemnon fights brilliantly at the start of Book XI and then is wounded and withdraws. What does this brief aristeia do for his character — and does it change how you see him?

**Probing:**
- As leader after leader is carried off wounded, the poem creates a cumulative sense of disaster. Notice that these men are not killed — they are *wounded*, removed from the story for now. What does the poem gain by incapacitating rather than killing its secondary heroes?
- Achilles watches the battle from his ship and notices a wounded man being carried away. He sends Patroclus to investigate. This is his first involvement since Book I. What does this small action tell us about what is still alive in him?
- Old Nestor, tending to the wounded Machaon, tells Patroclus a long story about his own youthful heroism and urges him to act. What is Nestor's argument, and why does it land differently with Patroclus than with Achilles?

**Broader:**
- The Greek army is suffering because of one man's withdrawal. Achilles' pride is costing others their lives. At what point does individual honor become a collective crime?
- Patroclus is described repeatedly as gentle, compassionate. Why might the poem choose its gentlest character to carry the plot forward to its most violent turn?

---

### Chapter 12

#### Before You Read

The Trojans have been pushing the Greeks back toward their ships. Now they face the wall the Greeks built to protect the ships — a massive defensive structure. Book XII is about what it takes to breach a fortification: not just courage, but the right kind of courage at the right moment. It ends with one of the poem's most dramatic images. Watch for the omen that is ignored, and for who ignores it.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** An eagle drops a serpent in front of the advancing Trojans — an omen almost everyone reads as a warning. Hector dismisses it and attacks anyway. Was he right to?

**Probing:**
- Sarpedon (a Lycian ally of Troy and son of Zeus) delivers a speech to his companion Glaucus explaining why they must lead the assault on the wall: "If you and I, escaping this battle, would be able to live on forever, ageless, immortal, so neither would I myself go on fighting in the foremost nor would I urge you into the fighting... But now... let us go on and win glory for ourselves, or yield it to others." What is his argument — and is it the same argument Achilles makes in Book IX?
- Hector picks up a boulder and hurls it at the gates. The image is superhuman — Homer compares him to a god carrying something no ordinary man could lift. How does this hyperbolism function in the poem?
- The wall the Greeks built is temporary — the gods have already foreseen that it will be destroyed after the war. Does the poem's knowledge of the wall's impermanence affect how you read the battle around it?

**Broader:**
- Sarpedon's speech is one of the clearest expressions of the heroic bargain in the poem: we lead because we have to justify the privileges we receive. Does this social contract hold in the world of the *Iliad*?
- Is there a version of Sarpedon's argument — risk your life in exchange for recognition and privilege — that still operates today?

---

### Chapter 13

#### Before You Read

The Trojans have broken through the wall; the fighting has moved to the Greek ships. Zeus, believing his work is done, looks away. But Poseidon — who favors the Greeks — takes advantage of his brother's distraction. Book XIII is a long, grinding book of attrition at the ships: fierce fighting, neither side able to break the other. It tests the reader's endurance the way war tests a soldier's. Let yourself feel the exhaustion of it.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Poseidon rallies the Greeks in disguise when Zeus looks away. What does this divine opportunism suggest about the relationship between the gods and the war they're overseeing?

**Probing:**
- Ajax the Lesser and Ajax the Great fight alongside each other in a famous paired action. The "two Ajaxes" — very different men sharing a name — are a minor but distinctive feature of the poem. What does Homer gain by doubling this character?
- Menelaus, often underestimated, fights with unexpected effectiveness here. The poem seems interested in revisiting its secondary characters and giving them moments of dignity. Why might this matter?
- The killing in Book XIII is dense, repetitive, and exhausting. This is deliberate. Homer is mimicking something. What?

**Broader:**
- Long attritional battles — where no decisive victory is possible — are one of history's recurring realities. What does the *Iliad* understand about why wars continue even when both sides are exhausted?
- Poseidon and Zeus have competing investments in the war's outcome. The men fighting below are partly instruments of a divine argument. Is this a satisfying way to understand conflict — that larger forces move through individual actors — or does it feel like a evasion?

---

### Chapter 14

#### Before You Read

Hera watches her husband Zeus favoring the Trojans and decides to act. What follows is one of the poem's most surprising and darkly funny episodes — a seduction, a deception, a god put to sleep. But behind the comedy is a serious act of political will: Hera is using her body as a weapon to change the course of a war. Book XIV shows the gods at their most manipulative, and the humans below at their most vulnerable.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Hera borrows Aphrodite's love-charm, seduces Zeus, and puts him to sleep — all in order to let Poseidon help the Greeks while Zeus isn't watching. What does this scene reveal about the nature of divine "order" in the poem?

**Probing:**
- The seduction of Zeus by Hera is explicitly erotic — Homer describes their lovemaking in an extended simile. Ancient commentators found this passage scandalous; later readers have seen it as comic deflation of divine authority. What do you make of it?
- Poseidon, liberated by Zeus's sleep, turns the battle decisively. Ajax stuns Hector with a thrown boulder. The Greeks rush back onto the offensive. This reversal was engineered entirely through Hera's deception. What does it say about power in the poem that war outcomes can be determined by one goddess's cunning?
- The gods throughout the poem have personal grievances, personal favorites, personal pride. They do not have moral authority. Is the poem critiquing religion — or does it reflect a genuinely different relationship between humans and divine forces?

**Broader:**
- Hera achieves more through seduction and deception than through any direct confrontation. The *Iliad* treats this as genuinely powerful. How does the poem think about the power available to those who cannot fight directly?
- We have now seen multiple gods subvert Zeus's plan. Is Zeus actually in control? Does the poem believe in any kind of ultimate authority?

---

### Chapter 15

#### Before You Read

Zeus wakes up. And he is furious. Book XV is the reckoning: the gods are forced back into their lanes, divine order is reasserted, and the Trojans resume their advance — now with Apollo personally driving the assault. The Greeks are again pushed to the edge. Hector is revived and leads his men toward the ships with something close to divine momentum. The poem is building toward a crisis that Achilles is still refusing to meet. Watch how close the ships come to burning.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Zeus wakes to find his plan disrupted, immediately reasserts control, and calmly describes what will happen next — including Patroclus' death and Achilles' eventual return. He knows everything in advance. What does this foreknowledge do to the poem's sense of tragedy?

**Probing:**
- Apollo revives Hector and leads the Trojan charge — a god personally clearing the path to the Greek ships. The Greeks resist with desperate courage. What does it mean that human bravery is not enough to stop what is coming?
- Ajax fights a long rearguard action almost alone, leaping from ship to ship. It is one of the poem's images of pure, losing heroism. What makes this kind of futile resistance worth depicting?
- Patroclus is with the healer Machaon when this battle unfolds. He is watching. His moment is almost here. How does the poem build toward his decision?

**Broader:**
- Zeus tells Hera the entire plan — including the deaths that are coming. He speaks of these futures as fixed. If everything is fated, what is the moral meaning of individual choices in the poem?
- Ajax defending the ships alone while his comrades fall back is a figure of isolated heroism that recurs across literature and history. What does this kind of last stand mean to us — and why does it keep appearing?

---

### Chapter 16

#### Before You Read

This is the pivot of the entire poem. Everything that follows — Achilles' return, Hector's death, the poem's final movement — flows from what happens in Book XVI. Patroclus has been watching the slaughter. He goes to Achilles and asks to borrow his armor, to lead the Myrmidons back into battle, to buy the Greeks time. Achilles agrees — but gives him strict limits. Watch what happens to those limits. And watch how the poem handles the death at the center of this book, with a care and precision that takes your breath away.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Patroclus weeps before Achilles and asks to be allowed to fight. Achilles, who has refused everything, agrees to this. What does Patroclus have that the embassy in Book IX did not?

**Probing:**
- Achilles tells Patroclus specifically: drive the Trojans from the ships, but do not pursue them to the walls of Troy. Patroclus ignores this. What does his disobedience suggest about his character — and about the nature of battle itself, once a fighter is in the grip of momentum?
- Zeus watches as his own son Sarpedon is killed by Patroclus. He considers saving him; Hera talks him out of it, arguing that doing so would undermine fate and set a precedent. Zeus weeps tears of blood. What does this divine grief tell us about the relationship between gods and mortality?
- Apollo is the instrument of Patroclus' death — he strikes him from behind, knocking off his helmet, stunning him. Then a mortal (Euphorbus) wounds him, and Hector delivers the killing blow. Why does the poem require three agents to kill Patroclus?

**Broader:**
- Patroclus' last words are a prophecy: Hector will be killed by Achilles. He dies knowing what his death will cost. Does that foreknowledge change how you feel about his fate?
- The question of when to listen to a warning versus when to trust your own instincts is live in Book XVI. Patroclus had a limit. He crossed it. Do you think he regretted it?

---

### Chapter 17

#### Before You Read

Patroclus is dead. Now the question is whether his body — and the armor he was wearing, which belongs to Achilles — can be recovered. Book XVII is a long, brutal fight over a corpse: Greeks trying to retrieve the body of a comrade, Trojans trying to strip it for the armor. It is a book about the obligations of the living to the dead, and about what it costs to honor those obligations. News of Patroclus' death hasn't yet reached Achilles. That is coming.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Both sides fight ferociously over Patroclus' body — a dead man already. Why does this matter so much to both Greeks and Trojans, and what does the poem gain by showing us the fight for a corpse?

**Probing:**
- Hector puts on Achilles' armor, stripped from Patroclus. Zeus watches this and reflects that Hector is wearing armor that belongs to a man who will kill him. What does it mean that the poem lets Zeus voice this tragic irony so plainly?
- Menelaus, often underestimated, is the first to stand over Patroclus' body and the last to leave it. What does this book do for his character?
- Achilles' divine horses stand weeping beside the battle, motionless, mourning Patroclus. Zeus looks at them and laments their fate — to be immortal in a mortal world. What does this extraordinary image say about the relationship between love and death?

**Broader:**
- The fight over Patroclus' body is, in part, a fight over honor — stripping a fallen enemy's armor is a mark of victory; protecting your comrade's body is a mark of loyalty. What does this code of care for the dead tell us about what the Homeric world values?
- The armor — Achilles', now worn by Hector — will be reclaimed only through Hector's death. Objects in this poem carry enormous weight: prizes, armor, gifts. What does it mean when the poem treats objects as if they hold the memory of the people who owned them?

### Chapter 18

#### Before You Read

Achilles hears the news. Book XVIII is about grief — specifically, a grief so enormous it becomes almost supernatural. Watch Achilles' physical responses when he learns Patroclus is dead. Then watch what happens when his grief begins to turn into something else. And then stay with the description of the shield Hephaestus forges: one of the most ambitious passages in all of ancient poetry, a miniature image of the entire world inscribed on a piece of bronze.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** When Achilles hears of Patroclus' death, his reaction is immediate and physical — he collapses, tears his hair, smears his face with ash. His grief is performed, public, total. What does this kind of open, embodied grief tell us about how the poem understands emotion?

**Probing:**
- Thetis hears her son's cry from the sea and comes immediately. She already knows he will die soon — this is fated — and she comes anyway to hold him and grieve with him. What does her grief tell us about the nature of divine love?
- The Shield of Achilles is a world within the poem: it shows cities at war and cities at peace, harvests, marriages, trials, dances, a lion attacking cattle. Why would Homer stop the poem's most urgent moment to describe this object in such detail?
- At the end of the book, Iris tells Achilles that the Trojans are about to drag Patroclus' body away. Achilles has no armor — Hector is wearing it. He walks to the trench and simply shouts. The Trojans flee at the sound of his voice. What is Homer saying about what Achilles is at this moment?

**Broader:**
- The shield shows both a city at war and a city at peace, side by side. Why might Homer choose to put a vision of peacetime inside the armor of the poem's greatest killer?
- Achilles' grief transforms into something that no longer looks like grief — it looks like war. Is this transformation a form of healing, or the opposite?

---

### Chapter 19

#### Before You Read

The rage has shifted. In Book I, Achilles' wrath was turned inward — against Agamemnon, against the Greeks who would not honor him. Now it is turned outward, toward Troy. Book XIX is about the transition: the formal reconciliation with Agamemnon, the arming of Achilles, and — in a moment of strange, prophetic poetry — the voice that speaks to him about what is coming. Something enormous is about to be released into the world.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Achilles publicly accepts Agamemnon's gifts and reconciles with him — not because he's forgiven him, but because Patroclus is dead and nothing else matters now. Is this a resolution, or just a suspension?

**Probing:**
- The Greeks want to eat before battle; Achilles refuses. He says he cannot eat while Patroclus lies unburied. Zeus and Athena have to intervene supernaturally to give him strength. What does this refusal tell us about the state Achilles is in?
- Achilles' horse Xanthus, given voice by a goddess, warns Achilles plainly that he will die soon — and names the agents of his death. Achilles responds: "Why do you prophesy my death, Xanthus? You need not. I myself know well that it is my destiny to die here." What does this exchange add to the poem's exploration of fate and choice?
- The arming of Achilles is described with almost liturgical deliberateness — each piece of armor named, fitted, tested. It is a ceremony of transformation. What is Achilles becoming as he puts on the armor?

**Broader:**
- Achilles knows he will die at Troy. He knows this before he makes the choice to avenge Patroclus. Does this make his decision heroic — or is it something closer to suicide?
- Reconciliation in this book is formal, public, transactional. Agamemnon is not really forgiven; the wounds are not healed. What does the poem suggest about the difference between formal peace and genuine resolution?

---

### Chapter 20

#### Before You Read

Zeus lifts all restrictions: the gods may now take sides openly and directly. What follows is a battle on two levels — gods against gods, men against men — with Achilles at the center of the human fighting. But Book XX, despite its violence, is also a book of restraints: there are several moments where Achilles *almost* kills characters he cannot kill, and divine power intervenes. The poem is managing its own pace, saving what it knows is coming.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Zeus allows all the gods to join the battle openly. Olympus itself is at war. Does divine involvement make the human stakes feel larger or smaller?

**Probing:**
- Achilles meets Aeneas on the battlefield. Aeneas is protected by Poseidon, who saves him from death — even though Poseidon favors the Greeks — because Aeneas is destined to survive Troy and carry on the Trojan line. What does this divine intervention for a Trojan tell us about the poem's cosmology?
- Apollo, disguised as a Trojan, lures Achilles away from Hector repeatedly. The poem is deliberately delaying the confrontation we all know is coming. What does this delay do to the emotional tension?
- Achilles in battle is described in terms of fire, of whirlwind, of an unstoppable force of nature. When a warrior becomes a natural phenomenon in the poem's imagery, what has happened to his humanity?

**Broader:**
- Aeneas is saved so that the Trojan line can continue — he will eventually, in later tradition, found Rome. The poem includes this destiny without explaining it. What does it mean that survival, not glory, is some characters' fate?
- The gods fight each other in this book almost comically — Athena knocks Ares down, Hera boxes Artemis's ears. Does this divine slapstick undermine the human tragedy below, or provide necessary relief?

### Chapter 21

#### Before You Read

Achilles drives Trojans into the river Scamander, filling it with bodies. And then the river itself rises against him. Book XXI is one of the most hallucinatory sequences in the poem: a mortal man fighting a god in the form of a river, nearly drowning, saved only by divine fire. Then the gods fight each other in the open plain. It is excessive, almost operatic. And through all of it, Achilles is pushing toward a single destination: Hector.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Achilles slaughters so many Trojans in the river that the river itself protests — filling with blood and bodies, unable to flow. Then Scamander attacks him. What is the poem saying when nature itself recoils from Achilles' violence?

**Probing:**
- Achilles kills the young Trojan Lycaon, who throws himself on the ground, clasps Achilles' knees, and begs for his life — having once before been ransomed after Achilles captured him. Achilles says: "Come, friend, you die also... why all this weeping and lamentation?" He kills him calmly. What has Achilles become at this point in the poem?
- Hephaestus' fire defeats the river Scamander at Hera's request. This is the second time fire defeats water in mythological tradition. What does fire symbolize in the poem's emotional register — and is it connected to Achilles?
- The gods' battle in the open plain is partly comic — Ares complaining to Zeus, Artemis fleeing after Hera spanks her with her own quiver. How does this comedy function next to Achilles' lethal seriousness below?

**Broader:**
- Achilles tells Lycaon: "Patroclus died, who was better by far than you are." Is this a reason, a justification, or neither? What has grief done to Achilles' moral reasoning?
- The poem uses the phrase "friend" (*philos*) in a jarring, almost violent way throughout this book. Achilles calls men "friend" as he kills them. What does this do to the concept of friendship in the poem?

### Chapter 22

#### Before You Read

Everything has been building toward this. Priam and Hecuba watch from the walls of Troy and beg Hector not to face Achilles alone. This is the book where the poem's two great figures finally meet — and where the outcome was never really in question. Read it slowly. Pay attention to what happens before the fight, what happens during it, and what happens after. The poem's treatment of the dead is at its most unflinching here, and also at its most morally challenging.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Priam and Hecuba beg Hector to come inside the walls. He waits for Achilles — and then, when he sees him coming, he runs. Why does Hector run? And what does the poem do with this moment?

**Probing:**
- Achilles chases Hector three times around the walls of Troy — his parents watching from the battlements, the Greek army watching from below. The gods on Olympus watch as Zeus considers saving Hector and is dissuaded. What does this extended, witnessed chase do to the emotional experience of Hector's death?
- Athena deceives Hector at the critical moment, appearing as his brother Deiphobus to trick him into stopping and fighting — and then vanishing when Hector needs him. Hector, realizing the deception, says: "So this is death... the gods have summoned me deathward." Does knowing you've been deceived change how you die?
- After killing Hector, Achilles drags his body behind his chariot three times around the walls while the Trojans watch. This is a deliberate desecration — a violation of the heroic code. What has Achilles become that makes this act possible?

**Broader:**
- Hector chose to wait outside the walls and face Achilles. He could have gone inside. The poem frames his choice as heroic — but is it? Who does his death actually serve?
- Weil wrote that the *Iliad* shows force turning even the powerful into things. Achilles in Books 20-22 is no longer exactly a person. What has replaced his personhood — and is the poem condemning this, mourning it, or something else?

### Chapter 23

#### Before You Read

The killing is over. Now the poem turns to ritual — the ceremony that transforms a death into a memory, a loss into a form of honor. Patroclus is burned and buried, and then Achilles holds funeral games: athletic contests in Patroclus' memory. Book XXIII is long and unexpectedly social, even festive. This is not diminishment; it is the poem's deep understanding of what mourning requires. Notice how Achilles conducts himself as a host and judge. Something is slowly returning to him.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Patroclus' ghost appears to Achilles in a dream and asks for burial. What does this scene tell us about what the unburied dead need — and what the living owe them?

**Probing:**
- The funeral games are detailed and specific — chariot racing, boxing, wrestling, archery — and they generate real conflict and resentment among the competitors. Achilles adjudicates these disputes with unexpected fairness and tact. What is the poem doing by giving Achilles this role at this moment?
- Nestor's son Antilochus cheats to win the chariot race and then admits it with charm; Achilles laughs. The games include both genuine contest and social negotiation. How do the games function as a form of community rebuilding after collective trauma?
- Achilles awards prizes even to those who lose — even to those who finished last. This generosity is unexpected given the Achilles we've seen in Books 20-22. Is this the same man?

**Broader:**
- Athletic competition in the ancient world was specifically a form of war-substitute — the same men who fought each other in battle competed in games. What does Book XXIII suggest about the relationship between competition, violence, and community?
- We honor the dead with public ceremony. What is the ceremony actually for — the dead, or the living?

### Chapter 24

#### Before You Read

The poem ends not with a battle but with a meeting. Old Priam, king of Troy, makes an extraordinary journey: across enemy lines, alone at night, guided by a disguised god, to kneel before Achilles and beg for his son's body. This final book is the poem's answer to its opening word. *Mēnis* — rage — has run its course. What remains is something the poem barely has a word for. Read the scene between Priam and Achilles slowly. It is one of the most astonishing things Homer ever wrote.

#### Chat Prompts

**Opening:** Priam clasps Achilles by the knees and asks him to think of his own father, Peleus, who is old and far away and will never see his son again. Achilles weeps — the first time since he armed for battle. What has Priam done that no one else in the poem could do?

**Probing:**
- Achilles and Priam eat together. They look at each other — enemy, killer of Hector, father of his killer — and they find something. The poem doesn't quite name it. What is it? Is it forgiveness? Recognition? Something that doesn't have a word?
- Apollo, Hera, and Iris all have roles in returning Hector's body. Even Zeus, who has driven the Greeks' suffering, intervenes to restore dignity to the Trojan dead. What does it mean that the gods, who caused so much of this, also attend to its resolution?
- The poem ends not with Achilles' death (which we know is coming) but with Hector's burial. "So they performed the burial rites of Hector, breaker of horses." Why does Homer end here, with the Trojan hero and the ritual of burial, rather than with Achilles?
- Hermes, guiding Priam through the Greek camp, wakes him before dawn and tells him to leave quickly — Agamemnon is unpredictable. This small warning is a reminder of how fragile the grace of this scene is, how easily it could be destroyed. What does it say that kindness, in this poem, exists only in stolen hours?

**Broader:**
- Achilles tells Priam: "Bear up, nor mourn endlessly in your heart... for there is not any advantage to be won from grim lamentation." He says this having spent the whole poem in grief and rage. Is he right? Has he earned the right to say it?
- The *Iliad* ends with an enemy's funeral. No victory, no homecoming, no triumph. What kind of poem ends this way — and what does it ask you to carry away from it?
- Weil wrote that the *Iliad* achieves something almost no other work of literature manages: it looks at force without flinching and without glorifying. Does Book XXIV support her reading? Does the compassion of the final scene redeem, complicate, or simply coexist with everything that came before?
