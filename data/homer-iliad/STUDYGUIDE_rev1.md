# Study Guide: The Iliad
## Homer (c. 8th–7th century BCE)

---

## Before You Read

We don't know who Homer was. That's the first thing to understand, and it's important.

"Homer" is a name attached to two epics — the *Iliad* and the *Odyssey* — that were composed in ancient Greek sometime around the 8th or 7th century BCE. Whether Homer was a single poet, a tradition, a name applied to a school of singers, or a brilliant individual who crystallized centuries of oral performance into something fixed — scholars have debated this since antiquity. What we can say is this: the poems exist, they are extraordinary, and they are old. Old in a way that most readers haven't fully reckoned with.

The Trojan War, if it happened at all, was fought around 1180 BCE — during the Bronze Age. Homer composed the *Iliad* roughly 400 years after those events. He was separated from his subject by about the same span of time that separates us from the American Revolution. In between was the Greek Dark Ages: a period of collapse, depopulation, and forgetting so severe that writing itself was lost and then reinvented. Homer was composing from the far side of a cultural catastrophe, drawing on traditions that had survived only in oral performance — singers carrying the past forward in their bodies and voices.

This matters for how you read. The *Iliad* is not a novel. It is not a play. It began as something sung, probably to a lyre, by professional singer-poets (*aoidoi*) performing at aristocratic courts and public festivals. The American classicist Milman Parry showed in the 1920s that the poem is saturated with "formulas" — repeated phrases that fit the meter: *swift-footed Achilles*, *rosy-fingered Dawn*, *the wine-dark sea*. These formulas were not clichés; they were tools. An oral poet improvising in real time needed ready-made building blocks to fill out the rhythm while his mind moved forward. Every repeated epithet is a trace of a tradition in which the poem was alive, changing slightly with each performance, carried by the memories of singers who had never read a book.

So when you hear "rosy-fingered Dawn" for the fifth time, don't skip it. Sit with it. It is a piece of ancient technology, and it is also quietly beautiful.

The poem begins with a word: *mēnis*. Rage. In ancient Greek, *mēnis* was almost exclusively reserved for the wrath of gods — a divine, overwhelming, annihilating fury. Homer uses it in the poem's first line to describe a man: Achilles, son of the sea-nymph Thetis and the mortal king Peleus, greatest fighter in the Greek army. The *Iliad* is not the story of the Trojan War. It covers only a few weeks near the end of a ten-year siege. It is the story of what happens when one man's rage tears a world apart — and what it takes, finally, to put it down.

Achilles is at the center, but the *Iliad* is not simply his story. It is also Hector's. If Achilles is the Greeks' greatest warrior, Hector is the Trojans' — a man fighting to defend his city, his wife, his infant son, in a war his brother Paris started. Hector is, in many ways, the poem's moral center: a soldier who knows his city will fall, who tells his wife as much, and goes back to the wall anyway. His dignity and his doom are inseparable.

Then there is old Priam, the king of Troy, whose walk across the enemy camp in the poem's final book is one of the most daring scenes in all of Western literature. And Agamemnon, commander of the Greeks, whose pride and poor judgment set the catastrophe in motion. And Patroclus, whose death is the hinge on which everything turns.

The French philosopher Simone Weil, writing in 1939 as France fell to Germany, read the *Iliad* as a poem about *force* — "that which turns anyone subjected to it into a thing." Weil noticed what many readers miss: the poem does not glorify war. It shows war's glory and its horror simultaneously, refusing to look away from either. It treats Greeks and Trojans with the same compassion, the same unflinching eye. No one controls force; it controls everyone who touches it, including the powerful.

What kind of attention does the poem reward? Slow attention. Notice who speaks and who is silent. Notice when a character's name is given before death — Homer almost always names a man right before killing him, a quiet act of dignity. Notice the similes: moments when the poem suddenly pulls back from the battlefield and shows us peasants harvesting grain, or bees swarming from a hollow rock, or a woman dyeing wool. These are glimpses of a world of ordinary life that the war is consuming. They are the poem's heartbeat.

You're reading Samuel Butler's 1898 prose translation. Butler used Roman names for the Greek gods — Zeus is Jove, Athena is Minerva, Hera is Juno, Poseidon is Neptune, Hephaestus is Vulcan, Ares is Mars. This can be momentarily disorienting. Stick with it. The prose is clear and moves quickly.

The *Iliad* is 15,693 lines long. It has been read continuously for nearly three thousand years. No one who has read it carefully has walked away unchanged. The question is what it changes in you — and that depends entirely on the attention you bring.

## Session 1: The Quarrel (Books 1–2)

### What to read

Nine years into the siege of Troy, and something breaks inside the Greek camp before a single scene of battle. The poem opens with a quarrel — Greek against Greek — and immediately tangles the gods in what looks like a dispute over property. The first word of the poem is *mēnis*: rage. Watch whose rage it is, what triggered it, and what it will cost everyone else.

Three concepts to carry through the whole poem: *mēnis* (rage, the divine, annihilating kind), *timē* (honor as social recognition, measured in prizes and public standing), and *kleos* (glory — "what others hear about you" — the only immortality available to a hero in a world with no afterlife worth wanting).

### Book 1

A plague from Apollo punishes the Greeks for Agamemnon's refusal to ransom the priest Chryses' daughter. At Achilles' urging, Agamemnon agrees to return her — but retaliates by seizing Briseis, Achilles' own prize, a public humiliation before the entire army. Achilles withdraws from battle and asks his mother Thetis, a sea-goddess, to persuade Zeus to favor the Trojans until his honor is restored. Zeus agrees.

### Book 2

Agamemnon tests his troops' will by falsely suggesting they sail home — and the army nearly does, stampeding to the ships before Odysseus and Hera stop them. The common soldier Thersites voices what many men must be thinking and is beaten into silence by Odysseus. Then the Catalog of Ships: hundreds of lines listing every Greek contingent and commander, their ships and home cities, followed by the Trojan allies and their lands.

You can skim the Catalog — no one will quiz you on the contingent from Boeotia. But let a few names land as you pass through. These were real people, or were believed to be, which amounts to the same thing. The poem pauses here for three hundred names because someone had to remember them. Epic memory sounds like this: a list of the dead, recited so they don't vanish entirely. That's what Homer is doing.

### Discussion

**Opening**

Walk me through the quarrel in Book 1. Who did what, and in what order? After reading it — who do you think had the stronger case, Achilles or Agamemnon? Does the poem seem to agree with you?

**Comprehension probes**

- The goddess Athena physically stops Achilles from drawing his sword, visible only to him. What does that moment suggest about how the poem understands the relationship between divine will and human choice?
- Achilles' mother Thetis is a sea-goddess who can appeal directly to Zeus. How does this fact — that one side in a camp dispute has a divine ally — change the stakes of what looks like a purely human quarrel?
- Nestor, the oldest Greek commander, tries to mediate and is ignored. What does the poem seem to be saying about wisdom and its limits in a world governed by *timē*?
- Odysseus beats the common soldier Thersites back into silence when Thersites says what many soldiers must be thinking. The poem appears to endorse this. Does it sit comfortably with you?

**Character focus**

*Achilles.* His rage is real and his grievance is real. But he responds by withdrawing from battle and asking his goddess mother to make his own comrades suffer. Is his rage justified — and is there a difference between justified grievance and self-destructive pride?

*Agamemnon.* He has authority, and he uses it to publicly humiliate the greatest warrior in his army. Is he a bully, or is he a king doing what kings do — asserting precedence, maintaining hierarchy, refusing to let a subordinate dictate terms? Does the poem ask you to choose?

**Broader**

- *Timē* — honor measured in social recognition and tangible prizes — is the social currency of this world. The whole catastrophe flows from a dispute about it. Do we still live by some version of this code, or have we replaced it with something else?
- Achilles' withdrawal is a conscious refusal to fight for a commander who dishonored him. At what point does a justified grievance become something worse?

## Session 2: The World Opens (Books 3–9)

### What to read

The world expands. These books move from the quarrel at the ships into something much larger: duels, the city of Troy itself, divine comedy on Olympus, a war that almost ends, and a failed embassy. You'll meet Helen — not quite the way you expect. You'll meet Hector at home with his family. And at the end, in Book 9, you'll watch Achilles refuse everything.

Two things to track through this chunk: what does Hector fight *for*, and what does Achilles actually want?

### Book 3

Paris — whose abduction of Helen started the whole war — steps forward to challenge Menelaus to single combat. The armies stop. If the duel settles it, the war is over. Menelaus wins cleanly, and Aphrodite rescues Paris at the last moment, whisking him to his bedroom and presenting him to Helen. Helen confronts the goddess with striking anger. The duel resolves nothing.

### Book 4

A real truce is arranged. Then Athena persuades the Trojan archer Pandarus to shoot Menelaus, wounding him and breaking the truce. The gods restart the war the humans were about to end. Full battle resumes across the plain.

### Book 5

This book belongs to Diomedes. Empowered by Athena, he does something the poem treats as almost unprecedented: he wounds Aphrodite on the battlefield, drawing the divine ichor that passes for blood. Then, at Athena's explicit encouragement, he wounds Ares himself. The most superhuman moment in the poem — a mortal at the outer limit of what a mortal can do.

### Book 6

The battle pauses and Hector enters Troy. He moves through the city in sequence: his mother Hecuba, Paris, Helen, and finally his wife Andromache and their infant son Astyanax at the city walls. He tells Andromache plainly that he knows Troy will fall and she will be enslaved. The baby screams at his father's plumed helmet; Hector removes it, laughing. Then he puts it back on and returns to fight.

### Book 7

Hector challenges any Greek to single combat; Ajax is chosen by lot. They fight to an honorable draw as darkness falls and exchange gifts before parting. Both sides collect their dead under truce. The Greeks build a defensive wall around their ships.

### Book 8

Zeus forbids divine interference and tilts the battle toward Troy — keeping his promise to Thetis. The Greeks fall back to their wall. For the first time, the Trojans camp on the open plain, fires lit in the dark, watching the Greek camp from outside. The image is almost peaceful: an army at rest under the stars, on the edge of winning.

### Book 9

Agamemnon, frightened, sends an embassy to Achilles — Odysseus, Ajax, and old Phoenix, his childhood tutor. They offer everything: lavish gifts, Briseis returned, future wealth, a royal marriage. By any measure of the heroic code, the offer is more than sufficient. Achilles refuses it all. His speech is one of the strangest in the poem — he speaks of life as worth more than glory, a thought with almost no precedent in the heroic world. The embassy fails and returns empty-handed.

### Discussion

**Opening**

Book 9 is the hinge of this whole chunk. Walk me through the embassy: who went, what they offered, and what Achilles said. What reason does he actually give for refusing — and does it satisfy you as an explanation?

**Comprehension probes**

- When Aphrodite saves Paris from Menelaus and delivers him to Helen's bedroom, Helen responds with striking anger at the goddess. What does that scene reveal about how the poem understands divine compulsion versus human agency?
- Diomedes wounds Ares and Aphrodite — literal Olympian gods. The poem treats this as extraordinary, almost transgressive. Does it frame the excess with admiration, with alarm, or both?
- Hector tells Andromache plainly that he knows Troy will fall and she will be enslaved — and then goes back to fight. He is not fighting because he thinks he can win. What is he fighting for?
- Ajax's argument in the embassy is the shortest and most direct: "Think of your comrades here who honor you above all others." Achilles admits it nearly moves him. What does Ajax understand about Achilles that Odysseus doesn't?

**Character focus**

*Hector.* The poem's most fully human figure — warrior, husband, father, soldier fighting in a war his brother started, defending a city he knows is already lost. The scene with Andromache and Astyanax is the poem's most anti-war passage — not because it argues against war, but because it shows precisely what war destroys. What is Hector fighting for, if not victory?

*Diomedes.* He wounds gods, he fights without ego, he is magnificent in a way that feels clean and uncomplicated. The poem presents his aristeia as what heroism looks like when it's working properly. What distinguishes his kind of force from Achilles' kind?

**Broader**

- Achilles says he carries two fates: stay at Troy, die young, win *kleos* that lasts forever — or go home, live long, and be forgotten. He knows which he'll choose. Does that make his refusal to fight tragic, or something else?
- Hector is fighting for something he knows is already lost. Does that make his heroism more admirable, or does it make it a kind of waste?

## Session 3: The Trojans Press (Books 10–15)

### What to read

Achilles is gone. These books are what his absence costs. The Greek commanders are wounded one by one. The wall is breached. The fighting reaches the ships. Watch how the poem accumulates the disaster slowly, through exhaustion rather than single catastrophe.

Feel the weight of it. This is what pride looks like from the outside.

### Book 10

A night raid. Odysseus and Diomedes volunteer for a dangerous mission behind enemy lines. They capture the Trojan scout Dolon, who gives up everything he knows before Diomedes kills him — despite an implicit promise of safety. They then slaughter the Thracian king Rhesus and his men in their sleep and steal his famous horses. Different in texture from anything around it: tighter, darker, closer to assassination than battle.

### Book 11

Agamemnon has a brilliant morning of fighting — his own brief aristeia — then is wounded and withdraws. One by one, the Greek commanders fall: Diomedes, Odysseus, Machaon. The army is collapsing from the top down. Patroclus, watching from Achilles' ship, sees the carnage and is shaken. Achilles notices a wounded man being carried from the field and sends Patroclus to find out who it is. A small motion. The mechanism is beginning to turn.

### Book 12

The Trojans storm the Greek defensive wall. An eagle drops a serpent in front of the advancing army — a clear omen, read by almost everyone as a warning. Hector dismisses it and attacks. He picks up a boulder no ordinary man could lift and hurls it at the gates, shattering them. The wall falls.

### Book 13

The fighting moves inside, to the ships. Zeus, believing his work is done, looks away. Poseidon, who favors the Greeks, takes advantage and rallies them in disguise. The two Ajaxes fight side by side. Neither side can break the other. The killing is dense, repetitive, and grinding — deliberately. Homer is making you feel the attrition.

### Book 14

Hera decides to act. She borrows Aphrodite's love-charm, seduces Zeus, and puts him to sleep on a mountain peak while the battle rages below. Poseidon seizes the opening and leads the Greeks in a counter-assault. Ajax stuns Hector with a thrown boulder. The Greeks push back — all because one goddess outmaneuvered her husband.

### Book 15

Zeus wakes. He is furious. He reasserts control, recalls Poseidon, and calmly tells Hera exactly what will happen next — including Patroclus' death and Achilles' return. He already knows it all. Apollo revives Hector and leads the Trojans back to the ships. Ajax defends them almost alone, leaping from deck to deck, a figure of pure, losing heroism. The ships come close to burning.

### Discussion

**Opening**

What is the effect of reading these six books without Achilles? Walk me through the Greek situation by the end of Book 15: who's still standing, what's happened to the wall, how close are the Trojans to the ships?

**Comprehension probes**

- Dolon is captured, gives up everything, and is killed anyway. What does this tell us about the ethics of the poem's heroic world — and is it different from the ethics of open battle?
- Hera achieves more through seduction and deception than through any direct confrontation. The poem treats this as genuine power. How does it think about the kinds of power available to those who can't fight directly?
- Zeus tells Hera exactly what will happen — Patroclus' death, Achilles' return, Hector's fall — and he speaks of it all as fixed. If everything is fated, what is the moral meaning of any individual choice in this poem?
- Ajax defending the ships alone — leaping from deck to deck, fighting a battle he can't win — is one of the poem's images of pure, losing heroism. What does the poem do with resistance that can't succeed?

**Character focus**

*Odysseus.* Clever, pragmatic, always surviving — in Book 11 he escapes from an encircled position with characteristic efficiency. The *Iliad* treats his kind of intelligence with real ambivalence. What does it mean to always survive, to always find the pragmatic path? What does that cost him in the world this poem values?

*Zeus.* He is nominally in charge. But Hera deceives him, Poseidon defies him, and he has to reassert control multiple times. He calmly describes the future as already determined. Is he governing, or is he simply the name for whatever happens to win out in the end?

**Broader**

- The Greek army is suffering because one man withdrew. No individual Greek soldier deserves what's happening to them. What does the poem think about collective punishment — and who is actually responsible for it?
- Long attritional battles where no decisive victory is possible are one of history's recurring realities. What does the *Iliad* understand about why wars continue even when both sides are exhausted?

## Session 4: The Pivot (Books 16–18)

### What to read

Three books. The emotional center of the poem. Everything that follows — Achilles' return, Hector's death, the final movement to Priam — flows from what happens here.

Patroclus. His death. Achilles finding out.

Read Book 16 slowly. Read Book 18 slowly. The grief in Book 18 is unlike anything else in ancient literature.

### Book 16

Patroclus comes to Achilles weeping and begs to be allowed to fight — at minimum, to lead the Myrmidons wearing Achilles' armor, to drive the panic of Achilles' name into the Trojans. Achilles agrees, and gives him a strict limit: push the Trojans from the ships, but do not pursue them to the walls of Troy. Patroclus enters battle and the Trojans break in panic. He kills Sarpedon — Zeus's own son — and pushes forward, past the limit, all the way to Troy's walls. Apollo strikes him from behind, knocking his helmet off, stunning him. Euphorbus wounds him. Hector delivers the killing blow. Patroclus dies prophesying Hector's own death.

### Book 17

Both sides fight over Patroclus' corpse — the Greeks to retrieve it, the Trojans to strip the armor for a trophy. Hector puts on Achilles' armor, stripped from the body. Zeus watches and notes the irony plainly: Hector is wearing armor that belongs to the man who will kill him. Achilles' immortal horses stand motionless in the battle, weeping, refusing to move — Zeus looks at them and laments that they are immortal in a mortal world. Menelaus and Ajax finally recover the body as darkness falls, but the armor is gone.

### Book 18

Antilochus brings the news to Achilles. Achilles collapses. He tears his hair, smears his face with ash, screams — and the cry carries to the sea, where Thetis hears it from her underwater cave and comes immediately with her sisters. She already knows her son will die soon. She comes anyway. Achilles tells her he will kill Hector. She tells him that after Hector dies, he dies. He says: I know. Then he walks to the edge of the trench and simply shouts — and the Trojans, who are about to drag the body away, flee at the sound of his voice. Thetis goes to Hephaestus, who forges new armor overnight — including the Shield: cities at war and cities at peace, harvests, weddings, dances, a lion attacking cattle, the great river Oceanus at the rim. The whole world on a piece of bronze.

### Discussion

**Opening**

Walk me through the death of Patroclus. What did he ask for, what limits did Achilles set, and what happened to those limits?

**Comprehension probes**

- Patroclus ignores Achilles' explicit instructions not to pursue to the walls of Troy. What does his disobedience tell us about his character — and about what battle does to a fighter once the momentum takes over?
- Three agents kill Patroclus: Apollo, Euphorbus, Hector. Why does the poem require three of them? What would it mean if Hector had simply killed him alone?
- Hector puts on Achilles' armor. Zeus watches and notes that Hector is wearing armor belonging to the man who will kill him. The poem lets Zeus voice this tragic irony quietly, without drama. What does it gain by doing that?
- Achilles at the trench, without any armor, shouts — and the Trojans flee. The poem treats this as entirely believable. What is Achilles at this moment? What has grief made him?

**Character focus**

*Patroclus.* Who is he to Achilles? The poem doesn't specify — the relationship is the deepest one Achilles has, whatever name you give it. Patroclus is described throughout as gentle, compassionate. What does it mean that the poem's gentlest character is the one whose death turns everything? And what does his disobedience — crossing the limit, pushing to the walls — tell us about who he was?

*Achilles in grief.* Compare this Achilles to the raging Achilles of Book 1. In Book 1, his pride was wounded and he withdrew. Here, something is gone from him entirely. How is this grief different from that rage? He walks to the trench and screams, and armies flee. Which is more dangerous — the Achilles of Book 1, or this one?

**Broader**

- The Shield of Achilles shows a world at war and a world at peace, side by side — harvests, weddings, dances, alongside siege and slaughter. Homer stops the poem's most urgent moment to describe this object in elaborate detail. Why would you put a vision of the whole peaceable world inside the armor of its greatest killer?
- Patroclus had a limit. He crossed it. The poem doesn't moralize about this — it just shows what followed. Is there a version of his choice that was right, or was crossing the limit always going to end here?

## Session 5: The Return (Books 19–22)

### What to read

Achilles comes back. These four books are the poem's terrifying final movement before the quiet of the ending. The reconciliation with Agamemnon, the new armor, the aristeia — and then the long, witnessed death of Hector.

Achilles in these books is not the sulking hero of Book 1, or the shattered mourner of Book 18. He is something else. Watch what the poem does with that.

### Book 19

Achilles publicly accepts Agamemnon's gifts and reconciles with him — not because he forgives him, but because Patroclus is dead and none of it matters anymore. The Greeks want to eat before battle; Achilles refuses. He cannot eat while Patroclus lies unburied. Zeus and Athena have to intervene supernaturally to keep him standing. His horse Xanthus, given voice by a goddess, warns him plainly: you will die soon at Troy. Achilles says: I know. He arms and rides out.

### Book 20

Zeus lifts all restrictions. The gods may now take sides openly. The battle has two levels: gods against gods on one plane, men against men below. Achilles begins his rampage. He faces Aeneas — who is rescued by Poseidon, even though Poseidon favors the Greeks, because Aeneas is fated to survive Troy. Apollo repeatedly lures Achilles away from Hector, delaying the confrontation the poem has been building toward since Book 16.

### Book 21

Achilles drives Trojans into the river Scamander, filling it with bodies until it can no longer flow. The river itself rises against him — floods, shouts, protests the desecration. Achilles nearly drowns. Hephaestus' fire, sent by Hera, defeats the river. The gods fight each other on the plain. Achilles kills the young Trojan Lycaon, who has thrown himself at his knees and begged for his life. Achilles says: "Come, friend, you die also. Patroclus died, who was better by far than you." And kills him without rage, almost calmly.

### Book 22

Priam and Hecuba watch from the walls and beg Hector to come inside. Every other Trojan has retreated. Hector stands alone outside the gates. Achilles comes. And Hector runs. Three circuits of the walls — his parents watching from above, the Greek army watching from below, the gods watching from Olympus. Zeus holds the scales of fate. Athena appears to Hector disguised as his brother Deiphobus, tricks him into stopping, and vanishes when Hector calls for help. Hector realizes the deception: "The gods have called me deathward." He turns and fights. Achilles kills him. Then ties the body to his chariot and drags it around the walls.

### Discussion

**Opening**

Hector waits outside the walls. His parents beg him to come inside. Every other Trojan has retreated. Then, when he sees Achilles coming, he runs. Walk me through it: why does he wait, and why does he run?

**Comprehension probes**

- Achilles refuses to eat before battle, and the gods have to sustain him supernaturally. What does this tell us about what it would mean, right now, for Achilles to treat himself as a living human being?
- Achilles says to Lycaon: "Come, friend, you die also. Patroclus died, who was better by far than you." Is this a reason? A justification? Or has his moral reasoning simply stopped?
- Hector, realizing Athena has deceived him, doesn't despair or rage. He says: "So the gods have called me deathward." And turns to fight. What does his response to the deception tell us about his character?
- Achilles drags Hector's body behind his chariot in front of the entire city. This is the exact opposite of what he did with Ajax at the end of Book 7 — those enemies exchanged gifts. What has happened to Achilles?

**Character focus**

*Achilles in his aristeia.* The poem describes him as fire, as whirlwind, as something nature itself recoils from. He calls men "friend" as he kills them. He fights a river. He defiles a corpse. Weil wrote that force turns even those who wield it into things. Is that what we're watching — and is the poem condemning it, mourning it, or something else?

*Hector's final choice.* He could have gone inside the walls. He waited. He ran. He turned back and died. Each of those decisions is its own thing. The poem shows them without judgment, his family watching from the battlements. What is Hector defending at the end — his city, his honor, or something he couldn't name if you asked him?

**Broader**

- Achilles knew before he returned to battle that he would die at Troy. He knew it before he made the choice to avenge Patroclus. Does that make his return heroic — or is it something closer to choosing death?
- The poem opened with *mēnis* — Achilles' rage. By Books 20–22, what has that rage become? Is it the same thing it was in Book 1?

## Session 6: The Ending (Books 23–24)

### What to read

Two books that feel nothing alike, held together by the poem's deepest commitments.

Book 23 is almost festive — funeral games, athletic contests, real conflict and laughter among the competitors. It's the poem insisting on the living after so much death.

Book 24 is the ending the poem has been building toward since its first word. An old king, alone, crossing the dark plain.

Read both slowly.

### Book 23

Patroclus' ghost appears to Achilles in a dream and asks for burial — he cannot cross into the underworld until he is burned. The cremation and funeral follow. Then Achilles holds elaborate games in Patroclus' honor: chariot racing, boxing, wrestling, throwing, archery. The Greeks compete fiercely and real disputes break out. Achilles judges them with unexpected fairness and generosity — awarding prizes even to those who finished last, defusing resentment with quiet tact. Something is slowly returning to him.

### Book 24

Achilles continues to defile Hector's body, dragging it around Patroclus' tomb every day. The gods preserve it miraculously from decay. Zeus intervenes: enough. He sends Thetis to tell Achilles to accept a ransom, and Iris to tell Priam: go to Achilles, go alone, bring gifts. Priam obeys. He crosses the Greek lines at night, guided by Hermes in disguise, and enters Achilles' tent. He clasps Achilles by the knees — the posture of supplication — and asks him to think of his own father, Peleus, who is old and far away and will never see his son again. Achilles weeps. The first time since he armed for battle. They weep together — Priam for Hector, Achilles for his father and for Patroclus. Achilles returns the body and promises eleven days of truce for the funeral. They eat. Priam sleeps. Before dawn Hermes wakes him and sends him quietly home — Agamemnon is unpredictable; the grace of this scene is fragile. Hector is buried. The poem ends.

### Discussion

**Opening**

Priam clasps Achilles' knees and asks him to think of his own father. Walk me through that scene. What does Priam say — and what happens in Achilles when he says it?

**Comprehension probes**

- Patroclus' ghost cannot enter the underworld until he is buried. The ritual of burial appears at every hinge point of the poem's final movement — the cremation of Patroclus, the fight over Hector's body, the ransom scene. What does burial mean in this poem? What does it restore, and for whom?
- The funeral games in Book 23 include real petty disputes — Antilochus cheats, Achilles laughs about it, an old man gets a consolation prize. The poem is almost comic here. What is it doing with this lightness, placed between the grief of Book 22 and the gravity of Book 24?
- Apollo, Hera, Iris, Hermes, Thetis, Zeus — the gods are all involved in returning Hector's body. Even Zeus, who drove so much of the Greeks' suffering, intervenes to restore dignity to the Trojan dead. What does it mean that the gods who caused this also attend to its resolution?
- Hermes wakes Priam before dawn and urges him to leave quickly — Agamemnon is unpredictable. This small warning is a reminder of how fragile everything in this scene is, how easily it could be destroyed. What does it say that genuine kindness, in this poem, exists only in stolen hours?

**Character focus**

*Achilles in Book 24.* He weeps. He feeds Priam. He returns the body. He promises eleven days of truce. This is the man who was dragging Hector's corpse around a tomb two days ago. What has changed — and is this the same arc as the rage, or something entirely different? The poem doesn't call it redemption. Does that stop you from calling it that?

*Priam.* He is the most daring figure in the poem's final movement — not because he is powerful, but because he goes anyway. He walks alone across the dark plain, enters his enemy's tent, and does something the heroic code would call humiliating: he begs. What does his bravery look like? And why does the poem give this act — the one that breaks through Achilles' grief — to an old man with nothing left to lose?

**Broader**

- Achilles tells Priam: "Bear up, nor mourn endlessly in your heart — there is not any advantage to be won from grim lamentation." He says this having spent most of the poem in grief and rage. Is he right? Has he earned the right to say it?
- The poem that opened with *mēnis* — rage — ends with two enemies weeping together. What is the poem's answer, finally, to the question its first word asked? Where did the rage go?
- The *Iliad* ends not with a victory, not with Achilles' death, not with the fall of Troy — but with an enemy's funeral. "So they performed the burial rites of Hector, breaker of horses." Why here? What kind of poem ends this way — and what does it ask you to carry away from it?
