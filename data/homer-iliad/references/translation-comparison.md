## Overview

The *Iliad* has been translated into English continuously since the 16th century. Each translator faces a fundamental set of choices:

1. **Meter vs. prose**: The original is dactylic hexameter, a meter with no exact English equivalent. Translators choose blank verse, heroic couplets, prose, or their own invented meters.
2. **Archaism vs. contemporary idiom**: How much should the language feel ancient?
3. **Literal fidelity vs. poetic flow**: Staying close to Homer's Greek vs. producing a readable English poem.
4. **Epithets**: Homer's repeated phrases ("swift-footed Achilles," "rosy-fingered Dawn") are metrically functional in Greek. How should they be handled in English — kept literally, varied, or streamlined?

Matthew Arnold's 1861 lectures *On Translating Homer* identified four essential qualities the translator must honor: Homer is (i) eminently rapid; (ii) plain and direct in thought and expression; (iii) plain and direct in substance; (iv) eminently noble.

## Major English Translations

### George Chapman (1611)
The first complete English translation of the *Iliad*. Published in installments beginning 1598, completed 1611. Written in "fourteeners" (14-syllable lines), a long ballad meter. Established itself quickly as a classic of English poetry; Pope praised "the daring fiery spirit" of Chapman's rendering. Made famous by Keats's sonnet *On First Looking into Chapman's Homer* (1816).

**Availability:** Public domain. Available at Project Gutenberg.

---

### Alexander Pope (1715–1720)
Pope's translation in heroic couplets (rhymed iambic pentameter) is "the classic translation that was built on all the preceding versions" and, like Chapman's, is a major poetic work in its own right. Pope's Homer dominated English culture for over a century, though later scholars criticized its departures from the Greek.

**Availability:** Public domain. Available at Project Gutenberg.

---

### William Cowper (1791)
Blank verse translation praised for its greater fidelity to the Greek than either Chapman or Pope. Cowper wrote in his preface: "I have omitted nothing; I have invented nothing." Influenced by Milton's blank verse.

**Availability:** Public domain. Available at Project Gutenberg.

---

### Samuel Butler (1898)
**Prose translation** — the one used on this platform (greatbooks.fm). Butler translated the *Iliad* into plain English prose, using Roman names for the gods (Jove for Zeus, Minerva for Athena, etc.). The Butler translation has been valued for clarity and accessibility, though it sacrifices verse form entirely. Butler graduated from Cambridge (Classics, 1859) and published this translation with Longmans.

**Availability:** Public domain. Available at Project Gutenberg and MIT Internet Classics Archive (https://classics.mit.edu/Homer/iliad.html).

---

### Richmond Lattimore (1951)
University of Chicago Press. "A free six-beat" line-for-line rendering in often unidiomatic, often archaic English. Lattimore aimed for fidelity to Homer's structure and diction rather than modern readability. Widely used in university courses for decades; considered the standard "scholarly" translation of the 20th century.

**Availability:** Copyrighted. Available for purchase; widely available in libraries.

---

### Robert Fitzgerald (1974)
Oxford World's Classics. Uses shorter, mostly iambic lines and numerous allusions to earlier English poetry. More free in its approach than Lattimore, producing a more lyrical English text.

**Availability:** Copyrighted. Available for purchase.

---

### Robert Fagles (1990)
Penguin Classics. Bolder than Lattimore in adding contemporary American-English idioms to convey Homer's formulaic language. Introduction and notes by Bernard Knox. Widely considered the most readable literary translation of the late 20th century; widely used in university courses.

**Availability:** Copyrighted. Available for purchase.

---

### Stanley Lombardo (1997)
Hackett Publishing. Like Fagles, uses contemporary American idioms. More colloquial and kinetic than Fagles; praised for capturing the energy of battle scenes.

**Availability:** Copyrighted. Available for purchase.

---

### Stephen Mitchell (2011)
Simon & Schuster. Draws on the Greek text to present a modernized English version; includes a detailed introduction. Mitchell also abridged the text, removing passages he considered interpolations. [The abridgement is controversial among scholars.]

**Availability:** Copyrighted. Available for purchase.

---

### Caroline Alexander (2015)
Ecco/HarperCollins. The first full-length English translation by a woman. Aims for literal fidelity while maintaining poetic quality. Widely praised by classicists for its accuracy and careful handling of the epithets.

**Availability:** Copyrighted. Available for purchase.

---

### Emily Wilson (2023)
W. W. Norton. Uses unrhymed iambic pentameter. Wilson, who also produced a celebrated translation of the *Odyssey* (2017, the first by a woman), brings a focus on gender, power, and social dynamics in her translation choices. The New Yorker ran a major profile on her Homer translations in September 2023.

**Availability:** Copyrighted. Available for purchase.

## Note on the Butler Translation (Used on greatbooks.fm)

The Butler translation used on this platform has one notable quirk: it uses Roman names for Greek gods throughout — **Jove** (Zeus), **Minerva** (Athena), **Juno** (Hera), **Neptune** (Poseidon), **Vulcan** (Hephaestus), **Mars** (Ares), **Diana** (Artemis). This can cause initial confusion for readers expecting the Greek names. The Butler prose is otherwise clear and accessible.
