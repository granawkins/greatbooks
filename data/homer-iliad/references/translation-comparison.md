# English Translations of the Iliad: A Comparative Guide

## Why Translation Matters

The Iliad is written in Homeric Greek — a literary dialect that was probably never anyone's everyday spoken language, even in Homer's time. It is dense, formulaic, archaic, and metrically strict (dactylic hexameter: long-short-short, long-short-short). No English translation can fully render all of this. Every translator makes tradeoffs.

The major tradeoffs:
- **Meter**: Should English try to reproduce Greek meter (hexameter)? Use another meter? Free verse? Prose?
- **Diction**: Should the English sound archaic/elevated or modern/colloquial?
- **Fidelity**: How close should the translation stay to the Greek word-for-word?
- **Epithets**: How to handle repeated formulas? Translate literally every time? Vary them? Omit?
- **Speed vs. Weight**: Greek epic moves fast. Some translations gain poetry by slowing down; others gain energy by speeding up.

## The Major English Translations

### George Chapman (1598–1611)
**Sample**: "Achilles' baneful wrath resound, O Goddess, that imposed / Infinite sorrows on the Greeks, and many brave souls loosed..."

The first great English Iliad. Chapman used heroic couplets (14-syllable "fourteeners") for the Iliad. Made famous by Keats's sonnet "On First Looking into Chapman's Homer" (1816): "Then felt I like some watcher of the skies / When a new planet swims into his ken." Highly poetic, Elizabethan in flavor, more Chapman than Homer in many ways. Historical interest and genuine power; not for primary study.

---

### Alexander Pope (1715–1720)
**Sample**: "Achilles' wrath, to Greece the direful spring / Of woes unnumber'd, heav'nly goddess, sing!"

Heroic couplets (iambic pentameter pairs). The dominant English translation for 150 years. Samuel Johnson said it was "a performance which no age or nation can pretend to equal." But it's Pope's poem as much as Homer's — elegant, witty, polished in the 18th-century manner, with none of Homer's rough power. Matthew Arnold famously criticized Pope's Homer for lacking Homer's "grand style."

---

### William Cowper (1791)
Blank verse. More faithful than Pope, less poetic. Important transitional translation.

---

### Samuel Butler (1898)
**Prose**. Very readable, direct, slightly Victorian. Butler controversially argued the Odyssey was written by a woman. His translations are in the public domain and widely available. Useful for quick reading or reference; lacks grandeur.

---

### A.T. Murray (1924) — Loeb Classical Library
**Prose**. Extremely literal, facing Greek text. Revised by William Wyatt (1999). The standard scholarly reference prose translation. Reads awkwardly as English but gives the closest word-for-word sense. Use when checking Greek meaning; don't use for pleasure.

---

### Richmond Lattimore (1951)
**Publisher**: University of Chicago Press
**Meter**: Long-lined free verse approximating hexameter rhythm
**Sample**: "Sing, goddess, the anger of Peleus' son Achilleus / and its devastation, which put pains thousandfold upon the Achaeans..."

**Strengths**: 
- Closest to Homer's Greek of any readable English version
- Preserves the epithets consistently ("swift-footed Achilleus," "Hera of the white arms")
- The long lines actually feel like epic verse — weighty, slow, cumulative
- Scholarly standard for decades; widely used in university courses
- Preserves Homeric formulas and repetitions rather than varying them
- Highly accurate; trusted by Classicists

**Weaknesses**:
- Often sounds stiff or awkward to modern ears
- The diction is slightly archaic ("Achilleus" for "Achilles")
- Less dramatically immediate than Fagles
- Can be slow going for inexperienced readers

**Best for**: Serious readers who want the closest thing to the Greek; university courses; scholars

---

### Robert Fitzgerald (1974)
**Publisher**: Anchor/Doubleday (originally); now widely available
**Meter**: Free verse, hexameter-length lines
**Sample**: "Anger be now your song, immortal one, / Akhilleus' anger, doomed and ruinous, / that caused the Achaians loss on bitter loss..."

**Strengths**:
- Beautifully poetic; reads as genuine English poetry
- More fluid and musical than Lattimore
- Fitzgerald was a fine poet in his own right (also translated the Odyssey and Aeneid)
- Very good on character voices

**Weaknesses**:
- Takes more liberties with the Greek
- Some epithets are varied or paraphrased
- Can feel slightly soft compared to Homer's brutal directness

**Best for**: Readers who want a poetic experience; literary reading; general audiences

---

### Robert Fagles (1990) — Penguin Classics
**Publisher**: Penguin Classics
**Introduction by**: Bernard Knox (brilliant, essential — one of the best introductions to Homer in English)
**Sample**: "Rage — Goddess, sing the rage of Peleus' son Achilles, / murderous, doomed, that cost the Achaeans countless losses, / hurling down to the House of Death so many sturdy souls..."

**Strengths**:
- Most widely sold and read modern translation
- Dramatic, visceral, immediate — the battle scenes have real energy
- Accessible to readers without classical background
- Bernard Knox's introduction and notes are exceptional
- Fagles renders Homeric violence with particular force
- Very natural in dialogue

**Weaknesses**:
- Takes significant liberties — sometimes paraphrases rather than translates
- Some epithets are varied for variety (loses the oral-formulaic effect)
- More "modern" in feel than Homer actually is
- Knox himself noted it is "sometimes too good to be true"

**Best for**: First-time readers; general audiences; dramatic power; audiobooks (Derek Jacobi's audio recording is outstanding)

---

### Stanley Lombardo (1997) — Hackett
**Publisher**: Hackett Publishing
**Meter**: Free verse; often very short, punchy lines
**Sample**: "Sing, Goddess, Achilles' rage, / Black and murderous, that cost the Greeks / Incalculable pain, pitched countless souls / Of heroes into Hades' dark..."

**Strengths**:
- Most energetic and colloquial; reads almost like spoken word
- Very good at conveying the speed and violence of battle
- Popular in courses that want something more "alive"
- Lombardo is also a good poet

**Weaknesses**:
- The colloquial diction can feel anachronistic
- Short lines can chop up the epic sweep
- Some readers find it too modern, too casual

**Best for**: Students; those who want energy over gravitas; performance reading

---

### Anthony Verity (2011) — Oxford World's Classics
**Publisher**: Oxford University Press
**Introduction by**: Barbara Graziosi
**Format**: Prose
**Strengths**: Extremely literal; excellent scholarly apparatus; Graziosi's introduction is excellent
**Best for**: Scholarly reference alongside Greek text; understanding precise meaning

---

### Caroline Alexander (2015) — Ecco/HarperCollins
**Publisher**: Ecco Press
**Meter**: Free verse
**Sample**: "Sing, goddess, of the ruinous wrath of Achilles, / Peleus's son, that brought ten thousand pains to the Achaeans..."

**Strengths**:
- Very literal — perhaps the closest readable verse translation to the Greek
- Preserves formulas consistently (like Lattimore)
- Written by a classical scholar who is also a gifted writer
- Excellent scholarly introduction (though Bernard Knox's for Fagles is arguably better)
- Very good on the epithets and the structure of Homeric verse

**Weaknesses**:
- Less poetic than Fagles or Fitzgerald
- Some find it dry

**Best for**: Serious readers who want accuracy with readability; good companion to Lattimore

## The Opening Line: A Comparison

The Iliad opens: **Μῆνιν ἄειδε θεὰ Πηληϊάδεω Ἀχιλῆος** ("Rage/wrath, sing, goddess, of Peleus's son Achilles")

The first word — **mēnin** (wrath/rage) — is critical. It is not a normal word for anger but specifically the extreme, dangerous wrath reserved for gods. Translators handle it very differently:

| Translator | Opening |
|-----------|---------|
| Chapman | "Achilles' baneful wrath resound, O Goddess..." |
| Pope | "Achilles' wrath, to Greece the direful spring..." |
| Butler | "Sing, O goddess, the anger of Achilles son of Peleus..." |
| Lattimore | "Sing, goddess, the anger of Peleus' son Achilleus..." |
| Fitzgerald | "Anger be now your song, immortal one..." |
| Fagles | "Rage — Goddess, sing the rage of Peleus' son Achilles..." |
| Lombardo | "Sing, Goddess, Achilles' rage..." |
| Alexander | "Sing, goddess, of the ruinous wrath of Achilles..." |

Fagles's "Rage" is the most dramatic and draws attention to the first word. Lattimore's "anger" is more literal but undersells the divine intensity of mēnin. Alexander's "ruinous wrath" tries to convey the destructive magnitude.

## Which Translation to Use?

**For first-time readers**: Fagles (Penguin) — most accessible, most exciting, Bernard Knox's introduction is essential.

**For serious study**: Lattimore (Chicago) — most faithful to the Greek, preserves formulas, standard scholarly reference.

**For poetic pleasure**: Fitzgerald — beautiful English poetry.

**For students/energy**: Lombardo (Hackett) — most vivid and immediate.

**For maximum accuracy**: Alexander (Ecco) or Verity (Oxford) — closest to the Greek.

**For historical curiosity**: Pope (1715) — magnificent as English poetry, very far from Homer.

**Most recommended pairing**: Fagles for reading, Lattimore for reference.
