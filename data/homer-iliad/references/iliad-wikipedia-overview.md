# The Iliad — Wikipedia Overview (Extracted Text)

## Basic Facts

- **Full title**: Ἰλιάς (Iliás), meaning "[a poem] about Ilion (Troy)"
- **Attributed to**: Homer
- **Composition**: circa 660/650 BCE (oral composition); circa 522 BCE (written)
- **Country**: Ancient Greece
- **Language**: Homeric Greek (literary mixture of Ionic Greek and other dialects)
- **Lines**: 15,693 in most widely accepted version
- **Meter**: Dactylic hexameter
- **Books**: 24
- **Subject**: Trojan War (final weeks of a 10-year siege)
- **First English publication**: 1598 (George Chapman)

The Iliad is one of the oldest extant works of literature still widely read by modern audiences, and is often regarded as the first substantial piece of European literature.

## Synopsis

### Exposition (Books 1–4)
The story begins with an invocation to the Muse. The events take place towards the end of the Trojan War. After nine years of fighting, Agamemnon takes Chryseis (daughter of Apollo's priest Chryses) as his prize, and Achilles claims Briseis. When Chryses's ransom for his daughter is refused, Apollo sends a plague on the Achaean army. Achilles convenes an assembly; Agamemnon agrees to return Chryseis but takes Briseis from Achilles as compensation. Achilles, furious, withdraws from fighting and asks his goddess mother Thetis to ask Zeus to favor the Trojans until his honor is restored. Zeus agrees.

Paris challenges Menelaus to single combat; when Paris is losing, Aphrodite rescues him. Athena breaks the truce by prompting the Trojan archer Pandarus to wound Menelaus. Fighting resumes.

### Duels of Greek and Trojan Heroes (Books 5–7)
Diomedes is elevated by Athena and wounds both Aphrodite and Ares. Hector visits Troy — urging his mother, meeting his wife Andromache and son Astyanax in a famous farewell scene. Hector and Ajax fight to a draw at nightfall.

### The Rout of the Greeks (Books 8–15)
Zeus prohibits divine interference; Trojans push Greeks back. Agamemnon sends an embassy (Odysseus, Ajax, Phoenix) to offer gifts and Briseis back to Achilles — Achilles refuses. Nestor urges Patroclus to persuade Achilles or to fight wearing his armor. Trojans breach the Achaean wall. Hera seduces Zeus and lulls him to sleep; Poseidon helps the Greeks. Zeus wakes, furious, and recalls Poseidon. Hector drives the battle to the Greek ships.

### The Death of Patroclus (Books 16–18)
Patroclus, unable to watch further, borrows Achilles's armor and leads the Myrmidons into battle. He kills many Trojans, including Zeus's son Sarpedon. Ignoring Achilles's instructions, he pursues the Trojans to Troy's walls, where Apollo and Euphorbus wound him, and Hector delivers the killing blow. Achilles, devastated by Patroclus's death, vows revenge. Thetis has Hephaestus forge new armor, including the famous shield.

### The Rage of Achilles (Books 19–24)
Achilles reconciles with Agamemnon, arms himself, and returns to battle. He slaughters Trojans mercilessly. After chasing Hector three times around the walls of Troy (his parents begging him to stop), Achilles kills Hector. He drags Hector's body behind his chariot.

In a climactic scene, old King Priam, guided by Hermes, crosses the Greek lines by night to ransom his son's body. He clasps Achilles by the knees and begs for Hector. The two men weep together — Priam for Hector, Achilles for his father and for Patroclus. Achilles is moved to compassion and returns the body. Hector is buried, and the poem ends.

## Major Themes

### Mēnis (Wrath)
The poem's opening word — μῆνιν (mēnin, "wrath") — establishes the central theme: the wrath of Achilles. It is specifically the destructive, god-level wrath reserved for divine beings. Agamemnon's dishonor triggers Achilles's rage, which ripples through the entire poem.

### Kleos (Glory/Fame)
Kleos (κλέος) is the concept of glory earned in heroic battle. Achilles faces the central choice of his life: stay at Troy and win immortal fame (kleos aphthiton — "imperishable glory") but die young, or go home and live long in obscurity. He chooses to avenge Patroclus, accepting early death.

### Timē (Honor)
Honor/respect is the social currency of heroic culture. Agamemnon's seizure of Briseis from Achilles is a public humiliation that strikes at his timē. The entire plot flows from this dishonor.

### Fate (Kēr)
Fate propels events. Neither gods nor men can ultimately alter it. Zeus himself is shown considering whether to save Sarpedon and Hector — his favorite mortals — from their fated deaths, but is persuaded by other gods not to upset divine order.

### Religion and the Gods
The Olympian gods take sides, interfere in battle, protect favorites, argue among themselves. Athena and Hera support the Greeks (partly because of the Judgment of Paris, in which Paris chose Aphrodite over them). Aphrodite, Apollo, and Ares support Troy. Zeus tries to maintain a balance. The gods' behavior is anthropomorphic — petty, passionate, concerned with honor.

### Hubris and Pride
Agamemnon's refusal to ransom Chryseis (hybris toward a priest of Apollo) sets the plague in motion. Paris's refusal to return Helen drives the entire war. Achilles's pride allows him to let his comrades die rather than fight for an insulting commander.

### Heroism
The poem deliberately complicates heroism. Achilles is the greatest warrior but also sulky, ruthless, almost inhuman in grief and rage. Hector, the Trojan defender of home and family, is in many ways the more sympathetic hero. Who is the "real" hero of the Iliad is left deliberately open.

### War
The Iliad shows both the glory and the horror of war. Battle scenes are detailed, visceral, and specific. But the poem also shows what is lost: Hector's farewell to Andromache and baby Astyanax, Priam's grief, Helen's ambivalence. It is neither a war poem nor an antiwar poem — it holds both in tension.

## Date and Textual History

- Scholarly consensus: late 8th century BC (some argue 7th century)
- Terminus ante quem: 630 BC (evidenced by reflection in art and literature)
- Herodotus placed Homer approximately 400 years before his own time (~850 BC)
- The historical backdrop is the Late Bronze Age collapse (early 12th century BC)
- Homer is thus ~400 years from his subject matter (the Greek Dark Ages)
- The Catalogue of Ships (Book 2) preserves pre-Dorian geography, suggesting ancient source material
- **Oldest extant manuscript**: Venetus A, copied in the 10th century AD (Biblioteca Marciana, Venice)
- **First printed edition (editio princeps)**: 1489, Florence, edited by Demetrius Chalcocondyles

## As Oral Tradition

- Classical scholars originally treated Homer as a writer, like Virgil or Milton
- By the 1920s, **Milman Parry** (1902–1935) launched the oral tradition movement
- Parry analyzed "stock epithets" and "reiteration" — formulaic phrases that fit the hexameter line
- Examples: "swift-footed Achilles," "resourceful Odysseus," "rosy-fingered Dawn"
- These formulas allow an oral poet to compose in real time, adapting traditional phrases to the meter
- Parry and assistant **Albert Lord** studied Yugoslav oral bards as a living parallel
- Lord published the landmark **The Singer of Tales** (1960)
- Later scholars: **Eric Havelock**, **Marshall McLuhan**, **Walter Ong**, **Gregory Nagy**
- Nagy argues the Iliad crystallized through centuries of competitive performance tradition, reaching something like its current form by the 6th century BC

## English Translations (Key versions)
1. **George Chapman** (1598/1611) — first major English translation; praised by Keats
2. **Alexander Pope** (1715–1720) — heroic couplets; most famous 18th-century version
3. **William Cowper** (1791) — blank verse; more literal
4. **Samuel Butler** (1898) — prose; public domain
5. **A.T. Murray** (1924) — Loeb Classical Library; very literal prose
6. **Richmond Lattimore** (1951) — hexameter-length lines; scholarly standard; close to Greek idiom
7. **Robert Fitzgerald** (1974) — more poetic; readable for general audiences
8. **Robert Fagles** (1990, Penguin) — most widely sold modern translation; dramatic, accessible
9. **Stanley Lombardo** (1997) — colloquial, energetic
10. **Anthony Verity** (2011, Oxford) — very literal prose
11. **Caroline Alexander** (2015) — scholarly, very literal; excellent introduction by Bernard Knox
12. **Emily Wilson** (forthcoming) — first complete Iliad by a woman (Wilson also translated the Odyssey, 2017)
