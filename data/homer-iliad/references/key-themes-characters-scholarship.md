# Key Themes, Characters, and Scholarly Interpretations of the Iliad

## The Poem's Central Structure

The Iliad is, at its heart, a poem about **the cost of wrath**. Its opening word — mēnin (wrath/rage) — announces its subject. The wrath of Achilles cascades through the poem:

1. Achilles withdraws → Greeks suffer and die
2. Patroclus enters battle and is killed → Achilles is destroyed by grief
3. Achilles returns in overwhelming fury → Trojans die, Hector dies
4. Achilles carries grief beyond reason → drags Hector's body
5. Priam's visit → wrath softens into shared grief

The poem does not celebrate wrath. It traces its cost to everyone — Greeks, Trojans, and Achilles himself.

## Major Characters

### Achilles (Ἀχιλλεύς)
The poem's central figure. Son of mortal Peleus and sea-goddess Thetis. The greatest warrior at Troy, but also the most morally complex.

**His essential situation**: Achilles knows he will die at Troy. His goddess mother has told him: stay, win eternal glory, die young; or go home, live long, be forgotten. He has chosen to stay — but when Agamemnon strips away his honor, the bargain collapses. He refuses to fight, and the Greeks suffer for it.

**The character arc**:
- **Books 1–9**: Wounded pride, withdrawal, refusal of embassy
- **Books 16–18**: Patroclus borrows his armor, dies, and Achilles is shattered
- **Books 19–22**: Achilles returns not for glory but for revenge — grief-mad, terrifying
- **Book 24**: Achilles meets Priam and is, briefly, human again

**Scholarly debates about Achilles**:
- Is he a hero or an antihero?
- Does he "develop" or simply change states?
- What does his choice of kleos over nostos mean philosophically?
- How does his grief for Patroclus function — friendship, love, something else?

**Achilles and Patroclus**: Ancient audiences generally understood their bond as the deepest friendship — Achilles calls Patroclus "the man I loved beyond all other companions, loved as my own life" (18.80–82, Fagles). Later ancient sources (including Plato's Symposium, Aeschylus's lost Myrmidons) treated the relationship as erotic. The Iliad itself is ambiguous and more interested in the bond's depth than its precise nature.

**Jonathan Shay, "Achilles in Vietnam" (1994)**: Shay, a psychiatrist, analyzed Achilles's behavior as a clinical case of PTSD — specifically what he calls "the berserk state." After Patroclus dies, Achilles enters a state of dissociation and murderous rage that Shay documents as identical to combat trauma in Vietnam veterans. This is one of the most powerful modern readings of the poem.

### Hector (Ἕκτωρ)
Troy's greatest warrior and its moral center. Son of King Priam and Queen Hecuba. Husband of Andromache, father of infant Astyanax.

Hector is in many ways the poem's most sympathetic character:
- He fights to protect his family and city, not for personal glory
- He is shown at home — in the famous Book 6 scene with Andromache and Astyanax
- He knows Troy will fall; he fights anyway
- He often shows better judgment than his allies (repeatedly warning Paris and the Trojans)

Yet Hector also makes fatal errors — refusing to withdraw into the city after the Greeks have the upper hand, driven by his own pride not to seem cowardly.

His death (Book 22) is one of literature's greatest scenes: chased around the walls of his own city by Achilles, his nerve failing him, tricked by Athena disguised as his brother Deiphobus, fighting to the end, and asking with his dying breath only that his body be returned to his family.

**Hector as the "real hero"**: Many readers argue Hector is the true hero of the Iliad — more morally admirable, more fully human, fighting for something beyond personal honor. The poem ends with his burial, not with Achilles's victory.

### Agamemnon (Ἀγαμέμνων)
King of Mycenae, commander of the Greek forces. A powerful but deeply flawed leader. His arrogance sets the plot in motion (seizing Briseis) and his incompetence repeatedly endangers the army. He is not a villain — he acknowledges his errors in Book 19 — but he represents the self-serving, honor-obsessed great man at his worst.

### Priam (Πρίαμος)
King of Troy. Old, helpless to stop the war, watching his city and sons die. His scene with Achilles in Book 24 — crossing enemy lines alone at night to ransom his son's body — is the emotional climax of the poem. He represents the pathos of war: what it costs fathers, families, whole peoples.

### Andromache (Ἀνδρομάχη)
Hector's wife. Appears in two key scenes: the farewell in Book 6 (one of the most moving passages in ancient literature) and her lament when she sees Hector's body being dragged behind Achilles's chariot in Book 22. She represents everything that is lost in war.

### Helen (Ἑλένη)
The cause of the war — but Homer's Helen is nothing like a simple femme fatale. She is ambivalent about herself, ashamed, aware of the suffering she has caused, yet unable to escape her situation. In Book 3, she watches from the walls as Paris and Menelaus duel. In Book 6, she speaks with bitter self-awareness to Hector. Simone Weil and others have noted that Homer treats Helen with remarkable sympathy and complexity.

### Patroclus (Πάτροκλος)
Achilles's closest companion. Gentle where Achilles is fierce. His death is the poem's pivot. It transforms Achilles from a sulking hero into something darker and more terrifying. The pathos of Patroclus's death — he died because he loved Achilles and wanted to save the Greeks Achilles had abandoned — is devastating.

### Odysseus (Ὀδυσσεύς)
Present throughout the Iliad as a practical, eloquent, sometimes morally flexible leader. His role is largely supportive and diplomatic here (the Embassy to Achilles in Book 9, the Night Raid in Book 10). He is not yet the Odysseus of the Odyssey — but his qualities are already evident.

### Nestor (Νέστωρ)
The oldest of the Greek leaders; a wise counselor who frequently recalls his earlier exploits. His role is partly comedic (he talks too much) but also structurally important: he advises Patroclus to go to battle wearing Achilles's armor, setting the tragedy in motion.

## Major Scholarly Interpretations

### James Redfield, "Nature and Culture in the Iliad" (1975)
Redfield argues the Iliad is fundamentally about the conflict between the world of war (the plain) and the world of culture (the city). Hector embodies this tension: he is a warrior, but his deepest ties are to the city, his family, and civilization. His tragedy is that he must destroy what he loves in order to defend it.

Redfield's analysis of Hector's funeral as the poem's closure is influential: the poem ends not with Achilles's victory but with the burial of civilization's greatest defender.

### Seth Schein, "The Mortal Hero: An Introduction to Homer's Iliad" (1984)
A clear, comprehensive scholarly introduction. Schein argues that the Iliad's central concern is mortality — what it means to be human in the face of death. The gods are immortal and therefore less fully alive; heroes are mortal and therefore tragic, but also capable of a kind of greatness unavailable to gods.

### Jasper Griffin, "Homer on Life and Death" (1980)
Griffin examines how Homer creates emotional depth through specific, individual scenes — the deaths of minor characters described with particular care, the similes that connect battle to peaceful domestic life. Homer's art, for Griffin, lies in his refusal to allow the epic sweep to flatten individual human lives.

### Bernard Knox, Introduction to Fagles's Iliad (1990)
One of the most accessible and brilliant short introductions to Homer. Knox covers:
- The historical background
- The oral tradition
- The poem's structure and themes
- The major characters
- The Greek heroic code
- Homer's impartiality (he is neither pro-Greek nor pro-Trojan)

Knox emphasizes that the Iliad is unique in ancient epic for its genuine compassion for both sides. He notes that the similes — which keep connecting the battlefield to peacetime life (farmers, mothers, children, seasons) — are Homer's way of insisting on the cost of war.

### Gregory Nagy, "The Best of the Achaeans" (1979)
Nagy approaches the Iliad through its key terms — especially kleos (glory) and nostos (homecoming). He argues that Achilles's choice of kleos aphthiton ("imperishable glory") over a long life is the poem's theological and cultural center: the exchange of mortal life for immortal fame through song. The poem itself is that fame — the Iliad is literally what Achilles chose death for.

## The Gods in the Iliad

The gods are not peripheral to the Iliad — they are woven through every major event. Interpreting their role is one of the central problems of Homeric scholarship.

**As divine machinery**: Gods cause things to happen that would otherwise require coincidence or probability. "A god made him do it" is a Homeric convention for explaining sudden decisions or reversals of fortune.

**As projections of internal states**: Some scholars (notably E.R. Dodds in "The Greeks and the Irrational") argue that divine interventions in Homer represent the externalization of psychological states. When Athena grabs Achilles by the hair and tells him not to kill Agamemnon, this may represent Achilles's better judgment checking his rage.

**As genuinely supernatural**: The text presents gods as real. The Greeks believed in them. Reading the divine as metaphor may be anachronistic.

**Julian Jaynes's bicameral mind theory**: Jaynes argued (controversially) that the divine voices in Homer represent an earlier stage of human consciousness in which "gods" were voices heard from the other brain hemisphere — before the modern unified self developed. The Iliad as evidence for pre-modern consciousness. Scholars generally find this interesting but unpersuasive.

**The gods as parody**: The divine scenes often have a comic quality — Ares and Aphrodite are wounded by Diomedes in Book 5, squealing and fleeing; Hephaestus limps comically; Hera tricks Zeus with a divine seduction. The contrast between divine comedy and human tragedy is deliberate and significant.

## The Shield of Achilles (Book 18)

One of the most analyzed passages in all of Homer. Hephaestus forges new armor for Achilles, and the shield is described in extraordinary detail:
- The cosmos (earth, sea, sky, sun, moon, stars)
- Two cities: one at peace (a wedding, a legal dispute), one at war (siege and battle)
- Agricultural scenes (plowing, harvesting, vintage)
- A meadow with cattle and a lion kill
- Dancing and a chorus

The shield has been interpreted as:
- A map of the human world — everything the war is threatening
- An image of cosmic order that Achilles, in his rage, is disrupting
- Homer's meditation on art itself — the shield is a work of art describing a world
- W.H. Auden wrote a famous poem "The Shield of Achilles" imagining what Thetis expects to see vs. what Hephaestus actually forges

## Homer and Tragedy

The Iliad powerfully influenced the Athenian tragedians. Notable connections:
- **Aeschylus**: Said his plays were "slices from Homer's banquets"; his lost Achilles trilogy (Myrmidons, Nereids, Phrygians) dealt with Achilles's grief and the ransoming of Hector
- **Sophocles**: Ajax deals with the aftermath of the Trojan War; Philoctetes with a related myth
- **Euripides**: The Trojan Women is explicitly about the defeat of Troy — from the Trojan side; Hecuba deals with Priam's queen

Homer's technique of showing multiple perspectives, including the losing side, directly prefigures Greek tragedy's interest in suffering and inevitability.
