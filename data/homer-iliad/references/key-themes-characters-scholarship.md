## The First Word: *Mēnis*

The *Iliad* opens: *"Sing, O goddess, the anger of Achilles son of Peleus..."* The Greek word translated "anger" is *μῆνις* (*mēnis*) — a word that in ancient Greek was almost exclusively reserved for the wrath of gods. Homer uses it on the poem's first line to describe a man.

This is not incidental. By opening with *mēnis*, Homer signals that Achilles is not operating on an ordinary human scale — his wrath has something divine and inhuman about it. The word appears only a handful of times in the *Iliad* applied to mortals; Achilles is the primary example.

## Key Theme: *Kleos* (Glory / Fame)

*Kleos* (κλέος) is the Greek concept of "renown" or "glory" — literally "what others hear about you," related to the verb *to hear*. In the heroic world of the *Iliad*, *kleos* is the primary value: the thing a warrior earns through great deeds, and the thing that outlives him after death.

According to scholar Gregory Nagy (Harvard), *kleos* also functions as the medium — the poem itself — through which glory is transmitted. The *Iliad* as a poem *is* the *kleos* of Achilles.

Achilles explicitly articulates the two-fate choice at the heart of his character in Book 9: he can either stay at Troy, die young, and earn eternal *kleos*; or he can sail home, live long, and have no *kleos*. He has already chosen the first. His withdrawal from battle in Book 1 is not a rejection of *kleos* — it is a protest that his *kleos* has been dishonored by Agamemnon's taking of Briseis.

## Key Theme: Honor (*Timē*) and the Heroic Code

Alongside *kleos*, *timē* (τιμή, "honor" or "worth") is central to the *Iliad*'s social world. Honor in this world is both internal (a warrior's sense of his own worth) and external (the recognition given by others, including tangible prizes and status). The quarrel in Book 1 is explicitly about *timē*: Agamemnon has dishonored Achilles by taking Briseis, a prize that represented the army's public recognition of Achilles' worth.

The heroic code of the *Iliad* demands that honor be defended even at extreme cost — a value that generates much of the poem's tragedy.

## Key Theme: Mortality and *Kleos Aphthiton*

A related concept is *kleos aphthiton* (κλέος ἄφθιτον) — "imperishable fame." In a world without belief in afterlife reward, the only immortality available to a hero is through the lasting memory of his deeds, preserved in song. This is the purpose of epic poetry in its original context: to keep the dead alive in the memory of the living.

Achilles' choice of early death and *kleos* over long life and obscurity is the poem's central existential statement about what makes life meaningful.

## Key Characters

### Achilles

The poem's protagonist. Son of the goddess Thetis and the mortal Peleus, king of Phthia; commander of the Myrmidons. Known as the greatest warrior in the Greek army. His defining characteristic is the capacity for extreme emotion — both wrath and grief.

The *Iliad* traces his arc from wounded pride (Book 1) through withdrawal and grief (Books 1–18) to consuming rage (Books 19–22) to something approaching grace (Book 24). Scholars debate whether this arc constitutes genuine growth or merely emotional exhaustion.

His death is not shown in the *Iliad* — the poem ends with Hector's funeral. Achilles is killed by Paris's arrow (guided by Apollo) after the poem's narrative ends, in events recounted in the *Aethiopis* (part of the Epic Cycle, now lost except for summaries).

**Source:** Wikipedia, "Achilles" — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achilles — verified.

### Hector

Eldest son of Priam, king of Troy; the Trojans' greatest warrior. Often described by scholars as the most fully human character in the poem: a husband, father, and soldier fighting in a war his brother started, defending a city he knows will fall.

The famous scene in Book 6 — where Hector visits his wife Andromache and infant son Astyanax at the city walls before returning to battle — is among the most emotionally complex passages in the poem. Hector explicitly tells Andromache that he knows Troy will fall and she will be enslaved; he goes back to fight anyway, for honor and for his people rather than hope of victory.

His death at Achilles' hands in Book 22 is the pivot of the poem's final movement. The dragging of his corpse behind Achilles' chariot is a deliberate violation of the heroic code — and its rectification (Priam's embassy in Book 24) is the poem's culmination.

**Source:** Standard textual analysis. Wikipedia, "Iliad" (as above).

---

### Patroclus

Achilles' closest companion. His death in Book 16 — killed by Hector after he ignores Achilles' command to stay away from Troy's walls — is the fulcrum of the entire poem. His death transforms Achilles' passive grief into active, destructive rage.

The nature of the Achilles-Patroclus relationship was debated in antiquity and remains discussed by scholars: intimate friends, cousins (in some traditions), or (in later readings, including Plato's *Symposium*) lovers. Homer does not specify; the poem treats their bond as the deepest human relationship Achilles has.

### Agamemnon

Commander-in-chief of the Greek forces. Powerful institutionally but repeatedly shown to be wrong in his decisions. His taking of Briseis from Achilles in Book 1 — an act the poem frames as a serious injustice motivated by wounded pride — sets the entire catastrophe in motion.

He is not a villain but a failure of a different kind: a man with authority and without the wisdom to use it well.

---

### Priam

The old king of Troy. His role is relatively small until Book 24, when he crosses enemy lines alone to beg Achilles for Hector's body. His speech to Achilles — invoking his own aged father Peleus — is one of the poem's supreme moments of rhetoric and pathos. Achilles weeps, and they eat together.

Scholars have described Priam's embassy as the poem's answer to its opening question about the costs of *mēnis*: by the end, both men are capable of recognizing their shared humanity across the wall of enmity.

## Simone Weil's Reading: Force as Subject

The French philosopher Simone Weil, in her 1939 essay "The Iliad, or the Poem of Force," argued that the poem's true subject is not any particular hero but **force itself** — "that which turns anyone subjected to it into a thing." Her reading emphasizes the poem's impartiality: it treats the suffering of Greeks and Trojans with equal seriousness, refusing to glorify either side's violence.

This reading has been enormously influential in modern Iliad scholarship and in how the poem is taught. See the dedicated reference file for fuller treatment.

## Gregory Nagy and the Oral-Formulaic Tradition

Gregory Nagy (Harvard) has extended Parry and Lord's oral-formulaic theory to argue that Homeric poetry was not composed once and then fixed, but underwent a long process of composition-in-performance across many generations. His influential *The Best of the Achaeans* (1979) examines *kleos* and related concepts as structuring principles of the entire Homeric tradition.

## The Gods in the *Iliad*

The Olympian gods in the *Iliad* take sides, argue, intervene, and behave according to favoritism and personal grievance rather than justice. This has been read in various ways:

1. As a theological statement: the gods are not reliable moral guides
2. As a narrative device: divine intervention allows Homer to show both inevitability (fate) and contingency (gods can change outcomes) operating simultaneously
3. As a way of representing forces beyond human control — the "divine" in the poem functions partly as a name for what mortals cannot predict or understand
