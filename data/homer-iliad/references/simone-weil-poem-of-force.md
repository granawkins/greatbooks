# Simone Weil: "The Iliad, or the Poem of Force"

## Context and Significance

Simone Weil (1909–1943) was a French philosopher, mystic, and political activist. She wrote this essay in 1940 as France fell to Nazi Germany — a catastrophe she experienced firsthand. Published in the Vichy-era journal Cahiers du Sud, the essay reads the Iliad as a meditation on the nature of force, and implicitly on what was happening in Europe at that moment.

The essay is widely considered one of the greatest pieces of literary criticism of the 20th century. It is remarkable for:
- Its moral seriousness and philosophical depth
- Its unusual sympathy for Trojans and Greeks alike — for all those crushed by force
- Its argument that Homer achieved something almost superhuman: genuine impartiality toward victors and victims
- Its implicit claim that the Iliad is relevant not just as literature but as a moral guide for understanding violence

---

## The Central Argument

**Force is the true subject of the Iliad.** Force — the power that turns a person into a thing — is what Homer is really writing about. Not heroism, not glory, not the will of the gods. Force.

Weil's opening line (in Mary McCarthy's translation):
> "The true hero, the true subject, the center of the Iliad is force. Force employed by man, force that enslaves man, force before which man's flesh shrinks away."

She defines force: "Force is as pitiless to the man who possesses it, or thinks he does, as it is to its victims; the second it crushes, the first it intoxicates."

This is the key insight: Force does not simply hurt those it crushes. It corrupts and dehumanizes those who wield it. The victor becomes a thing in a different way — arrogant, blind, drunk on power, unable to see the humanity of others or ultimately themselves.

---

## Force Turns People into Things

Weil identifies two ways force dehumanizes:

**1. It kills — turning a living person into a corpse (a thing).**
The poem is full of the moment of death, described vividly and specifically. Each soldier who dies is named. Their last moments are described with a kind of tenderness that prevents the reader from losing sight of the fact that these were people.

**2. It doesn't kill — it turns the living into things.**
This is subtler and perhaps more disturbing. The slave, the defeated warrior asking for mercy and not receiving it, the soldier who continues to fight only because he is trapped by circumstances — these are people who are alive but have been turned into objects by the force of others.

Weil focuses on the suppliant who kneels and asks for mercy — and is killed anyway. The Iliad returns to this scene again and again: a warrior catches another warrior, who pleads for his life, and is killed despite the plea. Achilles kills Hector's suppliant Lycaon in Book 21 in a passage Weil finds especially significant:
> Achilles says to Lycaon: "But, friend, you die also. Why do you cry so? Patroclus also is dead, who was better far than you are."

This reduction of the suppliant's plea — "you die too" — is force speaking with perfect clarity.

---

## The Victors Are Also Crushed

Weil's great insight is that the Iliad shows force crushing its wielders too — not just its victims. She traces the movement of force through the poem:

- The Greeks push the Trojans to the walls of Troy.
- Then the Trojans push the Greeks back to the ships.
- Then Achilles returns and reverses everything.
- No permanent mastery is achieved.

The force moves like a pendulum. Those who hold it today will be broken by it tomorrow. This applies even to Achilles, the greatest warrior — he is destroyed not by a superior warrior but by his own grief, his own rage, his loss of Patroclus.

Weil: "From the time when a man sets foot in the battle as a soldier, he is destined to lose all the rights of a human being."

---

## Homer's Impartiality

What makes the Iliad unique, for Weil, is that Homer shows this truth without taking sides. Both Greeks and Trojans are crushed by force. Both victimize and are victimized. The poem does not celebrate Greek victory — it mourns Hector just as it mourns Patroclus.

This is almost unheard of in history, Weil argues. Conquerors write history celebrating themselves. Nations celebrate their own heroes. But Homer — whoever Homer was — had somehow achieved the perspective of someone who could see both sides with equal compassion.

She writes: "Nothing the peoples of Europe have produced is worth the first known poem that appeared among them. Perhaps they will yet rediscover the epic genius, when they learn that there is no refuge from fate, learn not to admire force, not to hate the enemy, nor to scorn the unfortunate. It is doubtful if this will happen soon."

(Written in 1940. The resonance with Hitler's Europe is unmistakable.)

## Key Passages Weil Analyzes

### The Shield of Achilles (Book 18)
Weil sees Achilles's shield as the civilized world that force destroys. The shield depicts cities at peace — weddings, law courts, harvests, dancing. This world is what war destroys. Homer places this image at the precise moment when Achilles is about to re-enter the war and complete his destructive arc.

### Hector's Farewell (Book 6)
The scene where Hector says goodbye to Andromache and baby Astyanax — the child frightened by Hector's plumed helmet — is one of the moments Weil dwells on. Here we see what is at stake. Hector is defending this. He knows he will lose. He asks the gods only that his son be praised as greater than himself.

### Priam and Achilles (Book 24)
For Weil, this is the poem's greatest moment. Priam kneels before the man who killed his son and begs for Hector's body. Achilles weeps — he thinks of his own father. Two enemies, on opposite sides of an absolute divide, are briefly human to each other. Force momentarily lifts.

Weil calls this the culmination of Homer's vision: that even in the worst conditions, there are moments of grace.

---

## Weil on Suffering and Attention

This essay connects to Weil's broader philosophical and theological project. She believed that true compassion requires "attention" — the willingness to really look at a suffering person, to see them as fully real. The Iliad, for her, is the supreme example of this kind of attention in literature.

Her essay prefigures her later work on affliction (malheur) — the way extreme suffering can actually degrade a person's soul, make them less capable of being seen as fully human. The Iliad is remarkable precisely because it refuses to let that happen: even the most defeated characters are fully seen.

## Reception and Influence

- First published: Cahiers du Sud, 1940–1941
- First English translation: Mary McCarthy, Politics magazine, 1945
- NYRB Classics edition (2005) with translation by Christopher Benfey is the most accessible modern edition
- The essay influenced: Susan Sontag, Rachel Bespaloff (who wrote a parallel essay "On the Iliad"), James Wood, and many others
- It is now standard reading in Classics and Philosophy courses worldwide
- Rachel Bespaloff's companion essay "On the Iliad" (1943) offers a different perspective — more focused on the Greek side, Achilles's tragedy

---

## Comparison: Rachel Bespaloff, "On the Iliad" (1943)

Both Weil and Bespaloff (a Romanian-French philosopher) wrote about the Iliad in the same years, published together in NYRB Classics. Their approaches differ:

- **Weil**: Force is the subject; the poem is about universal dehumanization; Homer achieves radical impartiality; the Iliad is a near-Christian text of suffering and compassion.
- **Bespaloff**: More focus on character — especially Hector, Helen, and Achilles; more willing to see tragic grandeur in heroism even within force; Achilles as a tragic figure who knows his doom and chooses it anyway.

Herman Broch's preface to the NYRB volume is also excellent — he contextualizes both essays within the Western tradition of reading Homer.

"To define force — it is that x that turns anybody who is subjected to it into a thing. Exercised to the limit, it turns man into a thing in the most literal sense: it makes a corpse out of him."

"The Iliad is a poem of force. Force, employed by man, force that enslaves man, force before which human flesh shrinks back."

"Only he who has measured the dominion of force, and knows how not to respect it, is capable of love and justice."

"The whole Iliad lies under the shadow of the greatest calamity the human race can experience — the destruction of a city."
