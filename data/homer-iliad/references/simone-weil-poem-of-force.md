## About the Essay

**"L'Iliade ou le poème de la force"** ("The Iliad, or the Poem of Force") is a 24-page essay written by **Simone Weil** in 1939. It is her most celebrated piece of literary criticism.

**Publication history:**
- First published in 1940 in *Les Cahiers du Sud* (a French literary magazine operating in the free zone during WWII — described as the only significant literary magazine available there at the time)
- First English translation by **Mary McCarthy**, published in the New York journal *Politics* in 1945
- Republished many times since; as of 2007, it was the only one of Weil's writings on ancient Greek literature commonly used in university Classics courses

---

## Weil's Central Argument

Weil opens the essay with its core thesis:

> *"The true hero, the true subject, the centre of the Iliad, is force. Force employed by man, force that enslaves man, force before which man's flesh shrinks away. In this work, at all times, the human spirit is shown as modified by its relations with force, as swept away, blinded, by the very force it imagined it could handle, as deformed by the weight of the force it submits to."*

She defines **force** as that which turns anyone subjected to it into a thing — at worst, into a corpse. This is the essay's most cited formulation.

Key arguments:
- Force is dangerous not only to its victims but to whoever wields it: it intoxicates, numbs reason and pity, and ultimately turns even the powerful into things — unthinking automatons driven by rage
- No one in the *Iliad* truly controls force; even Achilles and Agamemnon briefly suffer when events turn against them
- The poem covers all forms of human love (parental, fraternal, erotic, between comrades), but these appear only briefly, as counterpoints to the surrounding violence
- The *Iliad*'s equity — its refusal to treat Trojans and Greeks differently in their suffering — is unequalled in Western literature before or after, though something of it was transmitted through Aeschylus, Sophocles, and the Gospels

## Context: The Essay's Wartime Writing

Weil wrote the essay in 1939, on the eve of and during the German invasion of France. Readers and scholars have consistently noted that the essay is not only about Homer — it is a meditation on force written in real time as France fell, and its analysis of how force dehumanizes those who yield to it and those who wield it clearly responds to the events of 1939–40.

## About Simone Weil

Simone Weil (3 February 1909 – 24 August 1943) was a French philosopher, mystic, and political activist. Born in Paris to an Alsatian Jewish family, she was a teacher and labor activist in the 1930s, took part in the Spanish Civil War on the anarchist side, and later worked for the Free French in exile. She died at age 34 of heart failure, possibly exacerbated by restricting her own food intake in solidarity with occupied France.

Most of her writing attracted little attention until after her death; in the 1950s–60s it became widely influential in Continental Europe and the English-speaking world.

## Reception

- *The New York Review of Books* has described the essay as one of Weil's most celebrated works
- *The Atlantic Monthly* has called it, alongside Rachel Bespaloff's *On the Iliad*, "the twentieth century's most beloved, tortured, and profound responses to the world's greatest and most disturbing poem"
- Elizabeth Hardwick described it as "one of the most moving and original literary essays ever written"
- Weil reportedly took over half an hour per line when translating the *Iliad* passages she cited in the essay, capturing a quality of sympathy and compassion that impressed those around her

---

## A Note on Rachel Bespaloff

*The Atlantic* quote above pairs Weil with **Rachel Bespaloff** (1895–1949), a French philosopher who wrote *On the Iliad* at roughly the same time — also in response to the war. Bespaloff's essay and Weil's have often been published together and discussed in tandem as companion pieces.
