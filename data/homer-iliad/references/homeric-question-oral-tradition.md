## The Homeric Question

The "Homeric Question" refers to the cluster of debates surrounding the authorship, composition, and historicity of the *Iliad* and *Odyssey*. Its key sub-questions:

- Who was Homer? Was "Homer" a single author or a tradition?
- Were the *Iliad* and *Odyssey* composed by one person or many?
- Were they composed orally, then written down — or composed in writing from the start?
- How old are the oldest recoverable elements of the poems?

The question has roots in ancient scholarship — critics in the Hellenistic period already debated these issues — but it flourished as a formal academic controversy in the 18th and 19th centuries, and was transformed in the 20th by Milman Parry's work on oral-formulaic composition.

## Friedrich August Wolf and the "Analysts"

The modern form of the Homeric Question is often traced to Friedrich August Wolf's *Prolegomena ad Homerum* (1795), which argued the poems were assembled from shorter, originally independent songs by an editor (*diaskeuast*), not composed by a single author. Wolf's analysis rested on the observation that writing was likely unavailable or uncommon at the time the poems were first composed, making a single-author epic of this length implausible.

This position gave rise to the "Analyst" school, which sought to identify seams, contradictions, and interpolations in the text as evidence of multiple authorship or editorial compilation.

The opposing "Unitarian" school argued that the structural and thematic unity of each poem pointed to single, deliberate authorship — that the apparent inconsistencies were features of the oral style, not evidence of patchwork assembly.

## Milman Parry and Oral-Formulaic Theory

The most consequential 20th-century contribution to the Homeric Question was Milman Parry's work in the 1920s–30s. Parry, an American classicist, demonstrated that the *Iliad* and *Odyssey* are saturated with "formulas" — repeated phrases and epithets (e.g., "rosy-fingered Dawn," "swift-footed Achilles") that recur in metrically convenient positions. He argued these formulas were not decorative but *functional*: they allowed a singer-poet (an *aoidos*) to compose verse in real time during performance, drawing on a memorized stock of traditional phrases that fit the dactylic hexameter.

Parry's conclusion: the Homeric poems are products of **oral-formulaic composition** — a tradition of live, improvised epic performance, not literary composition in the modern sense.

Parry has been described as "the Darwin of Homeric studies" for the transformative effect of this argument on the field.

## Albert Lord and *The Singer of Tales*

After Parry's death in 1935, his student Albert Bates Lord continued his fieldwork. Lord traveled to Yugoslavia to record living oral epic singers (guslars) performing South Slavic heroic songs, gathering comparative evidence for how oral-formulaic composition works in practice.

Lord's landmark book, ***The Singer of Tales*** (Harvard University Press, 1960), synthesized this fieldwork with analysis of Homer, *Beowulf*, and other epics. Its central argument: the oral poet does not memorize fixed texts but learns a system of formulas and themes, composing anew at each performance. This means there is no single "original" Homeric text — each performance was a distinct composition within a tradition.

Lord also argued for a sharp divide between oral and literate composition — that the move to writing fundamentally changes the nature of poetry — a position that has since been debated and refined.

*The Singer of Tales* was reissued in a 40th anniversary edition with an audio CD. It remains a foundational text in oral tradition studies and classics.

## Geoffrey Kirk's Response

Geoffrey Kirk's *The Songs of Homer* (Cambridge University Press, 1962) challenged Lord's direct application of Yugoslav oral poetry parallels to Homer. Kirk argued that Homeric poetry differs from South Slavic tradition in its "metrical strictness" and "formular system" — that Homer shows more systematic control and creativity than living oral poets Lord studied. Kirk proposed that Homer may represent a late stage of a tradition, a "monumental composer" who used oral techniques but achieved something qualitatively different.

## Current Scholarly Consensus

Most classicists now accept that:

1. The *Iliad* and *Odyssey* are substantially products of oral-formulaic tradition — the poems' formulaic density, repeated epithets, and type-scenes reflect oral composition techniques.
2. Whether a single "Homer" gave the poems their final shape, or whether they are the product of gradual crystallization over generations, remains genuinely debated.
3. The poems likely reached something close to their current form in the 8th or 7th century BCE, though elements may preserve genuinely ancient (Mycenaean-era) material.
4. They were performed by professional reciters (rhapsodes) at Greek festivals, including the Great Panathenaia at Athens.
