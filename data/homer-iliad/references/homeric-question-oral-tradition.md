# The Homeric Question and Oral Tradition

## What Is the Homeric Question?

The "Homeric Question" refers to a cluster of interrelated debates about Homer and the Homeric poems that have occupied scholars since antiquity:

1. **Did Homer exist?** Was there a single author named Homer, or is "Homer" a name for a tradition?
2. **Did one person compose both the Iliad and the Odyssey?** Or are they products of different poets or traditions?
3. **How were the poems composed?** Were they dictated? Written? Improvised in performance?
4. **When were they composed?** Late 8th century BC? 7th century? Earlier?
5. **How did they reach their current form?** Was there an "original" version, and when was it fixed?

These questions have no settled answers. Scholarship has shifted dramatically since the early 20th century, and the field remains active.

---

## Ancient Views

The ancient Greeks themselves debated these questions. The main ancient positions:

- **Unitarians** held that one genius, Homer, composed both poems as unified wholes.
- **Separatists** (ancient "chorizontes") argued that the Iliad and Odyssey were by different authors.
- The city of **Chios** claimed Homer as a native son; so did Smyrna. Seven cities fought over his birthplace in a famous epigram.
- **Alexandrian scholars** at the Library of Alexandria (especially Aristarchus, 2nd c. BC) worked to establish a standard text and remove "interpolations."

## The Analyst vs. Unitarian Debate (18th–19th c.)

In 1795, **Friedrich August Wolf** published his *Prolegomena ad Homerum*, arguing that:
- Writing was not known in Greece in Homer's time
- The poems were too long to be composed or memorized without writing
- Therefore the poems must be the product of multiple bards, compiled and edited later

This launched the **Analyst** school: scholars who tried to identify different layers of composition, contradictions, and editorial seams in the text. They argued for multiple authors ("lays" theory) or a single compiler working with older material.

The **Unitarians** responded that the poems show deep structural coherence — the Iliad in particular has a unified arc, consistent characterization, and sophisticated narrative design that points to a single artistic intelligence.

This debate dominated Homeric scholarship for 150 years. Today most scholars are "neo-Unitarians" who accept that the poems reflect the shaping hand of one (or at most two) great poets — but poets working within and from a long oral tradition.

## Milman Parry and the Oral-Formulaic Revolution

The most important development in 20th-century Homeric scholarship was the work of **Milman Parry** (1902–1935), an American classicist whose career was cut short by his death at age 33.

### Parry's Key Discovery
Parry analyzed the Homeric epithets — the repeated descriptive phrases attached to characters and objects:
- "swift-footed Achilles" (πόδας ὠκὺς Ἀχιλλεύς)
- "resourceful Odysseus" (πολύτλας δῖος Ὀδυσσεύς)
- "rosy-fingered Dawn" (ῥοδοδάκτυλος Ἠώς)
- "the wine-dark sea" (οἶνοπα πόντον)
- "grey-eyed Athena"

He noticed that these epithets are not chosen for their meaning in context — "rosy-fingered Dawn" appears even when Dawn is being described as bringing a grim day. They are chosen for their **metrical value**: each fits a specific position in the dactylic hexameter line.

More remarkably, there are very few overlapping epithets for the same character in the same metrical slot. The system is **economical**: it provides exactly the formula needed for each metrical position, without redundancy.

Parry's conclusion: the Homeric epithets are tools for **oral composition in performance**. A bard composing aloud, in real time, needs a repertoire of ready-made phrases that fit the meter. The formula system is not a sign of lazy repetition — it is a sophisticated instrument for improvised composition.

### The Yugoslav Field Research
In the early 1930s, Parry traveled to Yugoslavia with his assistant **Albert Lord** to study living oral epic poets (guslars) who composed and performed long narrative poems in performance.

They found that these poets:
- Could compose poems of epic length in real time during performance
- Did not memorize texts word-for-word but reproduced the story using a large repertoire of formulas and themes
- Described themselves as singing "the same song" each time, but no two performances were identical
- Could not write and did not need to

This demonstrated that oral composition of long, complex poems was possible and had a living tradition. It transformed understanding of Homer.

### Albert Lord's "The Singer of Tales" (1960)
Lord published the synthesis of this research after Parry's death. Key arguments:
- Oral poets compose by assembling formulas (word-level units) and themes (narrative units)
- The "theme" is a repeated narrative pattern: the arming scene, the battle aristeia, the embassy, the supplication
- Written texts and oral tradition are fundamentally different cognitive and creative modes
- The Homeric poems bear the marks of oral composition throughout

Lord also identified parallels between the Iliad and the Epic of Gilgamesh — similar narrative patterns of a hero, his beloved companion, the companion's death, and the hero's subsequent grief and transformation.

## Gregory Nagy and the "Evolutionary" Model

**Gregory Nagy** (born 1942), a Harvard classicist, has developed the most influential contemporary model of Homeric composition and transmission. His key works:
- *The Best of the Achaeans* (1979)
- *Pindar's Homer* (1990)
- *Homeric Questions* (1996)
- *Homer's Text and Language* (2004)
- *Homer: The Poetry of the Past* (1992)

### Nagy's Model
Nagy proposes that the Iliad and Odyssey were not composed at a single moment but evolved through a long period of oral performance. He identifies roughly five stages:
1. A "pre-Panhellenic" period of regional oral traditions (before ~750 BC)
2. A "Panhellenic" crystallization as the poems become associated with pan-Greek festivals (~750–550 BC)
3. An "Atheno-centric" period as Athens sponsors a standardized performance tradition at the Panathenaic Festival (~550–300 BC)
4. A "Hellenistic" period of text-based scholarship, especially at Alexandria (~300–150 BC)
5. The medieval manuscript tradition

In this model, there is no single "original" text of the Iliad. What we have is the result of centuries of gradual crystallization — like a river delta forming over time — with the Athenian tradition playing a key role in fixing the current text.

### Implications
Nagy's model means:
- The Homeric Question may be unanswerable in the traditional form ("who wrote it?")
- The text we have is authoritative as a tradition even if not as a single author's creation
- The epithets and formulas are not artistic choices but inherited tools — though a great poet can use inherited tools with genius
- The Iliad's "unity" is real but not the unity of a single author's design — it is the unity of a tradition that has been perfected over generations

## The Dictation Theory

A minority view, associated most prominently with **Barry Powell** and others: Homer was a real poet of genius who lived in the late 8th century BC. Writing had just arrived in Greece (the Greek alphabet was adapted from Phoenician around 800 BC). Homer — or someone in his circle — dictated the poems to a scribe.

This would explain:
- The poems' exceptional length (impossible to memorize word-for-word, but possible to compose and dictate once)
- The poems' artistic unity (a single poet's genius)
- The formulas (tools of oral composition, now written down for the first time)
- The textual stability (established early by writing)

This remains a minority view but is not without adherents.

---

## The Homeric Question Today

Modern scholarly consensus:
1. The Iliad and Odyssey were composed primarily by one or at most two great oral poets, working in the late 8th or early 7th century BC
2. These poets worked within a long tradition of oral epic poetry going back at least to the Bronze Age (there are clearly Mycenaean elements in the poems — Bronze Age weapons, Bronze Age place names)
3. The poems were transmitted orally for some period before being written down
4. The Alexandrian edition (Aristarchus, ~150 BC) established something close to our current text
5. The question of "Homer" as an individual remains genuinely unresolved — but most scholars are comfortable treating the Iliad as the product of a single (near-)genius, even if that genius worked within and from tradition

## Practical Implication for Reading

The oral-formulaic background matters for readers because:

**Repeated epithets**: Don't agonize over why Achilles is "swift-footed" even when he's sitting down. The epithet fills a metrical slot. But also — these epithets accumulate meaning through repetition. Every time you hear "swift-footed Achilles," you are reminded of who he is.

**Repeated scenes**: The Iliad has many "type scenes" — the arming scene, the departure scene, the supplication scene, the feast scene. These are inherited narrative units. Their repetition is not laziness but the poem's way of creating pattern and rhythm.

**The Catalogue of Ships (Book 2)**: Often skipped by modern readers. But it preserves ancient geographical knowledge, and its epic scope (29 contingents, 1,186 ships) establishes the magnitude of the enterprise.

**Similes**: The extended similes (comparing warriors to lions, fires, waves, farmers) are among the poem's greatest beauties. They are also oral composition tools — they give the bard time to think while providing the audience with vivid imagery. But Homer's similes are not mere filler; they create ironic contrasts and thematic depth.
