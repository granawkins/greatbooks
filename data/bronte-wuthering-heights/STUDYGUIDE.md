# Wuthering Heights: Chapter Summaries
## Emily Brontë (1847)

---

### Chapter 1
- In 1801, Lockwood, a new tenant at Thrushcross Grange, visits his landlord Heathcliff at the remote moorland farmhouse Wuthering Heights.
- He encounters a sullen Heathcliff, an unnamed young woman (later revealed as Cathy Linton, Heathcliff's daughter-in-law), and a rough young man called Hareton Earnshaw.
- Joseph, the elderly, cantankerous servant, is present and speaks in thick Yorkshire dialect, adding to the general inhospitality of the place.
- Lockwood misreads the household relationships — he mistakes Cathy for Heathcliff's wife and Hareton for his son.
- The house itself is described vividly: the name "Hareton Earnshaw" carved over the door, the strange architecture, and the wild dogs that attack him.

### Chapter 2
- Lockwood returns to Wuthering Heights the next day and finds Heathcliff absent. He meets Cathy and Hareton again in a frosty atmosphere.
- A snowstorm traps him at the Heights. He attempts to borrow a lantern to find his way home but is refused.
- Joseph accuses Lockwood of attempting to steal and sets the dogs on him, causing Lockwood a nosebleed.
- Heathcliff finally arrives and offers Hareton as a reluctant guide; Lockwood must stay the night.
- Joseph has a notable speaking role, scolding Lockwood in dialect: a key moment for audio — his speech is among the most phonetically challenging in the novel.

### Chapter 3
- Heathcliff lodges Lockwood in a closed-off room where he finds books with the name "Catherine Earnshaw" scratched on the covers.
- Catherine's diary entries — scrawled in margins — reveal glimpses of her childhood: Hindley's tyranny and her companionship with Heathcliff, and Joseph's pious bullying of the children.
- Lockwood falls into a nightmare in which the ghost of Catherine Linton scratches at the window, begging to be let in after "twenty years" of wandering.
- When Lockwood cries out, Heathcliff arrives in open distress — he tears open the window and calls desperately into the night for Catherine's ghost to return.
- This chapter establishes the novel's Gothic atmosphere and the obsessive grief at its center.

### Chapter 4
- Lockwood returns to Thrushcross Grange feverish and ill, and asks his housekeeper Ellen "Nelly" Dean to tell him the history of the family at Wuthering Heights.
- Nelly begins: thirty years prior, old Mr. Earnshaw returns from Liverpool with a dark, ragged orphan boy he names Heathcliff.
- Mrs. Earnshaw resents the child; Hindley beats and torments him; but Catherine quickly befriends him.
- Heathcliff is unusually silent and patient under abuse — a trait Nelly notes with ambivalence.
- Hindley, jealous of his father's favoritism toward Heathcliff, is sent away to school — the beginning of a lifelong enmity.

### Chapter 5
- Mr. Earnshaw ages and grows increasingly irritable, relying heavily on Heathcliff and neglecting Catherine and Hindley.
- Joseph feeds Earnshaw's religious severity and disapproves of the other children's wildness — he has a significant role here, reinforcing the household's harsh Calvinist atmosphere.
- Earnshaw dies quietly in his chair by the fire, with Catherine and Heathcliff nearby; Nelly describes it as a peaceful death.
- Hindley, now at college, has secretly married a woman named Frances and brings her home as the new master of Wuthering Heights.
- From this point Heathcliff's status degrades sharply — he is to be treated as a farmhand, not a member of the family.

### Chapter 6
- Hindley forces Heathcliff to work as a laborer and denies him education; Heathcliff and Catherine nevertheless roam the moors freely.
- One evening they spy through the window of Thrushcross Grange and observe Edgar and Isabella Linton quarreling childishly over a dog — contrasting starkly with Heathcliff and Catherine's wilder freedom.
- Catherine is bitten by the Lintons' dog and is taken in to recover; Heathcliff is driven away by Mr. Linton as an unfit companion.
- Heathcliff bitterly recounts this event to Nelly — his exclusion from the cultivated world the Lintons represent.
- Catherine stays at the Grange five weeks and returns transformed: polished, well-dressed, and newly conscious of class.

### Chapter 7
- Catherine returns to Wuthering Heights, now refined and ladylike, and greets Heathcliff with new self-consciousness about his dirty, degraded state.
- Edgar and Isabella Linton visit for Christmas; Heathcliff, by contrast, is shabby and resentful.
- Hindley humiliates Heathcliff in front of the Lintons; Edgar makes a cutting remark about Heathcliff's long hair.
- Heathcliff retaliates by throwing hot applesauce at Edgar; Hindley locks him in the attic as punishment.
- Nelly privately comforts Heathcliff and he declares his intention of revenge — one of the novel's most important foreshadowing moments.

### Chapter 8
- Frances gives birth to Hareton and dies shortly after of consumption; Hindley collapses into drunkenness and brutality.
- Catherine is increasingly divided: wild and natural with Heathcliff, refined and calculated with Edgar.
- Nelly observes this split self-consciously — Catherine uses Edgar's love as a source of vanity while her real soul belongs elsewhere.
- Edgar Linton visits constantly and proposes marriage; Catherine is flattered but also genuinely fond of him.
- Heathcliff, degraded and uneducated by Hindley's design, grows ever more sullen and dangerous.

### Chapter 9
- Edgar formally proposes; Catherine accepts and then, in a long confession to Nelly, tries to explain that she loves Heathcliff differently and more deeply than Edgar.
- She speaks the famous lines: her love for Edgar is "like the foliage in the woods," but her love for Heathcliff is "like the eternal rocks beneath" — "He's more myself than I am."
- Heathcliff overhears the early part of the conversation — hearing only that it would degrade her to marry him — and leaves the Heights that night, disappearing entirely.
- Catherine runs out into a storm searching for him, soaks herself, and falls ill; she recuperates at the Grange, and both Linton parents catch her fever and die.
- This chapter is the novel's central pivot: Heathcliff's disappearance sets the entire revenge plot in motion.

### Chapter 10
- Three years pass. Nelly now serves Edgar and Catherine, married at Thrushcross Grange. Catherine is mostly content, though Nelly senses an underlying unease.
- Heathcliff returns unexpectedly — taller, dignified, mysteriously wealthy and composed. How he acquired his fortune is never explained.
- Catherine is ecstatic; Edgar, suspicious and unsettled, agrees to receive him for Catherine's sake.
- Heathcliff reveals he has taken lodgings with Hindley at Wuthering Heights — he is exploiting Hindley's gambling addiction, acquiring a mortgage on the estate.
- Isabella Linton is immediately, dangerously infatuated with Heathcliff; Heathcliff observes this with cold calculation.

### Chapter 11
- Nelly catches Heathcliff kissing Isabella in the garden; she warns him and reports to Catherine, who confronts Heathcliff mockingly.
- Catherine tells Edgar about Heathcliff's interest in Isabella, partly to defuse it and partly out of a possessive need to control.
- Edgar confronts Heathcliff directly and forbids him entry to the Grange; a brief physical altercation follows and Edgar is shamed by his own weakness.
- Catherine, furious at the conflict between the two men, locks herself in her room and refuses food — the beginning of her self-destructive episode.
- Joseph features in scenes at Wuthering Heights, reinforcing the degraded atmosphere Heathcliff and Hindley inhabit.

### Chapter 12
- Catherine, shut in her room for days, begins to rave and hallucinate — she tears her pillow apart and confuses past and present, imagining herself back in her childhood bed at Wuthering Heights.
- She talks wildly about Heathcliff and the moors, half-believing herself a child again; Nelly is frightened and finally summons the doctor.
- Edgar returns from church to find Isabella's rooms empty — she has eloped with Heathcliff.
- Edgar, choosing between his wife's illness and his sister's disgrace, resolves to focus on Catherine but declares he will never acknowledge Heathcliff as a kinsman.
- Catherine is found to be pregnant.

### Chapter 13
- Isabella writes a wretched letter to Nelly from Wuthering Heights — she is deeply disillusioned, already miserable after barely weeks of marriage.
- She describes the filth and hostility of the Heights: Hindley drunk and dangerous, Heathcliff contemptuous and cold.
- Joseph is prominently featured in Isabella's account — his brutal piety and refusal of any comfort to her mark him as a significant presence in every Wuthering Heights scene.
- Isabella asks Nelly: "Is Mr. Heathcliff a man? If so, is he mad? And if not, is he a devil?"
- This letter gives the reader's first external view of Heathcliff's domestic cruelty.

### Chapter 14
- Nelly visits Wuthering Heights to see Isabella and is struck by her degradation — bruised, disheveled, and without hope.
- Heathcliff demands Nelly carry a letter to Catherine; Nelly refuses; Heathcliff insists with a mixture of threat and desperate pleading.
- He confesses his obsession: he cannot give up Catherine; he will see her once more whatever the consequence.
- Nelly, moved by his anguish and afraid of his violence, reluctantly agrees to deliver the letter.
- Joseph is present in the household and occupies his customary role as rigid, judgmental overseer of the domestic space.

### Chapter 15
- Nelly delivers Heathcliff's letter to Catherine, now frail and near death, barely days before she is due to give birth.
- Heathcliff secretly enters Thrushcross Grange and the two have a final, agonized meeting — both accusatory and deeply loving.
- Heathcliff reproaches Catherine for choosing to marry Edgar and for deliberately destroying herself and him; she says she's dying and it is equally both their faults.
- Catherine clings to Heathcliff in physical desperation; he holds her, knowing Edgar is approaching.
- Edgar returns; Heathcliff is forced to leave Catherine in Edgar's arms — the last time they see each other alive.

### Chapter 16
- Catherine dies in the early hours, two hours after giving birth to a daughter — the future young Cathy.
- Edgar is devastated and barely leaves the room; Nelly tends to the household.
- Heathcliff, learning of Catherine's death from Nelly, unleashes raw grief outside: he strikes his head against a tree, demands Catherine haunt him rather than rest in peace.
- He cannot bear the idea of her at peace without him: "I cannot live without my life! I cannot live without my soul!"
- Catherine is buried simply in a corner of the churchyard, on the edge of the moors — not in the Linton chapel, a compromise between her two worlds.

### Chapter 17
- Isabella arrives at Thrushcross Grange, having fled Wuthering Heights on the night of Catherine's death.
- She describes the violent night: Hindley had barricaded Heathcliff out and attempted to murder him with a pistol-knife; in the struggle Hindley was badly beaten.
- Heathcliff, knowing Catherine had just died, came in and beat Hindley viciously — Isabella watched in terror.
- Isabella escapes south where she later gives birth to Heathcliff's son, the sickly Linton Heathcliff; she will never return to Yorkshire.
- Hindley Earnshaw dies six months after Catherine, having drunk himself to death; Heathcliff, his mortgage-holder, assumes possession of Wuthering Heights. Hareton is left an orphan in Heathcliff's hands.

### Chapter 18
- A twelve-year gap. Young Cathy Linton has grown up sheltered at Thrushcross Grange, beloved by her father Edgar — bright, willful, and curious.
- At thirteen, she secretly crosses the moors and discovers Wuthering Heights, where she meets Hareton Earnshaw (now a rough, uneducated teenager) and explores freely.
- Nelly finds her there and is alarmed; Cathy is enchanted by the moors and puzzled by Hareton's uncouth behavior — she assumes he's a servant.
- Edgar keeps Cathy ignorant of both Heathcliff and her cousin Linton Heathcliff (now living south with his mother).
- This chapter begins the second generation's story, mirroring the first in striking ways.

### Chapter 19
- A letter arrives: Isabella has died, and Linton Heathcliff — aged twelve, delicate and pampered — is to come to Thrushcross Grange.
- Edgar receives him warmly; Cathy is excited to meet a cousin.
- The morning after Linton's arrival, Joseph appears at the Grange door to claim the boy on Heathcliff's orders.
- Joseph's arrival is abrupt and significant — he is the instrument of Heathcliff's claim on his son, and his manner is characteristically gruff and dismissive.
- Nelly must take the frightened, tearful Linton to Wuthering Heights; Edgar feels powerless — Heathcliff is Linton's legal father.

### Chapter 20
- Nelly accompanies Linton to Wuthering Heights to deliver him to Heathcliff.
- Heathcliff greets his son with open contempt, calling him a "whey-faced" weakling; he sees Linton purely as an instrument to inherit Thrushcross Grange.
- Linton is terrified of his father and clings to Nelly as she prepares to leave.
- Heathcliff explains his scheme to Nelly with cold clarity: if Linton dies before Edgar, the Grange reverts to the Linton bloodline — so Cathy must marry Linton first.
- Joseph is present in the household as Nelly departs, an ever-present background figure at the Heights.

### Chapter 21
- Three years pass. Cathy (now sixteen) and Nelly encounter Heathcliff on the moors; he invites them to Wuthering Heights.
- Cathy is introduced (or reintroduced) to Linton and Hareton. Linton flatters her; Hareton is rough and embarrassed.
- Heathcliff begins deliberately engineering a romance between Cathy and Linton — telling Cathy that Linton is in love with her.
- Cathy is charmed by Linton and by the idea of a secret correspondence; Nelly tries to discourage both.
- The parallels with the first generation are explicit: a cultivated girl, a dark outsider's scheming, and a young man caught between worlds.

### Chapter 22
- Nelly discovers Cathy has been secretly corresponding with Linton by letter; she confiscates and burns the letters.
- Cathy is tearful and resentful — she has formed a real emotional attachment to Linton through his letters.
- Out walking, they encounter Heathcliff on the road; he tells Cathy that Linton is pining for her and that Edgar's refusal to allow contact is making Linton ill.
- Cathy is moved and guilty; she believes she is responsible for Linton's suffering.
- Edgar, now visibly declining in health, forbids further contact with Wuthering Heights but cannot enforce it.

### Chapter 23
- Cathy and Nelly visit Wuthering Heights and find Linton petulant, demanding, and manipulative — clearly coached by Heathcliff.
- Linton tells Cathy she is causing his illness by staying away; he places all blame on her and wheedles her affection.
- Cathy is confused and upset; Nelly recognizes that Linton is Heathcliff's tool, not a genuine suitor.
- After they leave, Nelly falls ill with a cold that keeps her in bed for three weeks — leaving Cathy unsupervised.
- Joseph features among the household servants as the visit unfolds at the Heights.

### Chapter 24
- During Nelly's illness, Cathy has been secretly visiting Linton at Wuthering Heights every day.
- She tells Nelly what happened: Linton was sometimes sweet, sometimes cruel and selfish. On the worst visit he deliberately injured her by pulling her hair.
- Cathy, still fond of him, is troubled but continues to go — partly from pity, partly from loneliness with her father so ill.
- Edgar is now seriously, visibly dying; Cathy is increasingly frightened of losing him.
- Nelly reports the visits to Edgar, who summons Cathy and forbids them — but offers to allow Linton to visit the Grange instead.

### Chapter 25
- Edgar, now clearly near death, confides his anxieties to Nelly — he fears for Cathy's future once he is gone.
- He knows Linton would be a poor protector but hopes a relationship might be better than nothing; he writes to Linton and arranges meetings on the moors rather than at the Heights.
- Cathy and Linton meet on the moors as agreed; Linton is visibly, alarmingly ill — frightened of something Nelly cannot quite name.
- He is clearly being pressured by Heathcliff to maintain Cathy's affection; he is desperate and pathetic.
- This chapter underscores Edgar's powerlessness against Heathcliff's long game: both generations are being moved like pieces.

### Chapter 26
- Cathy and Nelly meet Linton on the moors for a second time; Linton is so weak he can barely sit up — he seems to be dying.
- He is secretive and frightened, watching the road; Cathy is alarmed and tender.
- Heathcliff appears — he tells them Linton is well enough but begs them to come inside for just a few minutes.
- He is persuasive and seemingly concerned; Cathy agrees, and Nelly follows.
- The trap is about to close.

### Chapter 27
- Inside Wuthering Heights, Heathcliff locks the doors and imprisons both Cathy and Nelly.
- He reveals the scheme: Edgar is dying; he needs Cathy to marry Linton immediately so that on Edgar's death, Cathy inherits the Grange — and as Linton's wife it passes to Heathcliff when Linton dies.
- Cathy tries to escape; Heathcliff strikes her hard across the face. She is humiliated and terrified.
- The forced marriage takes place off the page; Cathy is kept prisoner for five days.
- Joseph is prominently present throughout the captivity at the Heights, his complicity in Heathcliff's scheme evident. This is one of his most significant chapters for audio.

### Chapter 28
- Nelly is released after five days; she races back to Thrushcross Grange to find Edgar on his deathbed.
- Cathy eventually escapes Wuthering Heights and arrives just in time to say goodbye to her dying father.
- Edgar Linton dies peacefully, believing Linton is a sufficient protector for Cathy — not knowing the full extent of Heathcliff's scheme.
- Immediately after Edgar's funeral, Heathcliff arrives to claim both Cathy and possession of Thrushcross Grange, now Linton's by marriage.
- Linton himself dies within weeks, leaving Cathy a widow with no legal claim on either property.

### Chapter 29
- Heathcliff comes to the Grange after Edgar's funeral to take Cathy back to Wuthering Heights as his servant — she has no legal recourse.
- He tells Nelly, with strange intimacy, that he had bribed the sexton and opened Catherine's grave when Edgar was buried next to her — he needed to see her face.
- He describes removing the side of her coffin so that when he himself is buried, their dust will mingle.
- This necromantic confession reveals the absolute irrationality of his obsession: his revenge has been pursued, but his grief has never lifted.
- Nelly is horrified but also moved; the reader sees that Heathcliff's cruelty and his grief are inseparable.

### Chapter 30
- Nelly's narrative shifts: she now tells what she heard from Zillah, the housekeeper at Wuthering Heights.
- Linton Heathcliff dies shortly after the forced marriage. Cathy is left isolated at the Heights, ignored by Heathcliff and treated hostilely by the household.
- Zillah describes Cathy nursing the dying Linton without any help from Heathcliff or the servants.
- When Cathy is ill herself, Hareton alone brings her food occasionally, though awkwardly.
- Joseph is present as usual, indifferent to Cathy's suffering and self-righteously disapproving.

### Chapter 31
- Lockwood visits Wuthering Heights to deliver his notice of departure and pay his rent.
- He finds Cathy and Hareton together: Hareton has been trying to teach himself to read in order to impress her — she mocks his efforts, wounding his pride.
- They quarrel; Cathy's cruelty to Hareton mirrors exactly the way the young Heathcliff was once mocked by the Lintons.
- Lockwood observes the sadness of the household — Heathcliff cold, Cathy imprisoned by circumstances, Hareton stranded between classes.
- Joseph is visible in the background, occupying his customary place in the Heights' daily life.

### Chapter 32
- Eight months later, Lockwood returns to Yorkshire and finds Nelly Dean is now housekeeper at Wuthering Heights.
- Nelly recounts what has transpired: Cathy and Hareton have reconciled — she apologized for mocking his reading and has been teaching him; they have fallen in love.
- Heathcliff is deeply disturbed by their happiness: Hareton's face recalls Hindley's, but his spirit recalls Catherine — and Cathy's eyes are Catherine's eyes.
- Joseph bitterly resents Cathy's influence over Hareton and her "corrupting" him with her company and books; he complains to Heathcliff about it.
- Joseph's disapproval of Cathy and Hareton's budding romance is a notable moment — his rigid sense of propriety and loyalty to the old order makes him unable to accept any softening of the household.

### Chapter 33
- Cathy and Hareton are together, planting flowers in the garden; Joseph furiously complains to Heathcliff that Hareton has pulled up his currant bushes at Cathy's request.
- Heathcliff confronts them, raises his hand to strike Cathy — then stops. He is undone by the sight of her eyes, which are Catherine Earnshaw's eyes.
- He confesses to Nelly that his appetite for revenge has collapsed. He cannot harm Hareton — who reminds him of Catherine, not Hindley; and he cannot look at Cathy.
- "I have lost the faculty of enjoying their destruction," he tells Nelly; the revenge for which he destroyed his life no longer brings any satisfaction.
- Joseph's complaint about the currant bushes opens the chapter — his very literalness (currant bushes vs. love) provides dark-comic contrast to the existential crisis unfolding.

### Chapter 34
- Heathcliff stops eating, becomes increasingly abstracted and visionary — he tells Nelly he is close to peace, that he sees Catherine everywhere.
- He asks Nelly to give minimal directions for his burial — no minister, just placed in the coffin and carried to the churchyard beside Catherine.
- He is found dead in Catherine's old room, the window open, rain soaking in. His face wears an expression of exultation.
- Joseph is the one who discovers Heathcliff dead and marks the transition: the Heights now belongs to Hareton by rights.
- Nelly concludes her narrative: Hareton and Cathy are to marry and move to Thrushcross Grange; locals whisper of seeing Heathcliff's ghost walking the moors with Catherine. Lockwood visits the three graves — Catherine, Edgar, and Heathcliff — side by side, and cannot imagine their inhabitants as unquiet.
