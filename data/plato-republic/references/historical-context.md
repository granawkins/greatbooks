## Plato's Life

**Plato** (Greek: Πλάτων, *Plátōn*; c. 428–423 BC – 348/347 BC) was born in Athens into an aristocratic family. His mother Perictione was a descendant of Solon, the statesman credited with founding Athenian democracy. His brothers **Glaucon** and **Adeimantus** — both historical persons — appear as the main interlocutors in the *Republic*.

The name "Plato" may be a nickname referring to his broad (Greek: *platus*) physique; his birth name may have been Aristocles. This is debated.

Plato's family background made him a natural candidate for Athenian political life. His early years saw Athens embroiled in the Peloponnesian War (431–404 BC). The war ended in catastrophic defeat for Athens.

## The Peloponnesian War and Its Aftermath

Athens in the late 5th century BC was the dominant maritime and cultural power in the Greek world. Under Pericles (d. 429 BC), it built the Parthenon, hosted Socrates and the sophists, and ran the first sustained democratic government in Western history.

The **Peloponnesian War** (431–404 BC) was a catastrophic 27-year conflict between Athens and its allies on one side, and the Spartan-led Peloponnesian League on the other. Key events:

- **429 BC:** The plague of Athens kills perhaps a quarter of the city's population, including Pericles.
- **415–413 BC:** The **Sicilian Expedition** — Athens' disastrously overconfident invasion of Sicily — ends in total destruction of the Athenian fleet and army. A turning point.
- **404 BC:** Athens surrenders to Sparta. The democratic government is overthrown and replaced by the **Thirty Tyrants** — an oligarchic junta backed by Sparta.

The **Thirty Tyrants** (404–403 BC) ruled by terror. Plato's uncle Critias and his cousin Charmides were members. Plato himself was invited to join; he declined. The Thirty murdered at least 1,500 Athenian citizens in eight months.

In 403 BC, the democrats overthrew the Thirty. Democracy was restored.

## The Historical Socrates vs. the Socratic Character

**Socrates** (469–399 BC) was a real person — the son of a stonemason (or sculptor) and a midwife, who spent his life in Athens engaging citizens in philosophical conversation. He wrote nothing. What we know of him comes from:

1. **Plato's dialogues** — most extensive but most interpretively loaded; the Socrates here is Plato's literary and philosophical creation.
2. **Xenophon's** *Memorabilia* and *Symposium* — more anecdotal, less philosophically ambitious; Socrates appears as a practical moralist rather than a metaphysician.
3. **Aristophanes'** *The Clouds* (423 BC) — a comedy that ridicules Socrates as a sophist and natural philosopher, depicting him running a "think-shop" where students learn to cheat creditors. This may be slander or caricature.

**The "Socratic Problem":** Scholars debate which view of Socrates is most accurate. The Socrates of the early Platonic dialogues (e.g., *Apology*, *Euthyphro*, *Meno*) seems plausibly historical — he claims ignorance, asks questions, produces *aporia*, does not propound positive doctrines. The Socrates of the middle dialogues (including the *Republic*) has elaborate theories — the Forms, the tripartite soul, philosopher-kings. Most scholars believe the latter is Plato's own philosophy expressed through Socrates' mouth.

**Leo Strauss** and others note that Socrates in the *Republic* is probably more "Platonic" than "Socratic" in the strict sense.

## The Trial and Death of Socrates (399 BC)

In 399 BC — four years after democracy's restoration — Socrates was put on trial by the Athenian democratic assembly. The charges:

- **Impiety** (*asebeia*): failing to acknowledge the gods of the city and introducing new divine things.
- **Corrupting the youth** of Athens.

The accusers were Meletus (a poet), Anytus (a tanner and politician), and Lycon (an orator). The trial had 501 jurors. Socrates was found guilty by a majority, and then sentenced to death by a larger majority (he had proposed as an alternative penalty that Athens house and feed him at public expense — widely seen as mocking the court).

Socrates died by drinking hemlock. He was 70.

**Why did this happen?**

The trial's political context is disputed but crucial:
- Several of Socrates' associates had been members of the Thirty Tyrants (Critias, Charmides) or sympathizers with oligarchy.
- After the democratic restoration, a general amnesty prevented direct prosecution for crimes under the Thirty — the impiety charge may have been a legally available workaround.
- Socrates had long irritated Athenian democratic sensibilities by questioning conventional morality and associating with aristocratic young men.
- His famous *elenchus* (cross-examination) made powerful people look foolish.

**The effect on Plato:** The trial and execution of Socrates was the defining event of Plato's intellectual life. According to his *Seventh Letter* (authenticity disputed), it permanently destroyed his hope for Athenian political life and turned him toward philosophy. The entire *Republic* can be read as asking: how could a city that executed Socrates be a just city? What would a city look like that actually *valued* philosophical wisdom?

## When Was the Republic Written?

The *Republic* is generally dated to **approximately 375–380 BC** — the middle period of Plato's writing. This would be roughly 20–25 years after Socrates' death.

By 375 BC:
- Plato was approximately 50 years old.
- He had already founded the **Academy** (c. 387 BC), the first institution of higher learning in the Western world.
- He had returned from his first trip to **Syracuse** (c. 388 BC), where he had attempted (unsuccessfully) to advise the tyrant Dionysius I.
- Aristotle had not yet arrived at the Academy (he arrived c. 367 BC).

The Academy was located in a grove of olive trees northwest of Athens, dedicated to the hero Academus. Plato taught there until his death in 348/347 BC. The Academy continued for nearly 900 years after Plato's death, until the Emperor Justinian closed it in 529 CE.

**The dialogue's dramatic setting:** The *Republic* is set in the house of Cephalus in the Piraeus (Athens' port), during an unnamed festival. The dramatic date is uncertain — scholars estimate it to be during the Peloponnesian War (430s–400 BC), given references in the text. The dialogue is presented as Socrates recounting the conversation the following day — a literary device giving the whole text a retrospective quality.

## Why Plato Was Writing a Critique of Democracy

This is the essential context for the *Republic*: **Plato wrote it in the aftermath of Athens' democratic execution of the wisest man he knew.**

Plato came from an aristocratic family that had connections to the anti-democratic Thirty Tyrants — and he saw how badly that ended too. His experience left him with a consistent political position: **neither oligarchy nor democracy produces just government**. Both regimes are driven by private interest and appetite rather than by wisdom.

The *Republic* is not a neutral political philosophy text. It is an argument mounted against the specific failure of Athenian democracy, which:
- Allowed demagogues (Cleon, Alcibiades) to manipulate the assembly.
- Made catastrophically wrong decisions (the Sicilian Expedition).
- Executed the most philosophically serious person in Athens on charges of impiety.

Plato's implicit argument: A city governed by popular opinion rather than philosophical knowledge will always make these mistakes. The cure is a city governed by those who actually know what justice and the good are — philosopher-kings.

This also explains the *Republic*'s hostility to **poetry and rhetoric**: both are instruments of democratic manipulation, appealing to emotion and appetite rather than reason. Homer makes the crowd cry; sophists make the crowd vote for the wrong policy. Plato's ideal city has no room for either.

## Athens After the Peloponnesian War

The Athens in which Plato grew up and wrote the *Republic* was:

- Defeated and humiliated militarily (404 BC surrender).
- Politically traumatized (the Thirty Tyrants; the restored democracy's instability).
- Economically recovering but no longer the naval hegemon.
- Culturally still vital — this is the period of the great tragedians' legacy, Aristophanes, emerging natural philosophy, and the sophistic movement.
- Philosophically in ferment — the sophists (Protagoras, Gorgias, Thrasymachus) were arguing that justice and morality were conventional, not natural. Socrates had spent his life fighting this view.

The *Republic* takes place in this intellectually charged, politically anxious Athens. Thrasymachus — who argues that justice is the interest of the stronger — is a historical sophist, representing a real philosophical tendency. Plato is arguing against a living intellectual movement, not a strawman.
