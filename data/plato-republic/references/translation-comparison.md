## Overview

The *Republic* has been translated into English roughly a dozen times, each reflecting a different philosophy of translation. The key choices every translator must make:

1. **Technical vocabulary**: Greek philosophical terms like *dikaiosúnē*, *thumos*, *eidos*, and *logos* have no perfect English equivalents. Translators choose between familiar English words (which simplify), transliterated Greek (which obscures for non-specialists), and invented English phrases.
2. **Socrates' voice**: Is it elevated and formal? Conversational? Does it feel like a Victorian lecture or a street-corner debate?
3. **Dialogue format**: How natural do the exchanges sound? Plato's dialogues can feel like real conversation or like set speeches interrupted by "Yes, Socrates."
4. **Philosophical precision vs. readability**: Jowett is readable but sometimes paraphrases. Bloom is precise but sometimes opaque.
5. **Literal translation vs. interpretation**: Does the translator render words or ideas?

## Benjamin Jowett (1871, revised 1892) — **THE PLATFORM TRANSLATION**

**⚠️ Public domain. This is the translation used on greatbooks.fm.**

**Publication:** Clarendon Press, Oxford. Three editions: 1871, 1875, 1892. The 1892 revision is the standard text. Available free at Project Gutenberg (https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1497) and MIT Internet Classics Archive.

**Who was Jowett?** Benjamin Jowett (1817–1893) was Regius Professor of Greek at Oxford and Master of Balliol College. He spent roughly a decade on his complete translation of Plato's dialogues with introductory essays — a Victorian landmark of classical scholarship. He was a liberal Anglican theologian and educational reformer; his translation aimed to make Plato accessible to the educated Victorian reading public, not just classical scholars.

**Style:** Elevated Victorian prose. Dignified, literary, occasionally paraphrastic. Jowett's Socrates sounds like a distinguished Oxford don — urbane, slightly ironic, always in command. The dialogue flows naturally for literary readers; philosophers sometimes find it imprecise.

**Characteristic word choices:**
- *dikaiosúnē* → **"justice"** (standard; Jowett made this the dominant English rendering)
- *thymoeides* → **"spirited"** or "passionate" (not always consistent)
- *logistikon* → **"rational"** or "reason"
- *gennaion pseudos* → **"noble lie"** (Jowett's coinage; became the standard English phrase — it's so entrenched that modern readers sometimes forget it's a translation choice)
- *eidos/idea* → **"form"** or "idea" (Jowett tends toward "idea" in some passages; modern translators prefer "form")
- *polis* → **"city"** or "state" (Jowett often uses "state" with anachronistic political connotations)
- *eudaimonia* → **"happiness"** (standard; potentially misleading since Greek *eudaimonia* is better translated as "flourishing" or "doing well")
- *thumos* → **"passion"** or "spirit" (inconsistent; this is one area where Jowett is vague)

**Distinctive features:**
- Jowett sometimes **expands** where the Greek is terse, adding clarity but departing from Plato's compression.
- He is comfortable rendering the **Socratic irony** — his Socrates has the right lightness of touch.
- He uses "he" and "man" generically (Victorian conventions); in passages where Plato discusses female guardians, this can obscure the point.
- His **introductory essays** (not included on this platform) were enormously influential — for a generation, Jowett's introductions shaped how English speakers read Plato.

**Weaknesses:** Occasionally paraphrastic; sometimes chooses grace over precision; Victorian English can feel arch; anachronistic rendering of political concepts.

## Paul Shorey (1930–1935)

**Publisher:** Loeb Classical Library (Harvard University Press). Two volumes. **Paywalled (in print). Out of copyright as of 2026 but not widely available free online.**

**Background:** Paul Shorey (1857–1934) was a leading American classicist at the University of Chicago. His translation is in the Loeb bilingual series (Greek facing English).

**Style:** More literal than Jowett; aimed at scholars who want to follow the Greek. Not as readable as Jowett for general audiences.

**Key differences from Jowett:**
- More precise with technical terms; less willing to paraphrase.
- *thumos* rendered more consistently.
- Retains more Greek sentence structure.
- Considered reliable for scholarly use.

**Availability:** Loeb editions widely available in university libraries. Online via Loeb Digital Library (subscription, paywalled). Physical books available for purchase.

## Allan Bloom (1968)

**Publisher:** Basic Books. **Copyrighted. Paywalled.**

**Background:** Allan Bloom (1930–1992) was a political philosopher at Cornell and Chicago, famous/notorious later for *The Closing of the American Mind* (1987). His translation was accompanied by a substantial interpretive essay and notes, reflecting a Straussian reading of the text.

**Style:** Highly literal — sometimes uncomfortably so. Bloom aims to preserve every ambiguity in the Greek, rejecting the tendency to smooth over difficulties. If Plato's Greek is awkward or obscure, Bloom's English is too.

**Key differences from Jowett:**
- *dikaiosúnē* → Bloom sometimes uses **"justice"** but emphasizes it is always approximate.
- *thumos* → Bloom tends toward **"spiritedness"** — preserving the athletic/aggressive connotation Jowett softens.
- *gennaion pseudos* → **"noble lie"** (Bloom follows Jowett's coinage).
- *polis* → Bloom is careful to distinguish *polis* from "state" or "nation"; he often uses "city" to emphasize the small scale.
- *eudaimonia* → **"happiness"**, but with notes on the inadequacy of the rendering.
- Bloom preserves Plato's ironic and puzzling passages rather than smoothing them. Where Jowett makes Plato fluent, Bloom makes him strange.

**Interpretive stance:** Bloom believes Socrates' proposals in the *Republic* are not meant seriously — they are reductiones ad absurdum designed to expose the limits of political philosophy. His translation is designed to support this esoteric reading.

**Availability:** In print from Basic Books. Widely available in libraries. Not available free online.

## G. M. A. Grube, revised by C. D. C. Reeve (1992)

**Publisher:** Hackett Publishing Company. **Copyrighted. Paywalled.**

**Background:** G. M. A. Grube (1899–1982) was a classicist at the University of Toronto; C. D. C. Reeve updated Grube's translation in 1992 for modern scholarly use. This is currently the most widely assigned translation in North American philosophy courses.

**Style:** Clear, modern, philosophically precise. Splits the difference between Jowett's fluency and Bloom's literalism. Reads well and is technically reliable.

**Key differences from Jowett:**
- *thumos* → **"spirit"** (consistent, not Jowett's inconsistent "passion"/"spirit").
- *logistikon* → **"the rational"** or **"reason"** (similar to Jowett).
- *epithymetikon* → **"the appetitive"** (clearer than Jowett).
- *polis* → **"city"** (not "state" — more precise than Jowett's anachronism).
- *eidos* → **"form"** (not "idea" — modern consensus follows this).
- *gennaion pseudos* → **"noble lie"** (follows the Jowett coinage).
- *eudaimonia* → **"happiness"** (standard; often noted with caveat in footnotes).

**Key advantage over Jowett:** More consistent and philosophically up-to-date terminology. Less Victorian paraphrase. Better for tracking technical arguments precisely.

**Availability:** Standard Hackett paperback, ~$15. Widely available in libraries and for purchase. Not available free online.

## Comparison Table: Key Terms Across Translations

| Greek | Jowett (1892) | Shorey (1930) | Bloom (1968) | Grube/Reeve (1992) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| *dikaiosúnē* | justice | justice | justice | justice |
| *thumos* / *thymoeides* | passion / spirited (inconsistent) | spirit | spiritedness | spirit |
| *logistikon* | rational / reason | rational | the calculating | the rational |
| *epithymetikon* | appetitive / desires | appetitive | the desiring | the appetitive |
| *polis* | state / city | city | city | city |
| *eidos* / *idea* | idea / form (inconsistent) | form | form | form |
| *eudaimonia* | happiness | happiness | happiness | happiness |
| *gennaion pseudos* | noble lie | noble lie | noble lie | noble lie |
| *philosophos basileus* | philosopher-king | philosopher-king | philosopher-king | philosopher-king |
| *kallipolis* | ideal state / beautiful city | kallipolis (in notes) | city | city in speech |
| *paideía* | education | education | education | education |
| *dóxa* | opinion | opinion | opinion | belief |
| *epistēmē* | knowledge | knowledge | knowledge | knowledge |

**Notes on the table:**
- "Noble lie" (*gennaion pseudos*) is unanimous across translations — it is Jowett's coinage that became standard.
- "Philosopher-king" is also universal, though Bloom notes the Greek is actually "philosopher-kings and kingly philosophers."
- *thumos* is where Jowett is weakest — his inconsistency matters because *thumos* is structurally important to the tripartite soul.
- *polis* as "state" (Jowett) vs. "city" (modern) is a subtle but significant difference. "State" imports modern connotations of sovereignty and bureaucracy foreign to the Greek city-state.

## What Makes Jowett Distinctive for Audio

For greatbooks.fm purposes, Jowett has two key advantages:

1. **Public domain** — no licensing issues.
2. **Readability out loud** — Jowett's Victorian prose has a natural rhythm and dignity that works well for audio narration. The dialogue sections have appropriate register variation: Socrates sounds authoritative, the interlocutors sound appropriately surprised or challenged.

One disadvantage for audio: Jowett's longer, more elaborate sentences can become unwieldy to follow without a text to refer back to. Bloom's more clipped style might actually be clearer for listeners in places.

## On "Noble Lie" as a Translation

Worth flagging specifically because it is so widely discussed: Jowett's "noble lie" (*gennaion pseudos*) has been debated for over a century. The Greek *gennaion* means "noble" in the sense of "well-born" or "fitting to a noble person" — it does not mean "heroic" or "laudable." *Pseudos* means falsehood or fiction.

Some translators and scholars prefer **"noble fiction"** or **"magnificent myth"** to avoid the pejorative "lie," arguing Plato is describing a civic myth, not a deliberate deception. Others keep "lie" to preserve the moral edge.

The debate matters: if it is a "lie," Plato is explicitly recommending that rulers deceive their citizens; if it is a "myth" or "fiction," the rulers might know it is not literally true but treat it as symbolically true. Modern readers should be aware that Jowett's "noble lie" is not a neutral translation.
