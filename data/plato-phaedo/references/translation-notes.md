## The Translation Landscape

The *Phaedo* has been translated into English many times. For greatbooks.fm, we use **Benjamin Jowett's 1892 translation**, the same used for the *Republic* and the *Apology* in our corpus. Understanding what Jowett does — and what he doesn't do — is essential for readers who may come in from other editions, or who want to read scholarship that quotes different translations.

---

## Benjamin Jowett (1892)

**Edition:** *The Dialogues of Plato*, 3rd edition, Oxford. First published 1871; revised editions 1875 and 1892.

**Character:** Jowett's *Phaedo* is the most widely read English translation of the 19th and early 20th century, and still one of the most commonly encountered. His prose is polished, elevated, and somewhat formal — consciously literary. He aims for something that reads as a great work of literature, not merely a philosophical document.

**Strengths:**
- Reads beautifully aloud. The death scene, in particular, is among the finest passages of Victorian prose.
- The dialogue's philosophical arguments are rendered in clear, flowing English that does not require a philosophy background to follow.
- Jowett has a gift for the emotional register — Phaedo's narration, Socrates' calm, the friends' grief all come through.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Philosophical precision sometimes sacrificed for readability.** Jowett sometimes paraphrases rather than translates closely, smoothing out technical passages that are deliberately difficult in the Greek.
- **Victorian idiom** can jar contemporary ears: "methinks," "perchance," archaic inversions. Readers coming to the *Phaedo* for the first time may find occasional passages opaque without it being the *argument's* fault.
- **Key terms are not always rendered consistently** — different occurrences of the same Greek word can produce different English words, making it hard to track the philosophical structure without the original.
- **The Theory of Forms** — arguably the most important doctrine in the dialogue — is rendered with a variety of terms ("absolute beauty," "the beautiful itself," "beauty in the abstract") that can obscure that Plato is always talking about the same thing: Forms.

**When it shines:** The dramatic scenes. Xanthippe's distress. Socrates' last bath. The hemlock. The last words. These are among the best things Jowett wrote, and they carry the emotional weight of the dialogue fully.

## David Gallop (1975, Oxford University Press)

**Edition:** *Phaedo* (Clarendon Plato Series), Oxford University Press, 1975. Reprinted with corrections.

**Character:** A scholarly translation with facing-page Greek text and extensive commentary. The standard academic reference for close philosophical reading of the *Phaedo* in English.

**Strengths:**
- Extremely precise. Gallop tracks individual Greek terms carefully and notes where translation choices are contested.
- Excellent footnotes and commentary — arguably the best scholarly apparatus available in English for this text.
- Modern, readable prose without Jowett's Victorian idiom.
- The commentary is philosophically sophisticated: Gallop engages with the arguments analytically and notes the objections in the scholarly literature.

**Weaknesses:**
- Reads like a philosophy text, not a literary one. The death scene loses some of its emotional power in Gallop's careful, measured prose.
- The "Clarendon Plato" series is designed for specialists; the commentary assumes familiarity with Greek philosophy.

**When to recommend:** For readers who want to study the arguments carefully, especially the Final Argument and its relationship to the Theory of Forms. Gallop's notes on Stephanus 100b–107b are invaluable.

## G. M. A. Grube (Hackett, 1977; revised by John M. Cooper 1997)

**Edition:** *Plato: Phaedo* (Hackett Classics), first ed. 1977. Revised in *Plato: Complete Works*, Hackett, 1997, ed. John M. Cooper.

**Character:** The dominant translation used in American philosophy classrooms for the past forty years. Modern, direct, philosophically careful without being dry.

**Strengths:**
- Clear, accessible modern English. No Victorian idiom.
- Philosophically consistent: Grube tracks key terms more reliably than Jowett.
- The Hackett *Complete Works* is the standard one-volume reference for Plato in English; this makes Grube/Cooper the most cited translation in contemporary scholarship.
- Good for reading the *Phaedo* alongside the *Meno* (which also discusses recollection) and the *Republic* (which develops the Forms).

**Weaknesses:**
- Occasionally flat. The dialogue's literary quality — its drama, its warmth — is less present than in Jowett.
- The 1977 Grube and the 1997 Cooper revision differ in places; scholars sometimes specify which version they're using.

**When to recommend:** Academic study, especially if a reader wants their *Phaedo* translation to match what the scholarly literature cites.

## Hugh Tredennick (Penguin, 1954; revised 1969)

**Edition:** *The Last Days of Socrates* (Penguin Classics), including *Euthyphro*, *Apology*, *Crito*, *Phaedo*. Revised 1969.

**Character:** The mass-market general reader's translation; part of the classic Penguin Classics series. Readable and accessible.

**Strengths:**
- The grouping with *Apology* and *Crito* makes it natural reading as a continuous narrative of Socrates' final days.
- Clear, modern English.
- Inexpensive and widely available.

**Weaknesses:**
- Less philosophically precise than Gallop or Grube/Cooper.
- The arguments — especially the Final Argument — can feel underexplained without Tredennick's notes.

## Key Terms and Their Translations

### *psyche* (ψυχή) — soul

The central term of the dialogue. In Greek, *psyche* carries more life-force and animating energy than the English "soul," which has become heavily weighted with Christian theology. It is the animating principle of a living thing — but Plato is specifically arguing it is also the rational, immortal part.

- **Jowett:** "soul" (consistently). This is fine but loses the animating-principle nuance.
- **Gallop:** "soul" — but he notes the broader sense in his commentary.
- **Grube/Cooper:** "soul" throughout.

**For readers:** Keep in mind that when Plato says *psyche*, he means something like "the thing that makes you alive and also the thing that thinks and knows." It's not straightforwardly the Christian soul. The *Phaedo* is part of the reason the Christian soul *became* what it is — but the terms weren't identical at the outset.

---

### *eidos* / *idea* (εἶδος / ἰδέα) — Form / Idea

The technical vocabulary for Platonic Forms. Both words are used for the same thing. In ordinary Greek, *eidos* means "shape" or "appearance" and *idea* means something like "look" or "visible form." Plato appropriates them for his philosophical entities.

- **Jowett:** "Idea" (capitalized), "absolute beauty," "beauty in the abstract," "beauty itself," "the beautiful." His inconsistency is a real problem. The reader may not realize Socrates is always discussing the same kind of entity.
- **Gallop:** "Form" (capitalized) throughout — the standard modern scholarly convention.
- **Grube/Cooper:** "Form" (capitalized) throughout.

**For readers of the Jowett text on greatbooks.fm:** When you see "absolute beauty" or "beauty itself" or the "Idea of beauty," Jowett is talking about what is standardly called the **Form of Beauty**. The inconsistency is Jowett's; the concept is consistent.

---

### *katharsis* (κάθαρσις) — purification / cleansing

One of the structurally important terms in the *Phaedo*. Socrates describes the philosopher's life as a process of *katharsis* — purifying the soul from the body's influence. The same word is used for ritual purification in religion, and Aristotle later uses it in the *Poetics* for the effect of tragedy. In the *Phaedo*, it is the philosophical-ascetic project: separating the soul from bodily desires and distractions so it can contemplate Forms clearly.

- **Jowett:** "purification" — accurate and clear.
- **Gallop:** "purification" — consistent with Jowett here.
- **Grube/Cooper:** "purification."

**For readers:** All translations agree on this one. *Katharsis* = purification. Philosophy is a lifelong purification; death is its completion.

---

### *anamnesis* (ἀνάμνησις) — recollection

Jowett renders this as "recollection" throughout, which is the standard choice. The term appears in the *Meno* and *Phaedo* and is the technical term for Plato's theory that learning is recovery of knowledge the soul already possessed.

---

### *melete thanatou* (μελέτη θανάτου) — practice for death / rehearsal of death

Jowett translates Socrates' claim that philosophy is *melete thanatou* as "the study of death" or "a preparation for dying." The Greek means more precisely: *practice*, *rehearsal*, *training* for death. Socrates is using a word from athletic or musical training. Philosophy is not merely studying death as an object; it is *practicing* the separation of soul from body that death completes.

- **Jowett:** "study of death" — misses the *practice/training* sense.
- **Gallop:** "practice for dying" — more accurate.
- **Grube:** "practice of death" — close.

**For readers:** This distinction matters. Jowett's "study of death" can imply passive contemplation. The Greek means something more active — the philosopher has been *rehearsing* this their whole life, building the skill, and today is the performance.

---

### *athanatos* (ἀθάνατος) — deathless / immortal

The technical term for what the Final Argument establishes the soul to be. Jowett: "immortal." Gallop: "deathless." The distinction is subtle: "deathless" is more literally accurate and avoids connotations of eternal bliss. Something deathless cannot admit death — but what happens to it is left open.

## What Jowett's Victorian Idiom Does to the Arguments

The biggest practical effect of reading Jowett is that the *arguments* read less like arguments. Jowett's style is literary and flowing; he does not break arguments into steps, does not flag premises, does not make the structure visible. A reader following Jowett's prose may grasp the general thrust of each argument without noticing when Socrates shifts from one step to another, or without catching the exact claim being made.

This is a genuine limitation for the *Phaedo* in particular, where the Final Argument is quite technically structured. Readers who find the Final Argument confusing in Jowett may find Gallop's prose (with his explanatory notes) considerably clearer.

**Recommendation for greatbooks.fm users:** Read Jowett for the experience of the text — it is beautiful, and the death scene is extraordinary in his translation. When the arguments get technical (roughly from the Affinity Argument onward), slow down and reread rather than trusting the flow. The argument is there; Jowett's smoothing can hide the joints.

## A Note on Stephanus Numbers

All modern scholarship cites Plato by **Stephanus numbers** — a system derived from Henri Estienne's 1578 edition of Plato's works. These are the numbers printed in the margins of most scholarly editions (e.g., 64a, 100b). The Jowett translation does not include Stephanus numbers in some editions. When checking a scholarly source that cites "Phaedo 72e" or "Phaedo 107b," use the Gallop or Grube/Cooper translation to locate the passage, then match to Jowett if needed.

The Wikisource Jowett text (https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Phaedo_(Jowett)) does not include Stephanus numbers. The Perseus online text (https://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A1999.01.0170%3Atext%3DPhaedo) includes them.
