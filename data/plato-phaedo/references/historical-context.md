## The Dramatic Setting

The *Phaedo* is set in the spring of 399 BCE in an Athenian prison. Socrates has been convicted of impiety and corrupting the youth by an Athenian jury — condemned by a vote of 280 to 220 (or thereabouts; sources differ on the exact margin). He has refused to escape, as Crito urged him in the dialogue *Crito*. He has been in prison for roughly a month. Today is his last day.

**Why the delay?** The trial of Socrates coincided with the departure of the sacred ship of Theseus on its annual pilgrimage to Delos — a voyage commemorating the mythological rescue of Athenian youths from the Minotaur. During this voyage, Athens kept itself ritually pure; no executions could be performed. When the ship returned, the sentence could be carried out. This accounts for the approximately thirty-day gap between conviction and death.

The prison itself is described without drama. Socrates' legs have been in chains and have just been removed. He jokes about the strange relationship between pleasure and pain — the ankle was aching from the iron, and now there is pleasure at its absence. It is an ordinary observation. Socrates will use it to open the first philosophical argument.

## Who Is Phaedo?

**Phaedo of Elis** (fl. 4th century BCE) is the dialogue's narrator and one of the most poignant figures in the story. He was present at Socrates' death — young enough that Socrates stroked his hair during the conversation, which Phaedo wore long in the Spartan style.

His biography is remarkable. Born to a noble family in Elis, he was captured in war as a young man (likely during the Spartan-Elean war of 402–401 BCE, though his exact age at the time of Socrates' death in 399 is debated — some sources place his capture earlier). He was sold into slavery at Athens and forced into prostitution. Socrates heard of him and arranged for his ransom — various sources attribute the ransom to Alcibiades, Crito, or Cebes.

Phaedo attached himself to Socrates and was part of his circle for the last years of Socrates' life. After the death, he returned to Elis and founded the **Elean School of philosophy** — a school that survived him and was later moved to Eretria by his pupil Menedemus, where it became the **Eretrian school**. None of Phaedo's own writings survive.

**Why does Plato name the dialogue after him?** Phaedo is the eyewitness, the young man who was there and remembers — and who tells Echecrates the story after the fact. The frame device is not accidental. Phaedo is an emotionally credible narrator: deeply attached to Socrates, young, not yet fully a philosopher. When he weeps, the reader weeps. When Socrates comforts him, the comfort lands.

There is a historical footnote: Athenaeus reports that Phaedo and Plato were actually enemies, and that Phaedo denied the views Plato attributed to him in the dialogue. If true, this adds an ironic layer — Plato chose as his witness a man who later repudiated the testimony. This is unverifiable but worth noting.

## Why Plato Was Not There

The text notes, matter-of-factly, that Plato was absent due to illness. This is one of the very rare self-references Plato makes in his own dialogues (another is the *Apology*, where he is mentioned as being present at the trial).

The authorial significance is considerable. Plato is writing a scene he did not witness. He is reconstructing — from accounts he gathered from Phaedo and others — the final philosophical conversation of his teacher. The *Phaedo* is not a transcript. It is a literary and philosophical construction, written perhaps twenty years after the events it depicts. Plato was in his late forties or early fifties when he wrote it.

This matters for reading the text honestly: the Socrates of the *Phaedo* is Plato's Socrates. The four arguments for immortality, the Theory of Forms, the myth of the afterlife — these are almost certainly Plato's philosophical views, put into Socrates' mouth. The historical Socrates, as depicted in the earlier *Apology*, is deliberately non-committal about the afterlife: "I do not know" whether death is good or bad. The *Phaedo*'s Socrates is confident, systematic, and philosophically sophisticated in ways the Apology's Socrates is not.

## The Relationship to the Apology

The *Apology*, *Crito*, and *Phaedo* form a continuous dramatic sequence:

1. **Apology** — The trial. Socrates defends himself, refuses to beg, is convicted. He says he does not know whether death is good or bad. He is at peace with it.
2. **Crito** — A day or two before the execution. Crito urges Socrates to escape; Socrates refuses, arguing he must obey the laws. This establishes the ethical framework.
3. **Phaedo** — The final day. The execution. The philosophical argument for immortality. The death scene.

The *Euthyphro* also belongs to this period — set just before the trial, as Socrates is on his way to court — but it is less directly linked to the death.

**The continuity of tone:** The Apology's Socrates is puzzled, ironic, modest. He uses a famous formulation: he knows that he does not know. The Phaedo's Socrates knows far more — or at least argues far more confidently. Scholars have noted this tonal and philosophical shift as evidence that the *Phaedo* represents Plato's own developing philosophy rather than a portrait of the historical Socrates.

**The continuity of subject:** In the *Apology*, Socrates tells the jury he will continue to philosophize even under penalty of death — that the unexamined life is not worth living. The *Phaedo* is the answer to the implicit question that leaves open: *why* is the examined life worth living? Because it is practice for the most important transition any soul makes. The *Apology* is about what Socrates believes during life. The *Phaedo* is about what he believes about what comes after.

## The Broader Socratic Circle on the Last Day

The list of people present at Socrates' death is one of the remarkable sociological data points of the ancient world. It includes:

- **Simmias and Cebes** — Pythagorean-trained philosophers from Thebes. They are the primary interlocutors for the philosophical argument.
- **Crito** — Childhood friend, loyal supporter, who will take responsibility for the body.
- **Apollodorus** — Described as unable to stop weeping; elsewhere depicted as emotionally volatile.
- **Euclid of Megara** — Founder of the Megarian school, interested in logic and ontology.
- **Antisthenes** — Later regarded as a founding influence on the Cynic school.
- **Aeschines of Sphettus** — A Socratic philosopher who wrote dialogues, fragments of which survive.
- **Xanthippe** — Socrates' wife, brought with their youngest son. She is distressed; Socrates has her taken away. Plato's portrayal is sympathetic, unlike Xenophon's.

Plato notes the absences: himself (ill), and Aristippus (in Aegina).

## The Pythagorean Connection: Why It Matters

The *Phaedo*'s key interlocutors — Simmias, Cebes, and the frame-character Echecrates — are all associated with Pythagoreanism. The dialogue is explicitly engaging with the Pythagorean tradition of the soul's immortality and reincarnation. The arguments Socrates presents are, in part, philosophically rigorous responses to Pythagorean intuitions. The soul-as-harmony objection (Simmias) is drawn from Pythagorean music theory. Plato uses these characters not merely as convenient foils but as representatives of a serious philosophical tradition that Socrates' arguments must outperform.

**Philolaus of Croton** — the Pythagorean philosopher under whom both Simmias and Cebes studied — taught the immortality and transmigration of the soul, the doctrine of opposites, and likely some version of the harmony theory. Plato absorbed Pythagorean doctrine through his friendship with **Archytas of Tarentum** and possibly through time spent in southern Italy after Socrates' death.

## The Political Context

399 BCE was a politically raw moment in Athens. The Thirty Tyrants — an oligarchic junta backed by Sparta — had ruled Athens for eight months (404–403 BCE), killing some 1,500 citizens. Democracy was restored in 403 BCE. The democratic government that restored itself then tried and executed the most famous philosopher in Athens.

The charge of "impiety" (asebeia) and "corrupting the youth" was, in the political climate of 399 BCE, probably partly about Socrates' associations — his former students included Alcibiades (the general who betrayed Athens to Sparta during the Peloponnesian War) and Critias (the most ruthless of the Thirty Tyrants). Whether or not the charge was fair, it was politically charged.

Plato watched his teacher executed by the democracy he had been raised to admire. The *Republic*, written after the *Phaedo*, is Plato's systematic argument for why democratic governance is philosophically deficient. The *Phaedo* comes first: it saves Socrates' soul, if not his life.
