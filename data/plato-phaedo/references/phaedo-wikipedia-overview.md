## Overview

*Phaedo* (Ancient Greek: Φαίδων, *Phaidōn*; also known as *On the Soul*) is a Socratic dialogue written by Plato in the 4th century BCE. It depicts the final hours of Socrates' life in 399 BCE, set in an Athenian prison cell. Socrates discusses the immortality of the soul and the nature of the afterlife with his friends — principally the Pythagorean philosophers Simmias and Cebes of Thebes. The dialogue concludes with a mythological narrative of the afterlife geography (a descent into Tartarus) and the account of Socrates' execution by hemlock.

The dialogue is universally regarded as one of Plato's greatest literary and philosophical achievements. It is a dialogue of the **middle period**, along with the *Republic* and the *Symposium* — the period in which Plato's distinctive metaphysical doctrines (Forms, immortality, recollection) are most fully developed.

## The Frame Story

The dialogue is narrated by **Phaedo of Elis**, who was present at Socrates' death. He recounts the events to **Echecrates of Phlius**, a Pythagorean philosopher, at some point after the death. This frame is significant: the reader is receiving a remembered account, filtered through Phaedo's emotional experience. Plato himself was absent — he was ill, as noted within the text.

**Why was there a delay between trial and execution?** The trial of Socrates coincided with the annual voyage of the sacred Ship of Theseus to Delos, during which Athens kept itself ritually "pure" and postponed executions. This created a gap of roughly a month between the verdict (spring 399 BCE) and the death, during which Socrates remained in prison.

## Characters

**In the frame story:**
- **Phaedo of Elis** — A young follower of Socrates, formerly enslaved as a prisoner of war, ransomed at Socrates' request. He later founded the Elean School of philosophy. Young enough at the time that Socrates stroked his hair during the conversation.
- **Echecrates of Phlius** — A Pythagorean philosopher, the listener to whom Phaedo relates the story.

**In the prison dialogue:**
- **Socrates** — Philosopher in his seventies, calm, even cheerful, facing death.
- **Simmias of Thebes** — A follower of Socrates and student of the Pythagorean Philolaus of Croton. Raises the "lyre" (harmony) objection to the soul's immortality — one of the most penetrating objections in ancient philosophy.
- **Cebes of Thebes** — Also a follower of Socrates and Philolaus student. Raises the "weaver" objection — the soul may outlast some bodies while still being mortal.
- **Crito of Alopece** — Childhood friend of Socrates. Takes responsibility for Socrates' body after death and sacrifices a rooster to Asclepius on his behalf (fulfilling Socrates' dying request).
- **Xanthippe** — Socrates' wife, present early in the dialogue, distressed and taken away at Socrates' request.

**Also present:** Apollodorus (unable to stop weeping), Critobulus, Hermogenes, Epigenes, Aeschines of Sphettus, Antisthenes, Euclid of Megara, and others. Notable absences: Plato (ill) and Aristippus (in Aegina).

## The Pythagorean Connection

A significant feature of the dialogue often overlooked by general readers: almost all the main interlocutors are associated with Pythagoreanism. Simmias and Cebes studied under **Philolaus of Croton**, the most prominent Pythagorean philosopher of the period. Echecrates, the listener in the frame, is from Phlius — a stronghold of Pythagoreanism. The topics addressed in the dialogue — the immortality and reincarnation of the soul, the metaphysics of opposites, and the soul-as-harmony objection — are thought to derive partly from Pythagorean doctrines. Plato likely absorbed Pythagorean ideas through his close friendship with **Archytas of Tarentum**.

## The Four Arguments for Immortality

Socrates introduces four arguments to address Cebes's fear that the soul simply dissolves at death:

1. **The Cyclical Argument** (69e–72d) — Opposites generate one another. The living come from the dead, and the dead from the living. Therefore souls must exist between incarnations.

2. **The Argument from Recollection** (72e–77a) — Learning is really *recollection* of knowledge the soul possessed before birth. This implies the soul existed before the body. Combined with a version of the Cyclical argument, it suggests the soul continues after death.

3. **The Affinity Argument** (78b–80c) — The soul resembles the invisible, unchanging Forms; the body resembles the visible, changing world. What is akin to the divine and immortal is itself immortal.

4. **The Final Argument** (102a–107b) — The soul is the principle of life itself. What brings life cannot admit its opposite (death). Therefore the soul is deathless.

Between arguments 3 and 4, Simmias raises the **lyre objection** (soul as harmony) and Cebes raises the **weaver objection** (soul as a long-lived but ultimately mortal thing). Socrates addresses both before proceeding.

## The Myth of the Afterlife

After the final argument, Socrates presents a mythological account of the afterlife — an extended geography of the underworld, including:
- The true earth (far larger and more beautiful than the earth we inhabit, which is like a hollow at the bottom of a great sea of air)
- The rivers of the underworld: Acheron, Styx, Pyriphlegethon, Cocytus
- Tartarus — the deepest abyss into which the irredeemably wicked fall permanently
- The Isles of the Blessed — where the souls of the purified philosophers dwell

Socrates is explicit that the details are likely not exactly right — this is a "likely story" (*muthos*), not a demonstration. But some truth is captured in it.

## The Death Scene

The closing pages of the dialogue are among the most celebrated in ancient literature. Socrates bathes, speaks with his sons and the women of his household, then drinks the hemlock calmly. When his friends break down weeping, he chides them gently. The poison takes effect from the feet upward. His last words, to Crito: **"Crito, we owe a cock to Asclepius; pay it and do not forget."** Scholars have debated the meaning of this since antiquity. One reading: Asclepius was the god of healing; the hemlock is Socrates' cure from the disease of life, and the cock is his thank-offering.

## Place in the Canon and Later Influence

The *Phaedo* is Plato's fourth and last dialogue detailing Socrates' final days (following *Euthyphro*, *Apology*, and *Crito*). It is a middle-period work, thought to represent Plato's own philosophical views more than the early Socratic dialogues.

The dialogue has been enormously influential. Its dualities — soul and body, mind and matter, intellect and sense, reason and emotion, reality and appearance, immortal and mortal — were described by scholar David Gallop as having become "deeply ingrained in Western philosophy, theology, and psychology over two millennia." The *Phaedo* was the literary model for Gregory of Nyssa's *Dialogue on the Soul and Resurrection*. It was first translated into Latin by Apuleius; the medieval translation by Henry Aristippus (1160) was important for transmitting Platonic thought to the Latin West.

## Major Translations

- **Benjamin Jowett** (1892) — Used by greatbooks.fm. Elegant Victorian prose; reads well aloud; occasionally archaic idiom.
- **G. M. A. Grube** (1977, rev. Cooper 1997) — Widely used in academic philosophy; precise and modern.
- **David Gallop** (Oxford, 1975) — Scholarly edition with extensive notes; excellent for close reading of the arguments.
- **Hugh Tredennick** (Penguin, 1954) — Published as *The Last Days of Socrates*; accessible for general readers.
- **Harold N. Fowler** (Loeb, 1914) — Greek-English parallel text; useful for cross-referencing.
