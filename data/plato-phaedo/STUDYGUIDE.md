# The Phaedo: A Study Guide
## Plato (trans. Benjamin Jowett, 1892)

---

## Before You Read

If you read the *Apology*, you know what's coming.

In the *Apology*, Socrates stood trial before 500 Athenian citizens, defended himself without apology, was convicted by a slim margin, and sentenced to death. When the verdict came in, he told the jury: "I do not know whether death is good or bad. But I know that acting unjustly is bad." He was calm. He was at peace. He walked back to prison and waited.

That was thirty days ago.

The *Phaedo* opens on the last morning. Socrates' friends have gathered in his cell — Simmias and Cebes from Thebes, Crito his oldest friend, Apollodorus (who cannot stop weeping), and a young man named Phaedo of Elis, formerly a slave, ransomed at Socrates' request and now a devoted follower. They're devastated. He's not.

The text is written by Plato but narrated by Phaedo. He is telling the story to a Pythagorean philosopher named Echecrates, who was not there — and neither was Plato. Plato was ill that day. He was not in the room when Socrates died. This matters. The *Phaedo* is a literary construction, written perhaps twenty years after the events it depicts, by a man who heard the account from others and transformed it into one of the greatest philosophical texts ever written. The Socrates in this room is Plato's Socrates — the philosopher who believes in the immortality of the soul with full confidence.

And his confidence has a structure.

Philosophers, Socrates argues, spend their whole lives practicing a kind of separation: trying to free the mind from the distractions of the body, to pursue knowledge undistorted by appetite, pleasure, and desire. Pure knowledge of the things that really exist — Beauty itself, Justice itself, Equality itself — cannot be obtained through the senses. The body misleads. The body distracts. The body is a hindrance to truth. Death is simply the completion of what philosophy was already practicing. Why would a philosopher fear it?

This argument — that philosophy is *melete thanatou* (practice for death) — is the foundation of everything that follows. Then comes the argument that the soul actually survives. Four arguments, each more technically sophisticated than the last, building on something Plato calls the Theory of Forms. Two interlocutors raise serious objections. Socrates answers them, more or less.

And then the hemlock.

A note on the translation. Jowett's *Phaedo* is among his finest work — the death scene in particular is extraordinary Victorian prose. He occasionally uses archaic idiom (the odd "methinks," inverted syntax), and his rendering of technical terms can be inconsistent. When Socrates talks about "absolute beauty" or "beauty in the abstract," he means what modern philosophy calls the **Form of Beauty** — the perfect, non-physical, eternal standard against which all beautiful things are measured. Key terms are defined as they appear.

This is Book 2 of three. The *Apology* ended with a death sentence. Now you find out what Socrates believed about what comes next.

### Coming Up

The dialogue opens with a frame: Phaedo is telling Echecrates about that day. Then we're in the room. Socrates' chains have just been removed. His ankle aches; he jokes about pleasure and pain being unexpectedly linked. Then his wife Xanthippe arrives with one of their sons — and when she breaks down, Socrates has her taken away. The conversation begins. You'll meet Simmias and Cebes — young Pythagorean philosophers, intellectually curious, genuinely shaken. Socrates lays out why a philosopher should be willing to die. The first argument for the soul's immortality is underway before long. We'll check in before the arguments begin in earnest.

## Session I: Why Fear Death? (Opening through the Argument from Recollection)

### Summary

The *Phaedo* is one continuous dialogue — no book divisions. Session I covers the opening exchange, Socrates' argument that philosophy is practice for death, and the first two arguments for the soul's immortality: the Cyclical Argument and the Argument from Recollection.

**Opening through 69e:** Socrates is released from his chains. His wife is taken away. Cebes asks why, if death is so good, Socrates hasn't simply killed himself. Socrates answers: we are placed here as a kind of guard post, and we may not abandon it without the gods' command. But the gods are calling him now. He is ready.

He makes his central case: the philosopher's whole life is a practice of separating the mind from the body — avoiding bodily pleasures, distrusting the senses, pursuing pure knowledge through reason alone. Death is the completion of this project. A philosopher who feared death would be like a runner who trained for a race and then refused to run.

**Cyclical Argument (69e–72d):** Opposites generate each other. Sleeping comes from waking; waking from sleeping. The living come from the dead; the dead from the living. This cycle must continue, or all life would eventually exhaust itself. Therefore souls must exist between incarnations.

**Argument from Recollection (72e–77a):** All genuine learning is *anamnesis* — recollection of knowledge the soul possessed before birth. When we see two sticks and call them "equal," we're comparing them to **Equality itself** — a perfect standard the sticks merely approximate. We recognize the imperfection, which means we already know the standard. We didn't learn it in this life (we've never seen a perfectly equal pair of things). We must have known it before birth. Therefore the soul existed before the body. Combined with the Cyclical Argument, it suggests the soul also continues after death.

---

### Scenes, Themes & Characters

**Philosophy as Practice for Death**
Socrates' foundational argument is not morbid — it is structural. The philosopher pursues truth by systematically withdrawing from the body's interference: ignoring pleasure, distrusting sensation, relying on pure intellect. This is the same thing death does, but voluntarily. *Melete thanatou* — rehearsal for dying — is the name Socrates gives to the philosophical life. When Simmias initially laughs at the idea that a philosopher should be "willing to die," Socrates is already three steps ahead: he's not saying death is enjoyable. He's saying the philosopher has already been moving toward it their whole career.

**The Body as Obstacle**
The *Phaedo* contains the most sustained argument in ancient philosophy for the soul's independence from, and superiority to, the body. Socrates is thorough: the body gives us **pleasure and pain** (both distracting); **sight and hearing** (the most trusted senses — both unreliable); **desires and appetites** (which command the soul and drag it toward the material); **wars and factions** (which arise from wanting what bodies want — money, land, power). Pure truth — knowledge of the Forms — is accessible only when the mind operates alone, "refusing to be assisted in the hunt by the body." The argument anticipates objections by being comprehensive.

**The Cyclical Argument and Its Limits**
Socrates' first argument for immortality is the weakest, and he seems to know it. He gets Cebes to agree that opposites generate each other in nature, and that life and death must form a cycle — otherwise everything would eventually be dead. It establishes that something must be recycled; it does not clearly establish that individual souls persist. Cebes accepts it but almost immediately points out what it can't do on its own: it doesn't prove your soul specifically survives your death. The Recollection Argument is needed to begin that case.

**Simmias and Cebes**
These two Theban philosophers are not foils. They are trained thinkers — both students of the Pythagorean philosopher Philolaus, for whom the soul's immortality was a serious doctrine. When they push back on Socrates, they are pushing back from within a tradition that already believes in the soul's survival. Their objections (which come later) are the sharper for it. Cebes here is pragmatic: yes, Socrates has shown the soul pre-exists, but has he shown it survives? Not yet.

**Recollection and the Theory of Forms**
The Recollection argument is the first place in the dialogue where the Theory of Forms becomes explicit. If we know what "perfect equality" is — even though we've never encountered it in the physical world — we must have encountered it before. The soul's pre-natal existence is the only explanation Plato offers. This argument is more philosophically interesting than the Cyclical one: it raises a genuine epistemological puzzle about how we know abstract things at all, and offers a metaphysical solution. A skeptic can reject the Forms; but then they need a different account of how we know what we know.

Socrates argues that philosophy is *melete thanatou* — a practice for death, a lifelong rehearsal of separating the mind from the body. Is this a description of what philosophy actually is, or is it Plato's particular, and possibly eccentric, definition of it? What does it mean to say that seeking truth requires detachment from bodily experience?

## Session II: The Soul and the Forms (Affinity Argument through Final Argument)

### Summary

Session II covers the third and fourth arguments for immortality, the two major objections, and Socrates' responses. This is the philosophical core of the dialogue.

**Affinity Argument (78b–80c):** The soul resembles the invisible, unchanging, divine, simple — just as the Forms do. The body resembles the visible, changing, composite, mortal. The soul will not dissolve at death; it will depart to the realm it is akin to.

**The Objections:** Simmias raises the **Lyre Argument** (the soul might be merely the harmony of the body — invisible and excellent, yet mortal). Cebes raises the **Weaver Argument** (the soul might outlast many bodies but still eventually wear out). Together these temporarily shake the whole company.

**Warning against Misology (89c–91c):** Socrates pauses to warn against *misology* — hatred of argument, the temptation to give up on reasoning when arguments fail. Arguments are like people: some seem trustworthy and turn out not to be; this doesn't mean all arguments are worthless.

**Replies and Final Argument (91e–107b):** Socrates addresses both objections, then gives his fourth and most sophisticated argument. The soul participates in the Form of Life essentially. The Form of Life cannot admit Death. Therefore the soul is *athanatos* — deathless.

---

### Scenes, Themes & Characters

**The Theory of Forms, Stated Plainly**
At Stephanus 100b, Socrates makes his methodological commitment explicit: he proceeds by positing that "there is such a thing as the beautiful in itself, and the good and the great and all the others." Particular beautiful things are beautiful because they participate in the Form of Beauty. This is the foundation of the Affinity Argument and the Final Argument both. In Jowett's translation, Forms appear as "absolute beauty," "the beautiful itself," "beauty in the abstract." Same entity, different phrasings. The concept is: a perfect, non-physical, eternal standard that exists independently of any particular instance.

**The Lyre Objection — The Best Challenge in the Dialogue**
Simmias's objection is philosophically the most interesting moment in the *Phaedo*. He grants everything Socrates has said about the soul being invisible, incorporeal, excellent, and akin to the Forms. Then he says: but the *harmony* of a lyre is also invisible and excellent — and when you destroy the lyre, the harmony vanishes. What if the soul is just the body's harmony? Invisible and non-material, yes; but still dependent on the instrument it attunes?

This is not a sophist's trick. Simmias is reporting a genuine philosophical position from the Pythagorean tradition. The soul-as-attunement theory is coherent and has been held by serious thinkers. Socrates' three replies — that it's incompatible with Recollection; that harmonies can't resist their instruments; that all harmonies are equally harmonious — are interesting, but most scholars think the first reply does the real work (and depends on accepting Recollection) while the others are less decisive. Simmias accepts the replies. Whether Plato has *proved* the harmony theory wrong, or merely established its incompatibility with other things he believes, is a genuine question.

**The Weaver Objection — The Harder Problem**
Cebes' objection cuts deeper than Simmias's. He grants everything the Affinity Argument says: the soul is more durable, more excellent, more persistent than the body. But durable is not the same as immortal. A weaver outlasts many cloaks — makes them and wears them out one after another. He is more durable than any single cloak. But eventually, the weaver dies. The soul might outlast several bodies through reincarnation and still eventually exhaust itself. This directly targets the gap in the Affinity Argument: similarity to the divine is not the same as being divine.

This is what drives Socrates to the Final Argument — the only one that attempts to show the soul is *structurally incapable* of dying, rather than merely unlikely to die soon.

**The Final Argument's Architecture**
The structure is elegant: things that necessarily bring along a Form cannot admit that Form's opposite. Fire necessarily brings Heat — fire cannot be cold. The number three necessarily brings Oddness — three cannot be even. The soul necessarily brings Life (it is the animating principle of anything it occupies). Therefore the soul cannot admit Death. What cannot admit death is *athanatos* — deathless. When death approaches the body the soul occupies, the soul departs; it does not *become* dead.

The argument's dependence on Forms is complete. Without the Form of Life as a genuine metaphysical entity that the soul participates in essentially, the whole structure fails. This is not a weakness of the argument — it is what the argument is for. Plato is showing that the immortality of the soul and the existence of Forms stand or fall together. Believe in Forms; believe in the soul's survival.

### Coming Up

The arguments are done. There is one final question: what happens after? Socrates tells a story — not a proof, not a demonstration, but a myth. He describes the earth as it really is, the rivers of the underworld, the fate of different kinds of souls. Then comes the last hour. We'll check in before the myth and death scene.

### Essay

Simmias's lyre objection — that the soul might be merely the harmony of the body — is one of the most challenging objections in ancient philosophy. Socrates offers three replies. Do they succeed? Is the soul-as-harmony view defeated, or merely shown to be incompatible with other commitments Plato holds?

## Session III: The Last Hour (The Myth and the Death Scene)

### Summary

Session III covers Socrates' myth of the afterlife, his final conversation, and his death.

**The Myth (108c–115a):** Having finished the philosophical arguments, Socrates tells a likely story about the true earth and the geography of the underworld. He is explicit that he cannot prove the details — but something like this is probably true, and it is "worth the risk of believing."

**The Final Conversation (115a–116a):** The jailer comes, and the friends are told to begin their farewells. Crito asks how they should bury Socrates. Socrates gently corrects him: you will bury my body. I will not be there.

**The Death (116a–118a):** Socrates bathes, speaks with his children and the women of his household. The hemlock is prepared. He drinks it. He dies. His last words: "Crito, we owe a cock to Asclepius; pay it and do not forget."

---

### Scenes, Themes & Characters

**The Myth of the Afterlife: Not Literal, Not Nothing**
The myth of the true earth is one of Plato's most remarkable passages and one of his most misunderstood. Socrates is not asserting that this is exactly how the afterlife works. He says explicitly it is a *muthos* — a story — and that a reasonable person cannot be certain of the details. But he thinks something like it is true.

The vision: we think we live on the surface of the earth, but actually we live at the bottom of a vast hollow, like sea creatures at the bottom of the ocean who think the water *is* the sky. The real earth, at the true surface, is radiant with color, precious stones, light. Below our hollow run the rivers of the underworld — Acheron, Cocytus, Pyriphlegethon, and the Styx — all draining into the deepest abyss, Tartarus.

After death, souls go to Tartarus and are judged. Those who lived virtuously are rewarded and eventually reincarnated into better lives. Those who committed curable wrongs are punished and then released when forgiven. The incurably wicked are cast permanently into Tartarus. And the philosophers — those who have truly purified themselves — are freed from the cycle entirely. They dwell forever in the company of the gods.

**The Function of the Myth**
Why does Plato end a philosophical argument with a myth? Several reasons are worth considering. First, there are things that matter for how we live but that argument cannot reach — the myth establishes the *stakes* of the philosophical life in a way that demonstration cannot. Second, Plato uses myth deliberately at critical junctures (the Myth of Er in the *Republic*, the myth of Atlantis, the myth of the demiurge in the *Timaeus*) to gesture at truths that cannot be stated precisely. The myth says: this is the direction truth lies, even if we cannot triangulate exactly. Third, the myth ends the dialogue on hope rather than proof. The arguments may or may not be decisive. The myth says: live as if they are.

**The Death Scene**
The closing pages of the *Phaedo* are among the most celebrated in ancient literature, and they deserve to be read slowly.

Socrates bathes — ritual purity before death. He meets with his sons and the women of the household, presumably saying what needs to be said. Then he returns. The jailer who brings the hemlock has tears in his eyes and apologizes for having to carry out this task; Socrates thanks him and says he is a good man. He asks the man how to administer the poison. "Just walk about until your limbs feel heavy," the jailer says, "and then lie down."

Socrates drinks the cup without hesitation. His friends break down. He chides them gently: "What is this strange outcry? I sent away the women mainly in order that they might not misbehave in this way, for I have heard that a man should die in peace." He tells them to be quiet and bear it.

The hemlock moves from his feet upward. He covers himself with a sheet. As it reaches his waist, he uncovers his face and says his last words: **"Crito, we owe a cock to Asclepius; pay it and do not forget."**

Asclepius was the god of medicine and healing. The traditional offering for recovery from illness was a rooster sacrificed to Asclepius. Socrates' last act is to observe this religious custom — but the illness he is being cured of, scholars have argued since antiquity, is life itself. Death is the cure. The cock is his thank-offering for being healed.

Or perhaps he is simply being pious to the last, maintaining his obligations even in his final breath. Perhaps both.

Crito asks if there is anything else. There is no answer. He is gone.

Phaedo's last sentence: "Such was the end, Echecrates, of our friend, who was, as we may say, of all the men of his time the wisest and justest and best."

**What the Death Scene Proves That the Arguments Cannot**
No philosophical argument can do what this scene does. The arguments establish (or try to establish) that the soul is immortal. But the death scene shows what it looks like when a person actually believes that — and acts accordingly. Socrates is not performing courage. He is not suppressing fear. He is genuinely at peace, genuinely concerned for his friends' grief more than his own death, genuinely curious about what happens next. The scene is the argument's embodiment.

This is why the *Phaedo* ends here and not with Stephanus 107b. The arguments matter. The death scene proves they mattered to Socrates.

### Coming Up

In the course "The Death of Socrates," the *Phaedo* is the middle book. The *Apology* gave you Socrates' defense of the life he lived. The *Phaedo* gives you what he believed about what follows life. The *Republic* — coming next — is Plato's response to everything: the longer, harder argument for why philosophy matters, what justice is, and what a city governed by wisdom rather than opinion would look like.
